# An Approach to Branding Services

**Author:** Y.L.R. Moorthi, Associate Professor (Marketing), Indian Institute of Management, Bangalore, India
**Publication:** Journal of Services Marketing, Vol. 16, No. 3, 2002, pp. 259–274
**Publisher:** MCB UP Limited, 0887-6045
**DOI:** 10.1108/08876040210427236
**Original filename:** An approach to branding services.pdf

---

**Keywords:** Brands, Service

**Abstract:** This paper provides an approach for branding a service. It accomplishes this by bringing together David Aaker's brand identity framework, the 7Ps of services marketing and the economic classification of goods. The 7Ps of services are product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence, process and people. The economic classification divides goods into search, experience and credence goods. Typical examples for search, experience and credence goods are a consumer durable, a restaurant and a doctor respectively. The branding efforts needed for each of the three types of goods is different. The approach recommends what should be done in terms of 7Ps for each of the three types of goods and integrates this effort with their branding.

## Literature Review

### Issues of Branding Services

Although there is extensive literature on services marketing not much of it addresses the issue of branding services. A significant amount of literature on services deals with measuring or delivering customer satisfaction (Carman, 2000; Gronroos, 1983; Parasuraman et al., 1988, 1991, 1994; Spreng and Mackoy, 1996; Jones and Suh, 2000). Another stream deals with issues that relate to business success in services (Sin and Tse, 2000; Berry, 1999; Keaveney, 1995; Martilla and James, 1977; Schlesinger and Heskett, 1991). A fair amount deals with the operational aspects of services (Maister, 1984; Larson, 1987; Chase, 1981). There is also literature on what type of organization and personnel support the delivery of high quality service (Carlzon, 1987; Gronroos, 1990; Lovelock, 1992; Davis, 1989; Wyckoff, 1991). There is however not much literature on how to brand a service.

Extant literature that deals with branding of services, directly or indirectly:

**Dobree and Page (1990)** list five steps for effectively branding services:
1. Building a brand proposition
2. Overcoming internal barriers
3. Measuring delivery against the proposition
4. Continual improvement
5. Expansion

They also recommend developing a "service contract" internally to create ownership for the service brand across all levels of the organization.

**Onkvisit and Shaw (1989)** differentiate between the two levels of service offerings: the form and the brand. Credit cards have several forms: bank cards, airlines cards, car rental companies' cards, oil companies' cards, store cards and travel and entertainment cards. Each of these forms requires a different type of branding effort. In fact they suggest launching different brands for different service forms.

### Understanding Customer Needs

**Gale (1994)** defines a power brand as a "name that means satisfaction, quality and value to the customer." He recommends understanding customer needs, delivering superior quality on attributes that matter to customers, low "cost of quality," overall cost leadership and effective positioning as some of the steps that lead to building a powerful service brand.

**Kapferer (1992)** opines that branding culture is not strongly embedded in service firms. He specifically notes branding in banking services and touches on the issues of intangibility and invisibility of services. He also gives suggestions on how to name brands. **Berry and Parasuraman (1991)** give a more comprehensive checklist for naming brands.

**Levy (1996)** contends that successful service brands can be developed based on the principles of fast moving consumer goods (FMCG) branding. These principles are product definition, clear product benefit identification, brand differentiation, consumer motivation and measurement of product strength.

**De Charnatony and McDonald (1998)**, like Levy, feel that the FMCG model of branding can be used, with modifications, to build service brands. They sum up some of the important issues that affect branding (e.g. characteristics of services, importance of symbols in brand building, empowerment of staff, consumer participation in developing the brand).

### A Comprehensive Approach

Extant literature does throw light on how to build a service brand, but does not suggest a comprehensive approach. This paper proposes to present such an approach by applying Aaker's brand identity framework to the economic classification of three types of goods and the 7Ps of services.

**Aaker's (1996) brand identity framework** proposes four elements under which an identity is typically developed for a brand:
1. Brand as product
2. Brand as organization
3. Brand as person
4. Brand as symbol

- **Brand as product** — deals with the tangible and intangible aspects of the product and the manner in which the customer relates to it.
- **Brand as organization** — deals with the organization's innovation, consumer concern, etc., which are important for building strong brands.
- **Brand as person** — deals with the personality aspects of the brand (what happens to the brand when it is converted to a person).
- **Brand as symbol** — deals with the symbolic aspects of the brand like visual imagery, logo, brand heritage, etc.

---

[Page 260]

**The approach presented in this paper firstly blends the 7Ps of services marketing with Aaker's brand identity framework.** A service's marketing mix consists of 7Ps (product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence, process and people) (Booms and Bitner, 1981).

In this paper, the first five Ps (product, price, place, promotion and physical evidence) have been mapped to Aaker's **brand as product**. The **people** dimension, along with organizational culture, values and other issues, is put under **brand as organization**. The **process** dimension has been highlighted separately as **"brand as process"** — this is because the customer is intimately involved in the process of delivery of the service (Lovelock, 1992). Brand as person and brand as symbol retain their usual meaning in the new model.

### Figure 1: Aaker's Brand Identity Model
```
Brand ──► Brand as product
      ──► Brand as organization
      ──► Brand as person
      ──► Brand as symbol
```

### Figure 2: Proposed Services Branding Model
```
Brand ──► Brand as product (product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence)
      ──► Brand as process (process)
      ──► Brand as organization (people)
      ──► Brand as person
      ──► Brand as symbol
```

The next step in the proposed approach is to relate the modified Aaker model to the three-fold classification of goods by economists (Nelson, 1970; Darby and Karni, 1973). The discipline of services marketing has borrowed this three-fold classification:
1. **Search goods** — products have more search properties
2. **Experience goods** — services have more experience properties
3. **Credence goods** — services have more credence properties

**Most services are either experience goods or credence goods.** By relating the three types of goods to Aaker's modified framework, this paper provides an approach to branding services.

## Brand as Product

### Product

**Typical example:**
- A **scooter** is a typical search good — before buying, a customer goes through a lot of information search (fuel efficiency, ease of maintenance, price on the road). All these are measurable and therefore the brand promise is explicit in the case of a search good.
- By contrast, in an **experience good like a restaurant**, a customer demands satisfaction with the totality of experience — synergy in everything from the cuisine served, through the manner in which it is served, to the ambience. A *Gestalt* of the tangibles and intangibles is more important to the customer than the individual elements. Since there is a considerable degree of intangibility, the promise to the customer is implicit in an experience good.
- A **credence good** (e.g. consultant) is chosen overridingly for its reputation. There is greater intangibility — therefore brand promise in a credence good is implicit.

**Inseparability:** Low in search goods (service component is low); the service provider can never be separated from his service in an experience good (same is true of credence goods — a doctor or consultant cannot be separated from the service they provide).

**Perceived risk:** The perceived risk in judging the performance of the product is higher in buying services than goods (Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996). Services like medical diagnostics are so technical that the consumer possesses neither the knowledge nor the experience to evaluate the service. Credence goods are difficult to judge even after using them (Choi and Scarpa, 1994) — for instance, not many viewers find it easy to understand Picasso's paintings, and many admers of art lack the information to differentiate a fake from a genuine work of art. This information asymmetry between buyers and sellers creates strong incentives for sellers of credence goods to cheat (Emons, 1997). Perceived risk: lowest for search goods, medium for experience goods, highest for credence goods.

### Price

**Price estimation:**
- Easy for search goods (costs of production are known, anchor point available)
- Relatively easy for experience goods
- Difficult for credence goods — a renowned doctor or consultant can charge a premium; in many cases they do not know themselves what the service will involve until they have fully examined the client (Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996)

**Ability to charge premium:**
- Search goods: Difficult. The customer makes a detailed analysis of options. Literature shows price plays a role in the diffusion of high priced, medium priced, and low priced durable goods (Parker, 1992). Appropriate price can advance replacement purchase by one year (Bayus, 1988). Charging a premium in search goods is not easy.
- Experience goods: Also difficult. Table II (adapted from Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996) shows how customers define "value" in different services:

| Service | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Airline | "When tickets are discounted" |
| Hotel | "Price first and quality second" |
| MBA degree | "Very best education one can get" |
| Medical service | "High quality" |

Note that the first two (airline, hotel) are experience goods and the last two (MBA, medical) are credence goods. In experience goods, the customer is concerned with price; in credence goods, with quality. This makes credence goods relatively price-inelastic. In high-risk situations involving credence services (medical treatment, management consulting), the customer will look to price as a surrogate for quality (Zeithaml, 1982). There is, thus, a need to peg the price at a particular level, keeping in view customer expectations. Therefore **charging a premium is easier in a credence good than in an experience good.**

### Place

**Physical location:**
- **Search good:** Showroom should be as close to the customer as possible, to build shop traffic.
- **Experience good:** Customers are ready to travel a reasonable distance to avail services of an experience good (e.g. amusement park).
- **Credence good:** Customers are ready to travel any distance to get the right person. Therefore the physical distance dimension becomes progressively less important as one moves from search goods to credence goods.

**Risk of relying on channels:**
- The distribution/franchising function can be delegated to an intermediary or franchisee in search goods, but not so easily in experience goods.
- It can be done only with great difficulty in credence goods. For instance, the distributorship of scooters and washing machines is routinely offered to intermediaries. While the same is done in experience goods like fast food restaurants (e.g. McDonald's franchise), the process is much more difficult (Love, 1995). McDonald's lays down systematic procedures that the franchisees adhere to.
- A credence good like management consultancy or architecture is much more difficult to delegate to a franchisee — execution of a complex offering that conforms to the standards of the principal may be difficult (Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996).

[Page 265]

### Promotion

**Advertising needs:**
- **Search goods:** Communication should be designed to present as much information as possible to the customer. Information remedies are likely to be efficient in search goods (Cave, 1985). Also, information in product messages, if unique, insulates companies from adverse effects of price increases (Boulding et al., 1994). Therefore content and presentation should be information intensive, and the content of advertising can be direct.
- **Experience and credence goods:** Intangibles play a greater role; **the thrust of advertising is indirect** (Nelson, 1974). In both cases information alone will not serve the purpose. **Word-of-mouth** communication becomes important (Ekelund et al., 1995). Advertising for experience goods should be restrained so that it does not over promise. For instance, if a McDonald's commercial shows a young, smiling woman, the customer is likely to expect the same ambience at the outlet (Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996). Therefore the thrust of advertising should be indirect.
- **Credence goods:** Emphasis is on customer education. For high involvement services (long-term medical treatment, home purchase), customers are unlikely to comprehend or anticipate the service process easily. Therefore they have to be educated about each step in the process (Zeithaml and Bitner, 1996). Also, word-of-mouth for a highly rated credence good is generic (well known beyond its target audience). Since generic word-of-mouth is what drives the brand, advertising if any should be circumspect and indirect.

### Physical Evidence

**Need for physical evidence:**
- High in the case of a **search good**. Also, the perceived condition of the present unit is an important variable that drives replacement decisions in durables (Bayus and Gupta, 1992). Therefore physical evidence in the present purchase has a bearing on future purchases as well.
- **Experience goods** demand tangibles as well as intangibles.
- In the case of **credence goods** (like a doctor), the intangible (confidence in the doctor) comes first and the tangible (curing the patient) later. Therefore the insistence on tangible evidence is lower in a credence good.

**Physical Infrastructure:**
- **Search good (durable):** The showroom for a scooter or car should demonstrate some positives of the product, but the product itself is more important. Physical infrastructure is a support function.
- **Experience good (amusement park):** Physical infrastructure is the key to delivering the experience — physical surroundings become important (Lovelock, 1992).
- **Credence good:** Professional services are labor intensive (high ratio of labor cost to capital equipment cost). The expert is more important than the physical infrastructure (Lovelock, 1992). While physical infrastructure is more important in an experience good, it is less important in a credence good.

**Core vs. supplementary services:**
- **Search good:** Core product itself is important; so is the range of supplementary services (ease of billing, front office, etc.).
- **Experience good:** Core and supplementary aspects of the service are equally important. If a hotel is well kept but there is inordinate delay in billing or indifferent hospitality, its rating goes down.
- **Credence good (consultant):** The paraphernalia are less important than the advice tendered. The key issue is the expertise of the consultant. **The core service becomes more important than the supplementary elements.**

## Brand as Process

### Process of Interaction

**Search good (consumer durable):** The buyer follows certain well-known steps before reaching his decision (Engel et al., 1994). Customer requirements can be standardized through approaches like mass-customization (Wright, 2000). **Process is standardized.**

**Experience good:** Can also be standardized with time, though it demands greater variation. Disneyland treats a work shift as a performance, employees as cast members and the job description as a script (Collins and Porras, 1994). Therefore a standard book of instructions can be easily prepared because of the relatively routine nature of the workflow (Berry et al., 1990). **Process is fairly standardized; exceptions customized.**

**Credence good:** Witnesses a lot more variation. Customization and judgment in service delivery are higher in health services and education, both credence goods (Lovelock, 1992). In general, airlines, hotels and resorts (experience goods) call for less customization while professional services (credence goods) demand more. **Process is customized.**

### Degree of Customer Involvement

- **Search good:** At least some variables that contribute to the performance of the product can be separated from the customer (e.g., fuel efficiency of a scooter is independent of the customer and is an objective measure). Customer involvement: medium.
- **Experience good:** The customer cannot be separated from an experience good. His degree of involvement in the process of interaction is high. Whether the interaction is fruitful or not depends on the chemistry between customer and service provider. Thus the customer's contribution to the interaction, though important, is somewhat nebulous and difficult to judge. Customer involvement: medium to high.
- **Credence good:** Demands and gets a lot of involvement from the customer. However, the customer's contribution is only to the extent of providing inputs — a seasoned consultant is supposed to be able to isolate the wrong clues from the right ones. The patient's inputs are important, the consultant's handling of them is much more important. **The customer's role is much more ambiguous in a credence good.** Customer involvement: high.

### Content of Interaction

- **Search good:** Clear
- **Experience good:** Somewhat nebulous
- **Credence good:** Ambiguous

## Brand as Organization

### Organizational Culture

- **Search good:** Since tangibles are important, the product drives the organization. Organizational culture has to be **product driven**.
- **Experience good:** Since making the process exciting and pleasurable is what matters, the service provider has to find new ways of satisfying customers. The organization is more **innovation driven**. (Innovation in experience goods is a constant attempt to offer something new to the customer.)
- **Credence good:** Primarily **knowledge driven** because the practitioner needs to be equipped with cutting edge skills.

### Skill Set Required

- **Search good:** Relatively basic — problems posed by the customer are routine.
- **Experience good:** More various and aimed at engaging customer's interest and attention. Wal-Mart's "exceed customer expectations" and Nordstrom's "service to the customer above all" demand more than routine skills (Collins and Porras, 1994).
- **Credence good:** Even more advanced. In accounting firms, for instance, it is important to impress upon recruits the insufficiency and inadequacy of their routine preparation for the job. Those who fare well in the profession are those who break the mold of traditional role and take up challenges of unknown assignments (Fogarty, 2000). Thus skill set becomes more varied and complex from search to credence goods.

### Employee Compensation

- **Search and experience goods:** Compensation packages for average employees may not be high.
- **Credence goods:** Even frontline employees have much higher expectations and better compensation packages. Zeithaml and Bitner (1996) call the frontline employees "boundary spanners." A typical boundary spanner in an experience good is a waiter (restaurant) or a steward (airline). But a typical boundary spanner in a consultancy is a consultant, on whom often the prospects of winning or losing a high value client are hinged. The emoluments in the latter case are therefore higher. The quality of work life demanded by employees is also higher in the case of credence goods (Lovelock, 1992).

### Type of Person Needed

- **Search good:** A specialist who can solve routine problems; attention span somewhat narrowly focused.
- **Experience good:** A similar focus, but a little more flexibility — the person needs to be a specialist with a slightly broader focus.
- **Credence good:** Needs a **specialist with a very broad comprehension**. Employees associated with credence goods, despite being specialists, need a wide knowledge base and ability to deal with uncertainty. For instance, accountants are also expected today to know risk assessment, business performance measurements, electronic commerce and information systems. Such ever-increasing demands call for a wide knowledge base (Sauser, 2000).

## Brand as Person

The dimension "brand as person" reveals what happens to the brand when it is given human qualities and made a person. It is also called brand personality. Aaker (1996) defines brand personality as the set of human characteristics associated with a brand — encompassing gender, age, socio-economic class, as well as classic human personality traits such as warmth, concern and sentimentality (Aaker, 1996).

The human aspects of the brand are discussed below under: role, role expectation, personification, attitude and relationship with the customer.

### Role

- **Search good:** Service/good provider has to assume the role of a "routine problem solver."
- **Experience good:** Role of service provider is enchanting the customer — keeping the customer engaged and interested. Therefore role of the service provider is that of an **"entertainer."**
- **Credence good:** Role of service provider is consultative — that of an **"advisor."**

### Role Expectation

- **Search good:** Role expectation is that of a "doer" — emphasis is on immediate problem solving.
- **Experience good:** Communication and feedback help an experience good provider assess the gap between service delivery and customer expectations. Therefore an experience good has to be seen as a **"doer and talker."**
- **Credence good:** Role expectation is more serious — service is expected to be a **"doer and thinker."** A client would like an expert to be a thinker first and doer later — the successful delivery of service is crucially dependent on thinking rather than talking.

### Personification

- **Search good:** When personified, it becomes a **"friend"** — typically helps us in routine problem solving. Aaker and Joachimstahler (2000) — "What kind of a friend should he be? A party friend? A friend who is 'there for you'? A travelling companion? A friend you go to baseball games with? A business friend?" From the above, depending on the type of search good, it may be personified as a travelling companion (scooter) or a personal assistant (washing machine). The human personification: what **Friday** is to **Robinson Crusoe** or what **Watson** is to **Sherlock Holmes.**
- **Experience good:** Personification is that of a **"spellbinder"** — he delivers excitement, casts a spell like Merlin the magician or Charlie Chaplin.
- **Credence good:** Personification is that of a **"teacher"** — he earns confidence through wise counsel. When personified, he is an **Archimedes** or an **Einstein.**

### Attitude

- **Search good:** Attitude of a search good provider is functional — activity is routine trouble-shooting.
- **Experience goods:** Service is directed at people — there is an immediate need to "delight" customers.
- **Credence goods:** Service is often directed at the things people possess (money, property). A lawsuit or insurance claim cannot be settled immediately. The crux lies in **inspiring confidence** in the customer (Lovelock, 1992).

### Relationship with the Customer

- **Search good:** Can be imagined as a **"friendly young man."**
- **Experience good (restaurant or amusement park):** Can be visualized as **"avuncular"** and middle-aged.
- **Credence good:** Like a graying **"father-figure"** to whom one looks up for advice.

## Brand as Symbol

Since intangibles are more pronounced in services, branding them involves careful handling of symbolic aspects.

**Kapferer (1992)** opines that a brand is "a symbol, a word, an object and a concept — all at one and the same time." He states ". . . the flying red horse of Mobil, or the tree or the emblem of bull . . . have been deliberately chosen to reflect their brand's personality and culture. Personality and certain other values are considered of primary importance among the guide lines governing a company's design and graphic identity program."

**Aaker and Joachimstahler (2000):** "A symbol can provide cohesion and structure to an identity, making it easier to achieve recognition and recall." Symbols can be anything that represents the brand:
- A tagline ("Nobody doesn't like Sara Lee")
- A character (the Pillsbury doughboy)
- A visual metaphor (the Prudential rock)
- A logo (Nike's swoosh)
- A color (Kodak's yellow)
- A gesture (Alstate's "good hands")
- A musical note (Hellmannn's Mayonnaise)
- A package (the blue cylinder for Morton Salt)
- A program (the Ronald McDonald House Charities)

### Adjectives for the Brand

Projective techniques help us understand the idea behind a brand — they convert a brand into an adjective, or an animal, or an emotion or a person.
- **Search good (washing machine):** Should be handy, dependable, function without breakdowns. Adjectives: **"useful"** or **"convenient."**
- **Experience good:** Descriptive adjectives are **"lively"** and **"vibrant."** A typical symbol/logo for such a service would be "a smiling face." Brand name for an experience good could be "Fun-and-Frolic" or "Dreams Unlimited."
- **Credence good:** Adjectives are **"deep"** and **"wise."** A typical logo: "Rodin's Thinker." A typical brand name for a consultant: probably "Synergy" or "Gestalt" to reflect the values that go with the service.

### Typical Slogan

- **Search good:** "Non-stop performance" — reflects convenience and ease-of-use.
- **Experience good:** More enticing slogan (e.g. "Come, discover").
- **Credence good:** Slogans that reflect a high degree of competence (e.g. "The Complete Solution" people).

### Typical Sponsorship

- **Search good:** Probably sponsor a chat show that suggests remedies for household problems (in line with what the search good stands for — problem solving).
- **Experience good:** Should sponsor an event identified with celebration of life (e.g. Food Carnival or World Tour).
- **Credence good:** May like to sponsor an event that is cerebral (e.g. chess competition).

## Summary and Limitations

This paper should be viewed as a tentative, initial attempt at conceptualizing the branding of a service. There is a lot of literature in services marketing addressing different aspects of the 7Ps. This paper brings together some of that knowledge, blends it with principles of branding and uses it to brand services. Special emphasis has been placed on the intangible aspects, namely, brand as a person and brand as a symbol, something that has not been adequately addressed so far. The framework suggested, therefore, is a first approximation. Consequently it has its limitations. Since it is a proposed conceptual model, it needs testing and validation.

Conceptual limitations:
- If the service brand under consideration is a multi-product brand or a corporate brand, this approach cannot be used directly. Frameworks related to corporate branding will have to be integrated with this approach to brand a multi-product entity like GE.
- The present framework can be fine-tuned by including several nuances of the 7Ps as presented in services marketing literature.

## Executive Summary and Implications for Managers and Executives

*(Précis supplied by Marketing Consultants for Emerald)*

Moorthi presents a comprehensive approach for branding services by applying David Aaker's brand identity framework (brand as product, organization, person and symbol) to the economic classification of three types of goods (search, experience and credence goods) and the 7Ps of services marketing (product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence, process and people).

**Brand as product:** Most physical products are "search" goods. Services tend to fall into "experience" and "credence" categories. A restaurant visit is an "experience" product — the customer judges the totality, from the food served to the manner in which it is served and the ambience. A "credence" product (e.g. a visit to a consultant) involves the greatest intangibility and is mainly chosen for its reputation. For an experience or credence good, the service provider can never be separated from the service. Perceived risk is greatest for credence goods and least for search goods.

**Brand as process:** Customer requirements can be standardized for search goods through approaches like mass customization. An experience good can also be standardized with time, although it demands greater variation. A credence good involves much more variation. At least some variables that contribute to the performance of a search good can be separated from the customer. In contrast, the customer cannot be separated from an experience good. With a credence good, the customer's role is more ambiguous.

**Brand as organization:** The organization must be product driven for search goods, innovation driven for experience goods and knowledge driven for credence goods. The skills needed become more varied and complex as one moves from search goods through experience goods to credence goods. Employee compensation also needs to be higher for credence goods.

**Brand as person:** The service provider is routine problem solver for a search good, entertainer for an experience good and advisor for a credence good. He or she is a "doer" for search goods, doer and talker for experience goods and thinker and doer for credence goods. The personified search good becomes a friend, the personified experience good is a spellbinder and the personified credence good is a teacher.

**Brand as symbol:** Suitable adjectives for a search good are "useful" or "convenient," while "lively" and "vibrant" may suit an experience good, and "deep" and "wise" a credence good. A search good might sponsor a chat show that suggests remedies for household problems, an experience good might sponsor a food carnival or world tour and a credence good a chess championship.

## Services Branding Framework — Expanded

This document presents a comprehensive framework in the form of a large table that cross-references the **5 elements of service branding** (drawn from the Brand Identity Planning Model adapted for services) with the **7Ps of services marketing** integrated into those 5 elements, and then maps how each element behaves across three categories of goods/services: **Search Good**, **Experience Good**, and **Credence Good**.

### Framework Description

The table has 5 columns:
1. **5 elements of service branding** (left-most column, with merged cells spanning multiple rows)
2. **7Ps integrated into the 5 elements** (second column)
3. **Search Good** (third column)
4. **Experience Good** (fourth column)
5. **Credence Good** (fifth column)

The three good types are defined as follows:
- **Search Good**: Quality can be assessed before purchase (e.g., a hardware store — you can examine the product)
- **Experience Good**: Quality can only be assessed after or during consumption (e.g., a restaurant, a movie)
- **Credence Good**: Quality cannot be easily assessed even after consumption; requires trust in the provider (e.g., a doctor, a lawyer, a financial advisor)

### Interpretive Notes on the Framework

**Reading the framework across rows:**

- **Brand as product — Product**: For a search good, the product should "never fail" (reliability is the key since customers chose it based on pre-evaluated criteria). For an experience good, it should be "convenient, pleasant" (since quality is evaluated during use). For a credence good, it should be "enriching, clarifying" (since the customer cannot evaluate the quality — the service must educate and add value).

- **Brand as product — Physical evidence**: For search goods, physically demonstrate the product's attributes. For experience goods, create delight through physical environment. For credence goods, use physical evidence to build confidence (e.g., a doctor's diplomas on the wall, a lawyer's tidy office).

- **Brand as product — Price**: Search good = going rate (customer comparison shops). Experience good = value for money (assessed retrospectively). Credence good = perceived value (customer has no reference point; perception of value is constructed).

- **Brand as product — Place**: Search good = close to the customer (accessibility is a competitive factor). Experience good = reasonable distance (customer will travel if the experience is worth it). Credence good = distance not an issue (for complex credence services like brain surgery, patients travel globally).

- **Brand as product — Promotion**: Search good = provide information (factual). Experience good = word-of-mouth (peer endorsement builds confidence). Credence good = general word-of-mouth (broader reputation signals trustworthiness).

- **Brand as process**: Search good = standardized with some customization. Experience good = standardized with more customization. Credence good = standardized with lot more customization. The logic: the harder quality is to evaluate, the more the customer expects the process to be tailored to their specific situation.

- **Brand as organization — People**: Search good = basic skills. Experience good = more evolved skill set. Credence good = complex skill set. Credence goods require the highest expertise since the customer depends entirely on the provider's competence.

- **Brand as person**: Search good = problem solver (functional role). Experience good = entertainer (relational/hedonic role). Credence good = advisor (consultative, trusted role).

- **Brand as symbol (should reflect)**: Search good = useful (functional symbolism). Experience good = lively (expressive/energetic symbolism). Credence good = wise (authoritative, trustworthy symbolism).

### Summary Insight

The framework reveals that as we move from Search to Experience to Credence goods, the nature of service branding shifts from functional performance and information provision towards emotional engagement and trust-building. For credence goods especially, the Brand as Person (as advisor) and Brand as Symbol (as wise) dimensions become critical because the customer cannot rely on direct evaluation and must instead rely on reputation, relationships, and signals of expertise.

ABSTRACT
The article aims at making a conceptual contribution to services
marketing literature by proposing an eighth “P” called “proof.”
Proof is a simple operational measure or an intuitive indicator
that helps the customer quickly assess the efficacy of a service.
The article discusses services marketing literature and develops
propositions that underscore the importance of proof as the
eighth P. When proof is provided about a service to the customer,
it affects all service outcomes (i.e., service evaluation, purchase
intent, customer satisfaction, and repurchase intent). Though
conceptual, the article provides practical implications as well.
Introduction
A service in marketing is typically specified through its marketing mix, popularly
known as the 7Ps. The 7Ps are product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence,
process, and people (Booms & Bitner, 1981). However, what the 7Ps do not cover
is a measure or an indicator of the usefulness of the service to the consumer. This
is where we propose an eighth “P” of services marketing by name of “proof.” Proof
is making good the service promise and would help to measure if the service were
delivered well through 7Ps. It is an indicator intuitively understood by the customer
(e.g., “Few customers if ever complain about our service”) or is expressed as a num-
ber (e.g., “97% of our customers received feedback on time”).
Proof is different from other measures of services or the 7Ps. While physical evi-
dence is related to factors such as signage, employee dress, and store layout, proof
goes beyond that and provides a metric either from the present or the past that
gives confidence to the customer in service. Similarly, while processes would enable
a customer to evaluate service aspects, proof of how smooth or efficient the same
processes are would stimulate them to select/consume the service. Thus, proof bor-
rows from all the 7Ps, past records, dynamically presented evidence online, and
third-party certifications. Proof can alter perceptions about a service, and hence

service literature viz.: (a) servicescape; (b) service quality; (c) SLA, and (d) PoS.

Service evaluation and proof
The first proposition relates service evaluation to proof. Researchers have found var-

by the service provider. (Gautam, 2013) found service recovery to be correlated pos-

given evidence to the contrary. In addition, alerting customers to the possibility of a
rating bias on either side reduces the tendency to give negative evaluations. Instead,
if effective and positive proof is offered about a service, it brings down the tendency
to rate it negatively. The parameters of service evaluation are set by the expectations
of the customer. The service provider should satisfy them to the best of their abilities
(Alden, He, & Chen, 2010). It is important therefore that the service provider gives
the customer a proof that they value. We therefore, propose:

customers would rate a service more favorably even if there is a negative service
experience if: (a) the reasons are beyond control, (b) employees are seen as making
effort to address the problem, and (c) the service provider is generally known for
delivering good service. In all the mentioned cases, providing appropriate proof can
give greater room for the service provider in handling negative service experiences.

Proposition 2: A reliable service suffers less despite a negative customer experience, if it

Customer satisfaction and proof
Customer satisfaction or dissatisfaction has several sources such as the basic ser-
vice quality, employees (Guchait, Namasivayam, & Lei, 2011), service failure sever-
ity (Weun, Beatty, & Jones, 2004), and service firm’s culture (Ame, 2013). However,
when there is service recovery, satisfaction improves remarkably (Maxham, 2001).
Proof here seems to be a function of promptness of action. Therefore, proof artic-
ulated intelligently by employees can have a salutary effect on customer satisfac-
tion. Finally, the service outcome itself is crucial for customer satisfaction. Litera-
ture shows that if the outcome is satisfactory, lapses in service experience tend to
be ignored (Park, Cho, & Rao, 2012). In this aspect, Dabholkar and Overby (2005)
found that desired service outcomes are more important than desirable processes. In
other words, tangible proof the customer sees as output is more important than ser-
vice experience itself. Therefore, proof provided to customer during or at the exit
of service experience can positively affect satisfaction. This leads us to the fourth
proposition:
Proposition 4: Providing relevant proof affects customer satisfaction positively.
Repurchase intent and proof
Repurchase is based on the firsthand experience of the customer with the service and
overall customer satisfaction is an important trigger for repurchase (Tang, Jia, Zhou,
& Yin, 2010). Therefore, how the service encounter itself is handled is important for
repurchase (Park, Cho, & Rao, 2012). Maxham, and Netemeyer (2002) opined that
repurchase intent dips sharply if there are two consecutive service failures without
recovery. Hume (2008) hence contended that both core (e.g., the food served in a
restaurant) and supplementary (e.g., the steward smiling when they serve) compo-
nents of the service are important for repurchase. Consequently, a proof at the cor-
porate brand level might serve as a booster for repurchase (e.g., no IndiGo airlines
flight was rescheduled between December 1, 2013, and January 31, 2014, therefore
it is a reliable airline). This leads to the next proposition:
Proposition 5: Proof that underscores the strength of the corporate brand influences
repurchase.

The Model
Combining the propositions and the 7Ps of services marketing we create the con-
ceptual model in Figure 1. Thus, we propose that the 7Ps should lead to the concept
of proof that would in turn facilitate service evaluation, purchase intent, satisfac-
tion, and repurchase intent. We are not undermining the role of the 7Ps of services
marketing, but only highlighting the role of the new P (proof) in the same model.
The propositions in the model when tested, will establish the importance of proof
as a concept. They can collectively demonstrate that appropriate proof when pro-
vided to the customer impacts service outcomes across the board. These range from
service evaluation through purchase intent and customer satisfaction to repurchase
intent. If this framework is tested and accepted then proof can be called the eighth P
of the service marketing mix. Proof is in itself a consequence of the synergy between
the elements in the 7Ps. However, it acquires a meaning of its own for the customer.
When presented appropriately for a given context, proof can make a fundamental
impact on service evaluation, purchase intent, customer satisfaction, and repurchase
intent at every stage.
Conclusion
The present study establishes the role of proof as the eighth P of services market-
ing. However, the model and arguments developed in this present study are only
at a conceptual level and need empirical validation. Thereby the study only pro-
vides a new direction that has to be tested formally using empirical data. Besides,
the propositions need to be augmented using anecdotal and theoretical evidence
to create testable hypotheses. However, the study has its theoretical implication in
providing a new P of services marketing and provides managers a new direction to
think in terms of service creation and delivery.
