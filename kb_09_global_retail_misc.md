## 3 Paths to Globalization

This document presents a single comparative table contrasting three distinct organizational orientations or paths that a multinational company can adopt for its global strategy. These are: **Ethnocentric (Blinkered)**, **Geocentric (Consultative)**, and **Polycentric (Autonomous)**.

*Based on: STRATEGIC PLANNING FOR A GLOBAL BUSINESS: BALAJI CHAKRAVARTY AND HOWARD PERLMULTER, COLUMBIA JOURNAL OF WORLD BUSINESS, SUMMER'85.*

### The Three Paths to Globalization — Comparative Framework

The framework compares the three orientations across six dimensions: Goal Setting, Strategy, Product Planning, Marketing Mix Decisions, Structure, and Culture.

| Item | Ethnocentric (Blinkered) | Geocentric (Consultative) | Polycentric (Autonomous) |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Goal Setting** | Top Down | Negotiated at all levels of the corporation | Bottom Up |
| **Strategy** | Home Centered | Global Integrative & NR (National Responsiveness) | National Responsiveness (NR) |
| **Product Planning** | Product development focus home country needs/customers | Global product with local variations | Local product based on local needs |
| **Marketing Mix Decisions** | Made at headquarters | Made jointly after consultations | Made in each country |
| **Structure** | Hierarchical | Network of organisations | Hierarchy with autonomy to national units |
| **Culture** | Home country | Global | Host country |

### Detailed Explanation of Each Path

**1. Ethnocentric (Blinkered)**

In the Ethnocentric orientation, the home country is the centre of the universe. Goal setting is top-down — headquarters decides and subsidiaries execute. Strategy is home-centred, meaning the company extends its domestic strategy to foreign markets without significant adaptation. Product development focuses on home country needs and customers; foreign markets receive the same products. Marketing mix decisions are made entirely at headquarters. The organizational structure is hierarchical, with authority concentrated at the centre. The prevailing culture is the home country culture, imposed on all operations worldwide.

*The term "blinkered" in the document title signals that this approach is characterised by a narrow, home-country-biased worldview that fails to account for diversity in global markets.*

**2. Geocentric (Consultative)**

In the Geocentric orientation, the company attempts to balance global integration with national responsiveness. Goal setting is negotiated at all levels of the corporation — neither purely top-down nor purely bottom-up. Strategy is simultaneously globally integrative AND nationally responsive (dual imperative). Product planning produces global products but with local variations accommodated. Marketing mix decisions are made jointly after consultations between headquarters and local units. The structure is a network of organisations (rather than a hierarchy), reflecting interdependence. The culture is explicitly Global — transcending any single home or host country.

*This is generally considered the most sophisticated and difficult-to-implement orientation, requiring genuine collaboration across geographies.*

**3. Polycentric (Autonomous)**

In the Polycentric orientation, local subsidiaries are largely autonomous and self-directing. Goal setting is bottom-up — local units set their own objectives. Strategy emphasises National Responsiveness above all; each market is treated as distinct. Product planning produces local products based entirely on local needs. Marketing mix decisions are made independently in each country. The organizational structure retains hierarchy but grants significant autonomy to national units. The prevailing culture is the host country culture in each market.

*The term "autonomous" captures the defining feature: each national unit operates largely independently. This can lead to duplication of effort and inability to leverage global scale, but ensures deep local relevance.*

### Key Takeaways for Brand Management

- Ethnocentric firms tend to have uniform global brands (with no local adaptation), which may fail in culturally distant markets.
- Polycentric firms may end up with fragmented brand identities — each market develops its own version of the brand, losing global coherence.
- Geocentric firms attempt to build global brands that are coherent at the core but locally adapted at the periphery — what is often called "glocal" branding (global core + local expression).
- The choice of orientation has fundamental implications for brand identity, brand communication, and brand management structure.

## Overview

This article introduces the fundamental concept of customer value as the foundation of marketing strategy. It explains what value means, how it is created and communicated, and how the concept of value is central to brand building and brand management.

---

[Page 1]

## 1.0 What is Customer Value?

Customer value is at the heart of marketing. A brand's success ultimately depends on its ability to deliver value to the customer. Value is not just about the price paid — it is the customer's perception of what they receive relative to what they give up.

Value can be expressed as:

> **Value = Benefits / Costs**

Where:
- **Benefits** include functional benefits (what the product does), emotional benefits (how it makes you feel), and self-expressive benefits (what it says about you)
- **Costs** include monetary cost (price), time cost, effort cost, and psychological cost

A brand creates value when the customer perceives that the benefits received outweigh the costs incurred.

---

[Page 2]

## 2.0 Types of Benefits

### 2.1 Functional Benefits
These are the tangible, verifiable utilities delivered by the product. They answer the question: "What does this product do for me?" Examples:
- A car that delivers 25 km per litre of fuel
- A detergent that removes stains in a single wash
- A bank that processes loans in 24 hours

Functional benefits appeal to the rational mind. They are the easiest to communicate and the easiest to copy.

### 2.2 Emotional Benefits
These are the feelings a product evokes in the consumer. They answer the question: "How does this make me feel?" Examples:
- A greeting card that evokes nostalgia
- An insurance policy that gives peace of mind
- A movie that makes you laugh

Emotional benefits are harder to copy because they depend on the accumulated experience, heritage, and communication history of the brand.

### 2.3 Self-Expressive Benefits
These are the benefits a product delivers by saying something about the person who uses it. They answer the question: "What does this say about me?" Examples:
- Wearing a Rolex signals success and taste
- Driving a Tesla signals environmental consciousness and technological forward-thinking
- Using Apple products signals creativity and design consciousness

Self-expressive benefits are tied to the social and psychological identity of the user.

---

[Page 3]

## 3.0 Types of Costs

### 3.1 Monetary Cost
The price paid for the product or service. This is the most obvious cost and the one most commonly used in marketing communications around value.

### 3.2 Time Cost
The time the customer must invest to acquire, use, or learn a product. A brand that reduces time cost creates value. For example:
- Quick service restaurants reduce time cost compared to fine dining
- Mobile banking reduces the time cost of conducting financial transactions

### 3.3 Effort Cost
The physical or cognitive effort required to use the product. Products that are intuitive and easy to use reduce effort cost. Apple's "ease of use" is a classic example of minimizing effort cost.

### 3.4 Psychological Cost
The mental discomfort, anxiety or risk associated with a purchase decision. A new, unknown brand imposes higher psychological cost than a trusted, familiar brand. This is why brand building is important — it reduces psychological cost for the buyer by signalling reliability and quality.

---

[Page 4]

## 4.0 Value Creation and the Marketing Mix

Value is created through all elements of the marketing mix:

- **Product**: functional benefits are embedded in the product design, features, and quality
- **Price**: price signals value (too low signals poor quality; too high signals poor value for money). Pricing must be consistent with the value proposition
- **Place (Distribution)**: reducing time and effort costs by making the product available where and when the customer wants it
- **Promotion (Communication)**: communicating the value proposition clearly and credibly. If the customer is not aware of the benefits, value cannot be realized

The key insight is that value is ultimately **perceived** by the customer. A product may have excellent functional benefits, but if the customer is unaware of them or does not believe in them, value is not created in the customer's mind.

### 4.1 The Role of the Brand in Value Creation
A brand is a repository of value. Strong brands:
- Reduce search costs for the customer
- Reduce psychological risk by acting as a quality signal
- Add emotional and self-expressive benefits on top of functional benefits
- Command price premiums because customers are willing to pay more for trusted brands

Brands thus allow firms to extract more value from their products than would be possible without branding.

---

[Page 5]

## 5.0 The Value Proposition

A value proposition is the complete statement of value a brand makes to its target customer. It answers the question: "Why should you choose this brand over all the alternatives?"

An effective value proposition:
- Is relevant to the target customer segment
- Is differentiated from competitors
- Is credible and deliverable
- Encompasses functional, emotional, and/or self-expressive benefits

### 5.1 Value Proposition and Brand Identity
The value proposition is the output of the brand identity planning process. After conducting strategic brand analysis (customer analysis, competitor analysis, self-analysis) and defining the brand identity (brand as product, organization, person, and symbol), the strategist arrives at the value proposition.

The value proposition has three components:
1. **Functional benefits**: the rational benefits delivered
2. **Emotional benefits**: the feelings evoked
3. **Self-expressive benefits**: the identity signals conveyed

Not every brand needs to deliver all three. A budget brand may primarily deliver functional and emotional benefits. A luxury brand may primarily deliver emotional and self-expressive benefits.

### 5.2 Customer Value and Competitive Advantage
A brand that delivers superior customer value achieves competitive advantage. Superior value means:
- More benefits than competitors at the same cost, or
- The same benefits at lower cost than competitors, or
- Unique benefits (that competitors cannot replicate) at a justifiable cost

The ultimate test of brand success is whether the brand creates genuine, sustainable value for its customers — value that keeps them coming back, makes them willing to pay a premium, and turns them into advocates.

## How to write quizs

A student often has a question "What is a good answer? What is a bad answer? How do I know? How do I know my answer is a loser?

Ask yourself "Is your answer likely to look like your neighbor's?". Then, in all probability, both of you will lose marks. The more the number of people that write an answer, the less credit the answer gets.

There are, of course, standard excuses. "We prepared together, so our answers are similar". That is a lot of baloney! If a group of 4 study together, why don't you think of 4 illustrations for the same concept? Then all of you will benefit from brain storming.

In short, show novelty in your answers. That is the surest way to score. Ideally no two answers in the class should look alike.

Just to make sure you understand, let me also show you **some bad answers for quiz questions.**

**Q1)** What are the limitations of marketing mix? (let us say this is the question).

**Silly ans 1** - Give weightages for the items in this conceptual framework.

*[The original document shows a diagram of the Marketing Mix framework with "Marketing Mix" at the top, connected to four sub-elements: Product (product variety, quality, design, features, brand name, packaging, sizes, services, warranties, returns), Price (list price, discounts, allowances, payment period, credit terms), Place (channels, coverage, assortments, locations, inventory, transport), and Promotion (sales promotion, advertising, sales force, public relations, direct marketing), with "Target Market" at the centre.]*

**Comment 1** - See the above framework below. What the answer is saying is that each of the elements under 4Ps (e.g. – under Product we have elements like product variety, quality, design, features etc. Similarly, under Price we have list price, discounts, allowances and so on. The answer proposes that each of the elements be given numerical weightages. Then it is more rigorous/useful).

This is honestly a terrible answer (even if one wants to be charitable to student responses). How many theoretical frameworks have seen like balanced score card or Porter's 5 forces? How many of them have weightages? They cannot have weightages precisely because they are general purpose frameworks. The weightages depend on the product category, right? If the product is steel, the elements under the 4Ps will acquire certain weights, if it is cola then weights will be different and so on. This becomes obvious when we think.

**Silly ans 2** - 1/4th of the class could think of only McDonalds not using beef filling in India as an example of cultural convention.

**Comment 2** – it is obvious that they copied from one another. If not that, then the excuse is we prepared together (I already mentioned how that can be overcome). There are hundred and twenty languages in this country, countless dialects, 29 States. All our friends could think of was beef in the burger. For instance, for a festival called Pola in Maharastra they color the horns of bulls. The same is done for Maattu Pongal in Tamil Nadu and Kanumu in AP/Telangana (this is the 3rd day of Pongal/Sankranti, usually 15 Jan). This cultural convention offers an interesting opportunity for paints companies to sell their paint. One is looking for answers like this.

**Q3)** Give an eg of an innovative campaign on social media?

**Silly ans 3** – 1/3rd of class could only think of Google Doodle.

**Comment 3** - Meaning 25 guys in the class couldn't think of anything else in social media. There are a thousand stories that went viral on Whatsapp. Don't they know even one? Such poverty of preparation and imagination!

### Three more notes of caution on quizs

> **Note 1** (in red in original) - You cannot repeat the examples given by other presenting groups, illustrations discussed in class or examples given by faculty in quizs or presentations. Quiz answers must be your examples. You are expected to read up business press and get interesting info. Remember, this is an exercise to test your originality not your memorizing capability.

> **Note 2** (in red in original) – All egs should relate to Indian market. Do not give egs of US or Europe. You can however cite illustrations of Walmart or Caterpillar in India (not Wal mart of Cat in US). Or you can cite Indian brands like Mother's Diary and Infosys. In short you can give illustrations of MNCs or Indian Co.s wrt their Indian marketing operations.

> **Note 3** (in red in original) - Two e.g.s are banned in all BM quizs, namely Patanjali and Apple. These two have become stock answers for all marketing questions, hence banned. You will get negative marks if I see Patanjali or Apple in your quiz answers.

### Presentations

What are bad presentations?

Presentations that have motherhood, twenty thousand feet statements. A typical e.g. of such a statement is say "Dabur should be innovative". This is nonsense. To put it in the lingo you understand, this is GLOBE!

So, when is something GLOBE?

Consider the statement - "Dabur should be innovative". In this answer, the name "Dabur" can be substituted by any other company like HP, Microsoft, Mahindra tractors, Athica or even your mess. This is smoke-and-mirrors! Your answer is supposed to tell how exactly Dabur should be innovative. An interesting answer is, say, "branding neem sticks". To the extent I know, no FMCG company has branded neem sticks in India. Both in urban and rural India this product can click. Bcos urban Indians are looking for "green" products. Rural folks use neem sticks anyway for brushing.

---

[Page 4]

So also consider the statement - Whirlpool should be innovative. Here also the expectation is you write how exactly Whirlpool can become innovative. For instance, Whirlpool can launch washing m/cs that understand voice commands in Indian languages. That sure sounds like innovation.

Look at this answer - Marico should be innovative. Sure, but how exactly? For instance, by offering hair oil with customizable perfume.

Etc….etc….

This is how you make your answer interesting/original. In fact, the expectation is even less. The expectation is that you just read real life examples of innovation in marketing about brands in the Indian market and cite them in your presentations and quizs. (in red in original) That will more or less see you through.

What really helps is understanding the kind of questions he might ask and preparing accordingly. It will be a long paper, around 3 hours, but you probably won’t finish it. I didn’t even complete half of it, as far as I remember.

The questions are usually theoretical but need to be explained using egs. Like - Explain XYZ with the help of an eg. He also expects references and citations in the paper...you have to write the link, page number, etc., which takes a lot of time. What helped me was preparing a list of unique brand egs in advance and taking that with me. I had also uploaded articles, slides, and the textbook on NotebookLM. When a question came, I first used it to understand the theory. For eg, if the concept was brand resonance, I would first understand the concept there and then I would look at my list of egs and see which one could fit. After selecting one, I would find supporting evidence online, such as an article discussing that example, and cite it.

The examples were the most helpful part, because it’s hard to think of good ones during the exam. Also, he doesn’t like common examples like Amul or Maggi, and some examples are explicitly banned, so you need to know which ones you cannot use. So I would strongly suggest taking a well-prepared list of examples.

Studying the concepts in depth beforehand isn’t very useful because you don’t know what he will ask. But take the textbook with you. The index or keyword list at the back can help...if NotebookLM doesn’t give you an answer, you can find where the keyword appears in the book, read that section to understand the concept, and then attach an eg.
