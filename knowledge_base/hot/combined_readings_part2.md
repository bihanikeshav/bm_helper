# SECTION 2: Journal Articles — Brand Extensions and Architecture

## Article: "Managing Brand Extensions"
**Author:** David A. Aaker
**Publication:** California Management Review (or similar; adapted from Aaker's brand management work)
**PDF Pages:** ~108–175

[Page 108]

### Introduction

A **brand extension** occurs when a brand established in one product category is used to enter another product category. Brand extensions are extremely common — the majority of new products introduced are extensions rather than entirely new brands.

**Why firms use brand extensions:**
- Reduce financial risk of new product introduction
- Reduce consumer risk (existing brand name provides familiarity and assurance)
- Increase efficiency of advertising and promotion
- Facilitate new product acceptance by retailers
- Provide feedback benefits to parent brand

[Page 110]

### The Good: When Brand Extensions Work

**Factors that make extensions succeed:**

**1. Fit**

Consumers evaluate extensions based on perceived fit between the parent brand and the extension category. Fit operates on several dimensions:

- **Product feature similarity** — how similar are the attributes and features of the extension to those of the parent brand's products?
- **Benefit similarity** — do the products provide similar benefits?
- **Usage similarity** — are the products used in similar ways, at similar times, in similar situations?
- **User similarity** — are the products used by similar consumers?
- **Brand concept consistency** — does the extension fit the overall brand image and concept?

[Page 112]

**Concept consistency (Park, Milberg, and Lawson 1991):**
- **Function-oriented brands** (associated with reliability, durability, performance) extend well to products where these attributes are relevant
- **Prestige-oriented brands** extend well when products share the same prestige image

Examples of successful fit-based extensions:
- Jell-O Pudding Pops: Jell-O's "fun dessert" image transferred to pudding on a stick
- Ivory Soap → Ivory Shampoo (cleanliness association transferred)
- Arm & Hammer baking soda → Arm & Hammer toothpaste (baking soda as cleaning agent)
- Honda (small engine expertise) → lawnmowers, motorcycles, cars, generators

[Page 115]

**2. Transferability of Skills and Assets**

An extension works when the parent brand's underlying skills and assets can be applied in the new category:
- Technology or manufacturing expertise
- Marketing skills
- Distribution relationships
- Customer relationships

Example: Honda's expertise in small engine technology is highly transferable across motorcycles, lawnmowers, cars, and marine engines. This credibility transfer reduces perceived risk for consumers.

**3. Complementarity**

Products can be complementary — used together. This makes extensions natural:
- Colgate toothbrush extends Colgate toothpaste
- Gillette blades extend Gillette razor handles

**One basis of fit is sufficient:** Even if only one dimension of fit exists, an extension can work. Credibility problems are reduced if another firm manufactures the extension (licensing).

[Page 120]

### The Bad: When Brand Extensions Fail

**Reasons for poor extension quality perceptions:**

**Poor fit (Tab Cola → Tab flavor extensions):**
Tab was known specifically as a diet cola for women. Tab tried to extend to chocolate cake mixes and other flavors. Consumers could not reconcile Tab's diet cola image with other food products. The extensions failed.

**Category signals violated:**
Some categories have mental models of what a "proper" product should look like. Extensions that violate these signals fail. Example: Levi's tried to extend to suits (too formal for a casual denim brand).

[Page 125]

**Lack of credibility:**
The parent brand may not have the expertise or reputation to compete in the new category. Example: a beer brand extending to wine — consumers doubt the expertise transfer.

### The Ugly: When Brand Extensions Damage the Parent Brand

This is the most serious risk — the extension harms the equity of the parent brand.

[Page 130]

**Undesirable Associations Are Created:**

- **Sunkist Fruit Rolls:** Created unwanted health-food association in a brand known for fresh oranges
- **Black & Decker:** Extended the Black & Decker name to professional tools, but the "amateur/DIY" image of Black & Decker undermined the professional market → solution was to create a new brand: DeWalt
- **Sears Financial Network:** Sears extended into financial services. Consumers questioned whether a department store should be managing their investments; also created confusion about Sears' identity
- **Carnation Pet Food:** The Carnation name (associated with wholesome dairy products for people) created uncomfortable associations when applied to pet food

**Notable counterexample:** Honey Nut Cheerios did NOT damage the Cheerios brand equity. Diet Coke did not damage Coca-Cola's image. These worked because the extension actually reinforced the parent brand's positioning.

[Page 133]

**Existing Associations Are Weakened:**

- **Kleenex, Perrier, Tampax** — these brands are so strongly identified with their categories that any extension risks weakening the category-synonym status
- **Cadbury** extended into mashed potatoes, dried milk, and soups. Each successive extension made Cadbury seem like a "commodity food company" rather than a premium chocolate brand
- **Scott/ScotTowels/ScotTissue/Scotties** — overlapping extensions within the Scott family created confusion about what each sub-brand stood for
- **Jell-O Pudding Pops, Gelatin Pops, Fruit Bars** — each extension diluted the others; the Jell-O brand became associated with too many items

[Page 137]

**Quality Image Is Affected:**

- **Lacoste alligator overextension:** Lacoste licensed the crocodile logo to such a wide range of products (shoes, underwear, bags, watches, perfume) that the brand's premium sports-chic image was diluted. 14,000 different Gucci products at one point had similarly diluted the Gucci brand
- **Cadillac Cimarron:** General Motors tried to create a more affordable Cadillac (the Cimarron) to compete with BMW and Mercedes in the entry-level luxury segment. Instead, the car was widely seen as a Chevrolet Cavalier with a Cadillac badge, significantly damaging the Cadillac luxury image
- **Audi 5000:** The sudden acceleration crisis hurt the Audi brand. Significantly, it hurt the Audi 4000 more than the Audi Quattro. A depreciation study found differential impact: the luxury/performance Quattro retained value better because its stronger, distinct image provided protection

[Page 140]

**A Disaster Occurs:**

- **Ivory model pornography scandal** — Procter & Gamble's Ivory brand was damaged by a model who appeared in pornographic materials; had to be managed
- **Tylenol tampering (1982)** — cyanide-poisoned capsules in Chicago → massive brand crisis
- **Rely products** (also P&G) — tampons linked to toxic shock syndrome; product withdrawn; brand image seriously damaged
- **The "disaster" insight:** Brands with strong, specific, distinct identities (like the Audi Quattro) are more resilient to disasters than brands with vague, diluted identities

[Page 143]

**The Brand Franchise Is Cannibalized:**

- **Gillette Right Guard / Good News:** Good News, Gillette's disposable razor, cannibalized premium Gillette razor sales
- **Campbell's Chunky / Home Cookin' / Golden Classics / Soup-for-One:** Campbell's multiplied soup varieties, with each new variety cannibalizing others. Eventually launched Prego as a separate brand for tomato-based pasta sauces — recognizing that a separate brand was needed

[Page 147]

**A New Brand Name Is Foregone — More Ugly:**

Sometimes the biggest cost of extension is the failure to build a powerful new brand:
- **P&G's 80+ brands:** P&G has created many distinct, powerful brands (Tide, Pampers, Crest, etc.) — each with its own equity. In contrast, a company that always extends its corporate name misses the opportunity to create these powerful sub-brands
- **Macintosh vs. Apple 360 hypothetical:** What if Apple had pursued a pure brand extension strategy rather than building the Macintosh as a distinct identity?
- **Hewlett-Packard handicap:** HP's heavy use of sub-branding (HP Pavilion, HP Officejet, etc.) created a proliferation that sometimes weakened the clarity of the HP brand promise
- **Campbell's Prego:** Campbell's eventually recognized Prego needed to be a separate brand from Campbell's to compete in the pasta sauce category
- **Honda/Acura Europe example:** Honda separated the Acura brand in North America but sold identical cars under the Honda brand in Europe — a natural experiment showing that the same product can command different perceptions and prices depending on the brand architecture

[Page 152]

### Strategy Considerations

**Five strategic considerations for brand extensions:**

**1. Creating future extensions:**
Think about the brand as a platform for future extensions. Does the current extension leave room for logical future extensions? A too-specific extension (Tab chocolate cake) forecloses future options.

**2. Using nested brand names:**
Nested brand names (e.g., Healthy Choice Hearty Soups) can help signal both the parent brand's promise and the specific variant's characteristics.

**3. Hedging your bets:**
Consider using a separate brand name for risky extensions, to protect the parent brand equity if the extension fails.

**4. Maximizing comparative advantage — product life cycle:**
Extensions should leverage the parent brand's advantages at the optimal stage of the product life cycle. Early in a new category's development, the parent brand provides acceptance and credibility. Later, a distinct sub-brand may be needed to compete on specific category attributes.

**5. Protecting and nurturing the brand name:**
The parent brand name is a precious asset. Extensions that risk diluting, contaminating, or weakening the parent brand should be avoided or carefully managed.

[Page 158]

## Article: "Brand extensions — when to extend and when not to?"
**Author:** Jean Noel Kapferer
**Publication:** Note from Strategic Brand Management, 4th Edition
**PDF Pages:** ~176–195

[Page 176]

### The Strategic Evaluation Grid for Brand Extensions

Kapferer proposes a comprehensive evaluation framework — a checklist of questions that any brand team should work through before committing to an extension decision. This is the "extension strategic evaluation grid."

### Full Checklist of Extension Evaluation Factors

[Page 177]

#### 1. Is it a growing market?

**The logic:** Entering a declining or stagnant market limits the potential even for a well-executed extension. Growth markets offer more room for new entrants.

**Indian market examples:**
- **Talcum powder vs. deodorants:** Talcum powder is a declining/stagnant market in India. Deodorants are a fast-growing market. An extension into talcum powder would face structural headwinds; an extension into deodorants offers more opportunity
- **Washing machines vs. TVs penetration:** Washing machines have far lower penetration in India than TVs, suggesting higher growth potential
- **Tea bags vs. coffee bags:** Both relatively small segments vs. loose tea and instant coffee
- **Frost-free refrigerators:** Growing rapidly as incomes rise
- **Shampoo sachets:** Single-use sachets drove massive growth in shampoo consumption among lower-income consumers in rural India

[Page 179]

#### 2. Are its success factors close to our strengths?

**The logic:** Every market has specific success factors. If those factors don't match the extending brand's strengths, the extension will underperform.

**Examples:**
- **Tata / Bread / Britannia:** Britannia's strengths are in manufacturing, distribution of packaged baked goods, and consumer understanding of the biscuit/bakery space. Success factors in the bread market — freshness, local distribution, low price — are very close to Britannia's strengths. This is why Britannia successfully entered the bread market
- **Masalas market:** The masalas (spice mixes) market in India requires deep regional flavor expertise, fragmented distribution, and intense price competition. A large corporate brand without these specific capabilities would struggle

[Page 181]

#### 3. Are the brand assets transferable?

**The logic:** Even if the brand is well-known, its specific associations may not be relevant or credible in the new category.

**Failures:**
- **Lux to shampoos:** Lux's beauty soap image tried to extend to shampoos. But soap and shampoo have different product attributes, and consumers did not transfer the Lux "beauty soap of the stars" imagery to hair care
- **Ponds toothpaste:** Ponds' skincare/cold cream heritage did not transfer to dental care
- **Ponds premium soaps/shampoos:** Multiple Ponds extensions into adjacent categories performed below expectations because the brand's cold cream/face care associations did not transfer effectively
- **Fastrack youth watch brand to helmets:** Fastrack is positioned as a "rash, cool, rebellious" youth accessories brand. Helmets are about safety. The brand's associations around recklessness actively conflicted with the safety message required for helmets. Result: failure. The brand assets were not only non-transferable — they were counterproductive

[Page 183]

#### 4. Are the brand assets still assets in this market?

**Three types of change that can make existing brand assets obsolete:**

1. **Skills domain transfer:** The brand's expertise may be in an area that no longer commands respect or premium in the new market. Example: a newspaper brand extending to online news — the newspaper's credibility in print journalism may not transfer to the fast-moving digital space where consumers expect different qualities
2. **Past fame relevance:** A brand that was once famous may find its fame is irrelevant in a new context. Example: Dunlop tyre brand in India — historically a famous brand but declining against modern competitors
3. **Technology obsolescence:** A brand built on a technology that has been superseded. Example: Gestetner was the leading brand in cyclostyling (a type of duplicating technology). When photocopying replaced cyclostyling, Gestetner's entire brand equity — built on cyclostyling expertise — became an asset in a market that no longer existed

**Amazon offline:** Amazon's brand assets (convenience, vast selection, user reviews, fast delivery) were built for online. Whether those assets transfer fully to physical retail remains uncertain — Amazon Go and Amazon Fresh are experiments testing this question.

[Page 185]

#### 5. Will it impact positively on brand equity?

**The logic:** Some categories are so inconsistent with a brand's values that entry would damage the parent brand, even if commercially successful in the short term.

**Indian examples:**
- **Tatas not entering liquor:** The Tata Group is associated with trust, reliability, ethics, and Indian national pride. Entering the alcohol business — even if profitable — would conflict with these brand values and the Tata family's public commitment to social responsibility
- **Mahindras not entering cigarettes:** Similar logic. Mahindra is a respected industrial and consumer brand. Cigarette manufacturing would be inconsistent with their brand values

[Page 186]

#### 6. How entrenched are competitors?

**The logic:** Even with good brand assets and a growing market, a new entrant faces the challenge of dislodging entrenched competitors.

**Examples:**
- **Maggie in pickles failed:** Nestlé's Maggie brand tried to extend into pickles/achar (a large, culturally important category in India). But the category was dominated by hundreds of regional players with strong local ties. Maggie withdrew
- **Lipton in multiple categories withdrew:** HUL's Lipton brand tried to extend into various food categories but found competition too intense
- **24 life insurance competitors in India:** When private players entered the Indian life insurance market after deregulation, there were suddenly 24 competitors. HDFC and Max Life later pursued merger to gain scale
- **LIC entrenched incumbent:** Life Insurance Corporation of India has enormous brand recognition (government-backed, trusted, omnipresent). Private insurers face an uphill battle against LIC's entrenchment

[Page 187]

#### 7. How fast can they copy?

**The logic:** If competitors can quickly replicate the extension, first-mover advantage disappears.

**The Vim dish wash example (a detailed case):**

Success and failure analysis of Vim's extension from dish bars to liquid dish wash:
- Pril (Jyothy Henkel) launched liquid dish wash in 2003
- Vim recognized the category was growing and launched its own liquid dish wash
- Vim then withdrew from the market (strategic retreat)
- Vim relaunched in 2006 with a superior formulation
- **Lesson:** The pioneer must be perpetually ready for substitutes. The fact that a competitor can copy quickly does not mean you should not enter — but you must continuously innovate to maintain advantage

**Moderate success: HUL Fair and Lovely → Fair and Lovely Max Fairness for men:**
- Fair and Lovely (for women) extended to target men under the "Max Fairness" name
- But Emami's "Fair and Handsome" achieved 57.5% market share by being first in the men's fairness category and positioning more specifically for men

[Page 188]

**Failure: Godrej No. 1 soap → Godrej Fair Glow fairness cream:**
- Godrej tried to leverage its soap brand into fairness cream
- Fair & Lovely (HUL) had 60% market share in fairness creams and was deeply entrenched
- Godrej Fair Glow could not overcome Fair & Lovely's position

#### 8. Does the product have clear differentiation?

**Success: Marico's Saffola cooking oil → Saffola Oats:**
- Saffola's positioning around heart health transferred naturally to oats (also heart healthy)
- Communication: "Majeedar Khao Jeebharke" (eat what you love, wholeheartedly) — made oats emotionally appealing, not just functionally healthy
- Saffola Oats became a Rs 100 crore brand (approximately INR 1 billion) — a significant success

[Page 189]

#### 9. Is it a motivating difference?

**Success: Colgate → Colgate Cibaca Vedshakti vs. Patanjali Dantkanti:**
- Colgate's Vedshakti positioned around traditional herbal/Ayurvedic ingredients as a motivating difference in a market where many consumers prefer natural products
- Competed directly with Patanjali Dantkanti, which had disrupted the category with its Ayurvedic positioning

**Moderate success: ITC Sunfeast biscuits → Sunfeast Yippee noodles (2010):**
- ITC's Sunfeast brand launched Yippee noodles in 2010, 7 years after Sunfeast biscuits were established
- Key differentiation: round noodle cake (vs. square Maggi) and non-sticky texture
- Market share: 0.7% in 2010 → 28.5% by December 2015 — dramatic success
- Critical context: the Maggi ban in India (2015) due to lead contamination created a massive market share opportunity; Yippee stepped into the void

[Page 190]

#### 10. Can it produce at a normal cost?

**Success: Tata from lorries → LCVs → cars:**
- Tata's manufacturing expertise and supply chain in commercial vehicles transferred to light commercial vehicles and eventually passenger cars (Indica, Nano, Nexon)
- International acquisitions: Jaguar Land Rover acquired from Ford for $2.3 billion (2008) — moved Tata into ultra-premium automotive

**Failure: BPL from TVs → appliances / power / telecom:**
- BPL was a leading consumer electronics brand in India
- Tried to extend into appliances, power equipment, and telecom
- Competition from Korean giants Samsung and LG, with superior manufacturing scale and quality, made it impossible for BPL to match quality at competitive cost
- BPL gradually retreated from most categories

[Page 191]

#### 11. Will distribution accept it?

**Success: Himalayan mineral water — Pepsi distribution tie-up:**
- Himalayan Water leveraged PepsiCo's distribution network to achieve nationwide reach rapidly
- Starbucks distribution in US and UK through licensed stores and supermarket channels

**Failure: Britannia Biscuits → other food products:**
- Britannia attempted to extend into bakery and dairy beyond biscuits
- Daily Bread bakery venture was eventually closed
- Milkman dairy range suffered significant losses
- Britannia's biscuit distribution network (dry goods, ambient temperature) was not well-suited to fresh/chilled dairy products

[Page 192]

#### 12. Is it consistent with company and brand identity?

**Success: Park Avenue clothing → men's grooming (deodorant):**
- Park Avenue's "premium, professional man" positioning transferred perfectly to men's grooming
- Park Avenue deodorant reportedly beat Axe in some markets — significant achievement

**Failure: Coke → Coke Vanilla:**
- Introduced in India with a nostalgia campaign ("Wakaw" campaign referencing older, more innocent times)
- The nostalgia positioning did not resonate with Indian consumers; failed to connect emotionally
- Withdrawn from the Indian market

#### 13. Advertising?

**Success: Dove cream soap → Dove Shampoo:**
- Dove's "be yourself" platform — celebrating real beauty, natural femininity — transferred powerfully from soap to shampoo
- 1993 launch at double the price of competitors (premium positioning)
- Became a Rs 1500 crore brand (approximately INR 15 billion) — enormous success
- The "Real Beauty" campaign later extended across all Dove categories globally

[Page 193]

**Pricing/promotion success: Tata steel premiumization:**
- Tata Steel created premium sub-brands for different segments:
  - Steelium (cold rolled steel, premium)
  - Shaktee (corrugated sheets)
  - Tiscon (construction/TMT bars)
- Steelium commanded a 25% premium for cold rolled steel
- These brand architectures allowed Tata to move from commodity to branded steel, capturing higher margins

#### 14. Does it meet profitability objective?

**T-Series (music to multiple categories):**
- T-Series built its empire in music cassettes and Bollywood film music
- Failed in consumer products and electronics
- Succeeded in:
  - Devotional music (religious content — massive segment)
  - Non-film music
  - Going digital (embraced YouTube early, now the most-subscribed YouTube channel in the world)
  - Film making and music rights ownership
- T-Series owns approximately one-third of all Hindi film music rights

[Page 194]

#### 15. Can the company sustain competition?

**Failure: Birla from rayon into tyre (Kesoram Industries):**
- Birla extended into tyre manufacturing
- Kesoram Industries accumulated Rs 4,500 crore in debt by September 2014
- Facing competition from MRF, Apollo, JK (domestic players) plus Michelin, Bridgestone, Goodyear (global players)
- The tyre industry requires enormous capital investment in R&D, manufacturing, and distribution — barriers that newcomers struggle to overcome

Sources include Indian business publications (Business Standard, Economic Times, Mint), Nielsen India reports, and academic sources on brand extension theory.

## Article: "The Brand Relationship Spectrum: The Key to the Brand Architecture Challenge"
**Authors:** David A. Aaker and Erich Joachimsthaler
**Publication:** California Management Review, Vol. 42, No. 4, Summer 2000
**Adapted from:** Brand Leadership (The Free Press, 2000)
**PDF Pages:** ~196–220

[Page 196]

### Introduction: The Brand Architecture Challenge

**Brand architecture** is the organizing structure of the brand portfolio that specifies the brand roles and the nature of relationships between brands and between the brand and the products/services of the firm.

The **brand relationship spectrum** is a key tool for managing this challenge. It provides a systematic way to think about the relationships between master brands, sub-brands, endorsed brands, and co-brands.

[Page 197]

### Figure 1: Brand Relationship Spectrum

The spectrum runs from left (House of Brands) to right (Branded House). Full description:

**Left side: House of Brands**
- Different brands for different products
- Master brand plays no role
- Sub-categories:
  - **Not Connected:** RCA/GE, Saturn/GM, NutraSweet/G.D. Searle — the master brand has no visible presence
  - **Shadow Endorser:** Tide/P&G, Lexus/Toyota, Touchstone/Disney — master brand is known but not prominently displayed

**Middle-left: Endorsed Brands**
- Master brand endorses a sub-brand
- Sub-categories:
  - **Token Endorsement:** Grape Nuts "from Post", Universal Pictures "A Sony Company", Dockers "LS & Co." — small logo/text link
  - **Linked Name:** DKNY (Donna Karan New York), McMuffin, Nestea — name signals connection to master brand
  - **Strong Endorsement:** Courtyard by Marriott, Obsession by Calvin Klein, Friends & Family by MCI — strong explicit endorsement

**Middle-right: Subbrands**
- Master brand is modified by a sub-brand
- Sub-categories:
  - **Co-Drivers:** Gillette Mach3, Sony Trinitron, DuPont Stainmaster — both brand and sub-brand drive purchase
  - **Master Brand as Driver:** Buick LeSabre, HP DeskJet, Dell Dimension — master brand drives, sub-brand descriptive

**Right side: Branded House**
- Single master brand across all products
- Sub-categories:
  - **Different Identity:** GE Capital vs. GE Appliances, Club Med Singles vs. Club Med Couples, Levi-Europe vs. Levi-US
  - **Same Identity:** BMW, Healthy Choice, Virgin — one consistent brand across all products

[Page 198]

### The Driver Role Concept

The **driver role** refers to the degree to which a brand drives the purchase decision and the use experience. The spectrum is fundamentally a spectrum of driver roles — from "house of brands" (where sub-brands are the sole drivers) to "branded house" (where the master brand is the sole driver).

### A House of Brands

**Rationale for House of Brands:**

**1. Incompatible associations — need for separation:**
- **Budweiser Cola** would never work. The Budweiser beer associations are incompatible with cola
- **VW/Porsche/Audi:** Volkswagen keeps these separate because each commands a different image; mixing them would dilute each brand's specific positioning
- The desire for a unique brand image creates the need for separation

[Page 199]

**2. Signaling a breakthrough:**
When a product represents a significant innovation or departure from the existing brand's associations, a new brand name signals clearly that this is something truly different. The existing brand might create false expectations.

**3. Owning product class association:**
- **Gleem toothpaste** — Procter & Gamble created a separate brand to own the "whitening" association in toothpaste
- **Reach toothbrush** — Johnson & Johnson created Reach as a standalone brand to own specific orthodontic design positioning
- A brand too closely tied to the master brand cannot fully "own" the category association

**4. Avoiding channel conflict:**
- **L'Oréal:** Sells Lancôme in department stores, L'Oréal Paris in mass market, and Maybelline in drugstores. Using the same brand name across channels would cannibalize premium sales and upset trade partners
- **VF Corporation:** Lee, Wrangler, Maverick, Old Axe — all denim brands sold in different channels at different price points
- **Purina ProPlan** (specialty pet stores) vs. **Purina** (grocery) — avoids channel conflict

[Page 200]

### Shadow Endorser

The shadow endorser's role is to provide credibility and reassurance at the corporate level without diluting the individual brand. The shadow endorser is known but not prominently featured.

**Examples:**
- **Lettuce Entertain You Enterprises** has 39 distinct restaurant concepts, each with its own name and identity. The holding company provides quality credibility that knowledgeable consumers recognize
- **Lexus / Toyota:** Toyota's name is known to Lexus buyers but deliberately kept in background
- **DeWalt / Black & Decker:** The relationship is known in the trade but not prominently advertised to consumers
- **Banana Republic / Old Navy / GAP:** Gap Inc. is the shadow endorser for all three
- **Saturn / GM:** Saturn was created as a separate company within GM, specifically to distance it from GM's quality problems. The GM shadow endorser provided corporate reassurance
- **Dockers / Levi-Strauss:** Levi's credibility provides reassurance for Dockers khakis
- **Mountain Dew / Pepsi:** Pepsi's distribution and corporate resources support Mountain Dew without overshadowing it
- **Touchstone Pictures / Disney:** Disney was concerned that its "family entertainment" brand was too restrictive for adult films — Touchstone allowed Disney to produce adult movies without damaging the family brand

[Page 202]

### Endorsed Brands

In an endorsed brand strategy, the sub-brand carries most of the marketing weight but the endorsement of a recognized master brand adds credibility.

**The endorsement continuum:**

**Token Endorsement examples:**
- **GE light bulb logo** appearing on products — the GE institutional quality signal
- **Betty Crocker spoon** appearing on products — quality and reliability signal
- **"A Sony Company"** on Columbia Pictures — credibility transfer

Conditions for effective token endorsement:
1. The endorser must be well known
2. The endorser must be consistently presented
3. The endorser works best as a visual metaphor or symbol
4. The endorser should appear on a well-regarded product family

[Page 204]

**Linked Name examples:**
- **McDonald's:** Every product in the McDonald's system uses Mc- or Mac- prefix: Egg McMuffin, Big Mac, McRib, McPizza, McKids Wear, Chicken McNuggets, McApple
- **HP OfficeJet / LaserJet:** The Jet suffix signals a family connection while each product has its own identity

**Strong Endorsement examples:**
- **Courtyard by Marriott:** The Marriott endorsement is central to Courtyard's positioning — it says "affordable but Marriott quality"
- The UK confectionery study found that Cadbury, Mars, Nestlé, Terry's, and Walls all relied heavily on corporate endorsement to reassure consumers in their chocolate and candy purchases

**Marriott's organizational vs. product brand distinction:**
Marriott uses its name for both corporate identity and product endorsement. The name carries different weight in each context — as a product brand (Marriott Hotels) it signals luxury; as an endorser (Courtyard by Marriott) it signals quality assurance at a more accessible level.

[Page 205]

**Nestlé and Kit-Kat in the UK:**
Nestlé acquired Kit-Kat (a Rowntree brand) in the UK. The challenge: should Nestlé stamp its name on a beloved British brand? The decision was to maintain the Kit-Kat identity with minimal Nestlé reference — recognizing that Kit-Kat's heritage was more valuable than any Nestlé endorsement could provide.

### Subbrands

In a subbrand strategy, the master brand is the primary identity but a sub-brand modifier specifies a particular aspect of the offering.

**Examples:**
- **Black & Decker Sweet Hearts Wafflebaker** — Black & Decker is the driver; Sweet Hearts specifies the product
- **Microsoft Office** — Microsoft drives; Office specifies the productivity suite
- **Sony Walkman** — Sony drives; Walkman defines the portable music category
- **Audi TT** — Audi drives; TT specifies the sports car model
- **Nike Force** — Nike drives; Force specifies the basketball line
- **Ocean Spray Craisins** — Ocean Spray drives; Craisins names the dried cranberry product
- **Revolutionary Lipcolor from Revlon** — Revlon drives; Revolutionary Lipcolor describes the product innovation

[Page 207]

### The Subbrand as Co-Driver

When both the master brand and the sub-brand drive the purchase decision equally, they are **co-drivers**.

**Gillette Mach3:**
Both Gillette (the shaving excellence master brand) and Mach3 (the specific triple-blade technology innovation) drive the purchase. Men buy a Gillette because of the brand's shaving reputation AND they buy Mach3 specifically for its triple-blade performance.

**Virgin Vie:**
Virgin created the Vie sub-brand to give its beauty retail concept enough separation from Richard Branson and the Virgin airlines/music associations, while still benefiting from Virgin's innovation and challenger spirit.

**Marriott / Courtyard co-driver risk discussion:**
If Courtyard becomes too strong as a brand in its own right, it may want to separate from Marriott — as was the case with Marriott worrying that Courtyard was drifting toward a co-driver relationship where Marriott endorsement was less important.

[Page 208]

### A Branded House

In a branded house, a single master brand is used across all products. The brand is the promise, the product is merely the expression.

**Examples:**
- **Virgin:** Virgin Airlines, Virgin Express, Virgin Radio, Virgin Rail, Virgin Cola, Virgin Jeans, Virgin Music — all united under the Virgin brand promise: challenger spirit, fun, irreverence, value for money, quality
- **Healthy Choice (ConAgra):** Single brand promise of healthy, convenient food across dozens of product categories
- **Honda:** Cars, motorcycles, marine engines, generators, lawnmowers — all united under Honda's small-engine excellence and reliability
- **Sony:** Electronics, entertainment, music, gaming — all united under Sony's innovation and premium quality
- **Adidas:** Performance sports equipment, footwear, apparel — united brand promise
- **Disney:** Theme parks, movies, merchandise, television — unified "fun family entertainment" promise

[Page 209]

**GE synergy examples:**
- GE's small appliances (toasters, irons) benefited from being associated with GE's larger appliance line (refrigerators, washing machines). The brand signaled quality and reliability across all categories
- **GE Plastics ad spillover:** GE's advertising for its industrial plastics business actually increased perceptions of GE's consumer products because consumers saw GE as a technology innovator
- **Leverage concept:** In a branded house, every marketing dollar spent on one product benefits all other products under the same brand

### Same Brands but with Different Identities

Sometimes a single brand name must serve very different market segments:
- **GE Capital vs. GE Appliances:** Both are GE, but GE Capital needs to be seen as a sophisticated financial institution while GE Appliances needs to be seen as a consumer-friendly appliance maker. These require quite different marketing approaches, visual identities, and communication styles
- **Club Med Singles vs. Club Med Couples:** Both are Club Med holiday resorts, but the singles and couples experiences are very different — separate product identities within the same brand name

[Page 210]

### Figure 2: Selecting the Brand Separation — Framework

A two-column framework for deciding where on the spectrum to position:

**Left column — Toward a Branded House:**
- Does master brand contribute by adding value proposition?
- Does master brand add credibility?
- Does master brand add visibility?
- Will the master brand deliver communication efficiencies?
- Will the master brand be strengthened by association with the new offering?

**Right column — Toward a House of Brands:**
- Is there compelling need to create/own a new association?
- Is there a need to represent a truly new and different offering?
- Is there a need to avoid an unwanted association?
- Is there a need to retain or capture a specific customer/brand bond?
- Is there a need to deal with channel conflict?
- Will the business sustain the investment required to build a new brand name?

[Page 211]

### Selecting the Right Position: Four Key Questions

**Question 1: Does the Master Brand Contribute to the Offering?**

*Associations Enhancing Value Proposition:*
- **Calvin Klein fragrances:** Calvin Klein's associations with chic, sensual New York fashion directly enhance the desirability of CK fragrances. The brand contributes meaningfully to the product offering

*Credibility with Organizational Associations:*
- Quality (HP): HP's engineering heritage credibilizes new technology products
- Innovation (Shiseido): Shiseido's reputation for cosmetic innovation endorses new formulations
- Customer Concern (Nordstrom): Nordstrom's service reputation endorses any Nordstrom-branded product
- Globalness (AT&T): AT&T's global network credibility endorses telecommunications products worldwide
- Reliability and Trust (Sears): Sears' trust heritage endorses its private-label products (DieHard, Kenmore, Craftsman)

[Page 212]

*Visibility:*
- **CitiGroup existing visibility:** Citibank already has enormous visibility — leveraging the Citi name for a new product costs little incrementally
- **Mega Brokers as new entrant challenge:** A new financial services entrant has no visibility and must either spend enormously to build a new brand or leverage an existing name

*Communication Efficiencies:*
- **GE jet engines/appliances spillover:** GE's advertising investment for jet engines increases the perceived innovativeness and quality of GE appliances — a free marketing effect
- Olympic sponsorship across business units: A single Olympic sponsorship by GE benefits all GE business units simultaneously
- Economies of scale in communication occur when a branded house strategy allows many products to benefit from a single brand building investment

[Page 213]

**Question 2: Will the Master Brand Be Strengthened?**

- **Healthy Choice:** Each new healthy food product added to the Healthy Choice line strengthened the brand's position as the leading healthy food brand
- **Sunkist candy/soda risk:** Sunkist considered allowing its name to be used on candy and soda products (licensing). Risk: Sunkist's "fresh oranges / healthy citrus" positioning could be damaged if the brand became associated with high-sugar candy
- **Clorox bleach vs. Hidden Valley Ranch:** When Clorox (cleaning products) acquired Hidden Valley Ranch (food products), they had to decide whether to use the Clorox name or keep Hidden Valley Ranch separate. They chose separation — "The HVR Company" label — because the Clorox bleach associations would be harmful to a food brand
- **Bayer aspirin dilution by non-aspirin products:** Bayer's extension into non-aspirin products (Bayer Select) risked diluting the powerful, singular Bayer = aspirin association

[Page 214]

**Question 3: Is There a Compelling Need for a Separate Brand?**

*Creating and Owning an Association:*
- **Pantene under Head & Shoulders/Pert impossible:** If Pantene had been named "Head & Shoulders Pro-V" or "Pert Vitality," it could not have owned its specific "hair that shines with health" positioning as distinctively
- **GM's 33 brands problem:** GM at its peak had 33 distinct brands in various markets globally — too many to manage effectively, with significant brand overlap and confusion

*Representing a New, Different Offering:*
- **Viper / Taurus / Neon (Chrysler):** Each of these cars had a very distinct personality and target customer. Viper (extreme performance) and Neon (fun, affordable) needed to be separate to own their specific associations

[Page 215]

*Avoiding an Association:*
- **Saturn/GM test results:** Research showed that consumers who didn't know Saturn was made by GM actually rated it higher on quality. The GM association (quality problems of the 1980s) was harmful to Saturn's positioning
- **Micro-brewed beers:** When large brewers created "craft" beers, they often used completely independent brand names (e.g., Blue Moon by Coors) to avoid the association with mass-market beer
- **Clorox/Hidden Valley Ranch:** As above
- **Gap/Banana Republic/Old Navy separation:** When Gap tried "Gap Warehouse" (later renamed Old Navy), the warehouse discount associations threatened the Gap brand itself. Fully separating as Old Navy solved this
- **Nestlé and Alpo/Fancy Feast pet food:** Nestlé deliberately keeps its name off pet food products because the human food associations of Nestlé would be unwanted in pet food

[Page 216]

*Retain/Capture a Customer/Brand Bond:*
- **Schlumberger retaining Anadrill/Dowell/GeoQuest:** When Schlumberger acquired these oilfield services companies, each had a loyal customer base that valued the specific brand. Schlumberger retained the brand names to preserve these customer bonds
- **HP always changing to HP brand:** In contrast, HP has historically moved acquired brands quickly to the HP umbrella — this strategy risks losing the customer bond that made the acquired brand valuable in the first place

*Conditions making brand equity transfer difficult (4 conditions):*
1. Customer bond is to the specific brand name/identity, not the product category
2. The customer relationship is personal and based on long-standing trust
3. The brand has distinctive visual/verbal elements deeply embedded in customer experience
4. The acquired brand competes with existing HP products (channel conflict risk)

[Page 217]

*Avoiding Channel Conflict:*
- **L'Oréal (Lancôme/L'Oréal/Maybelline):** Three distinct brands at three price points in three channel types — department stores, mass market, drugstores. Using one brand would destroy the channel economics and trade relationships
- **VF Corporation (Lee/Wrangler/Maverick/Old Axe):** Different denim brands for different retail channels
- **Purina ProPlan / grocery:** ProPlan is sold in specialty pet stores at premium prices; Purina is in grocery at mass-market prices. Same parent company, different brands, no channel conflict
- **Fragrance and clothing brands:** The same logic applies widely in fashion — Armani has Giorgio Armani (super-premium), Emporio Armani (premium-accessible), Armani Exchange (mass fashion), each in different channels

**Question 4: Will the Business Support a New Brand Name?**
A new brand name requires enormous investment to build awareness and equity. Before choosing a house of brands approach, the business must be large enough and sustained enough to justify building brand equity from scratch.

[Page 218]

### A Closing Note

**GE as example of both strategies:**
GE is largely a branded house — most of its businesses carry the GE name (GE Aviation, GE Healthcare, GE Appliances). But it also owns Hotpoint (appliances) and NBC (media) which operate outside the GE name. This shows that even committed practitioners of the branded house strategy make exceptions when necessary.

In practice, most large companies use a mixture of positions along the spectrum — and the spectrum framework helps managers make those positioning decisions consciously and strategically rather than by accident.
