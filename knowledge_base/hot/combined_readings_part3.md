### Reinforcing Brands

[Page 221]

**The fundamental rule:** Brands can never stand still. Even the strongest brands must be constantly reinforced and refreshed.

**Three key issues in brand reinforcement:**
1. The advantages of maintaining brand consistency
2. The importance of protecting sources of brand equity
3. The trade-offs between fortifying a brand and leveraging it

**What to reinforce:**
- Brand awareness: ensuring the brand is salient at key purchasing and consumption moments
- Brand image: ensuring key associations are still relevant, differentiated, and strong

### Maintaining Brand Consistency

[Page 223]

Two aspects of brand consistency:
1. **Consistency of marketing support** — maintaining investment levels over time
2. **Consistency of brand associations** — ensuring the brand means the same thing over time, even as execution evolves

**Branding Brief 14-1: Patagonia**

**Brand Flashbacks / Retro-Branding / Nostalgia Marketing:**

Nostalgia marketing leverages consumers' emotional connections to the past.

- **Colonel Sanders KFC reappearance:** KFC brought back the Colonel Sanders character (using rotating celebrity impersonators including Norm MacDonald, Rob Lowe, Reba McEntire) as a consistent brand icon
- **Oreo 100-year anniversary (2012):** "Celebrate the kid inside" campaign; Daily Twist 100-day promotion featuring a new Oreo image daily connected to current events; 25 million Facebook likes; 25% sales increase; India-specific: "Oreo Togetherness Bus" campaign reinforcing family connection

**Research on nostalgia advertising:**
Nostalgic advertising tends to generate positive affect and purchase intention, particularly among older consumers. However, it can feel irrelevant or alienating to younger demographics — the challenge is using nostalgia to bridge generations.

- **Pokémon GO nostalgia connection:** The massive success of Pokémon GO in 2016 was partly explained by nostalgia among the 25-35 year old demographic that grew up with Pokémon in the 1990s
- **Kraft Mac & Cheese "You Know You Love It" campaign:** $50 million campaign; Mac & cheese is an iconic childhood comfort food brand; campaign leaned into the guilt-pleasure nostalgia angle

**Branding Brief 14-2: Pabst Blue Ribbon (PBR)**

Pabst Blue Ribbon was born in 1882. It peaked at 18 million barrels in 1977 before declining steadily.

The **PBR revival story:**
- 2001: Young trendsetters in Portland, Oregon began drinking PBR ironically — as a rejection of mainstream beer marketing and corporate advertising
- "Blue-collar Americana" authentic positioning — the brand's lack of advertising became its advertising
- Strategy: word-of-mouth, on-premise promotions at independent bars, sponsorship of grassroots events (roller derby, fixed-gear cycling, bike polo)
- Positioned against the marketing machines of Anheuser-Busch InBev and MillerCoors — the David vs. Goliath positioning
- Craft brewery partnerships

**Key insight:** PBR succeeded by deliberately NOT doing mainstream brand management. The brand's authenticity came from its resistance to marketing.

### Protecting Sources of Brand Equity

[Page 225]

#### Brand Crises

**Toyota 2009:** Faulty accelerator pedals/floor mats led to one of the largest automotive recalls in history — 9 million vehicles worldwide.

#### Recovering from a Brand Crisis

**Two key principles:**

**1. Swiftness:** The longer a crisis festers without a decisive response, the more damage accumulates. Speed signals that the brand takes the issue seriously.

**2. Sincerity:** Consumers can detect inauthentic apologies. The brand must genuinely acknowledge the problem, take responsibility, and demonstrate commitment to fixing it.

**VW diesel emissions scandal response (and failure):**
Volkswagen's initial response was widely criticized for being neither swift nor sincere — executives denied knowledge, the full extent was minimized, and communication was inconsistent.

**Science of Branding 14-1: Understanding Brand Crises — 5 Key Research Findings:**

1. **Consumer attribution matters:** Whether consumers attribute the crisis to intentional wrongdoing vs. accident/negligence significantly affects brand damage
2. **Pre-crisis brand image protects:** Brands with strong, positive images prior to the crisis suffer less long-term damage — a "brand equity buffer"
3. **Counter-argumentation by loyal consumers:** Highly loyal brand consumers tend to counter-argue negative brand information, defending their preferred brand from evidence that should logically harm their evaluation
4. **Spillover to parent brands:** Crises in sub-brands can spill up to damage the parent brand (and vice versa)
5. **Opportunity to strengthen relationships:** Well-managed crisis responses can actually strengthen brand–consumer relationships by demonstrating brand character

**Table 14-1: Recovery Strategies for Brand Crises**

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| 1. Apology and admission | Full acceptance of responsibility, genuine apology | United Airlines/Dr. David Dao incident (April 9, 2017) — dragged passenger off overbooked flight; $250M market value lost before apology; later settled lawsuit |
| 2. Bolstering brand image | Proactively reinforce positive brand attributes | Maggi Noodles India (2015 lead contamination scandal); Chipotle E. coli outbreak — gave away double food portions |
| 3. Not just me | Point to industry-wide issue | Southwest Airlines 2013 flight delays — attributed to weather and high holiday travel loads |
| 4. Downplaying the crisis | Minimize the perceived severity | Samsung Galaxy Note 7 battery fires — initially pointed to third-party battery supplier rather than design flaw |
| 5. Limit damage | Confine the damage to a specific product/segment | Ford Explorer/Firestone tires — Ford explicitly limited recall to Firestone-equipped models |
| 6. Flat-out denial | Deny the facts | Tommy Hilfiger racism accusation — publicly stated "I've never even been on Oprah" (denying the alleged incident took place on Oprah's show) |
| 7. Counterattack | Go on offense | Hewlett-Packard and Xerox filing patent infringement lawsuits against each other simultaneously |

### Fortifying versus Leveraging

[Page 228]

The fundamental tension in brand management: investing in the existing brand (fortifying) vs. stretching it to generate additional revenue (leveraging).

**The risk of overleveraging:**
- Too many extensions dilute the brand's associations
- Licensing revenue in the short term can damage brand value in the long term
- "Milking" a brand depletes its equity without reinvestment

### Fine-Tuning the Supporting Marketing Program

**Product-Related Performance Associations:**
- **Progressive Insurance:** Continuously innovated with technology (Snapshot usage-based insurance) to maintain the "consumer champion" positioning
- **Smith Corona:** Failed to adapt from typewriters to computers; went bankrupt — a cautionary tale of failure to update product performance associations
- **Blockbuster vs. Netflix:** Blockbuster refused to update its product/service model (late fees, physical stores) while Netflix built streaming; Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy 2010

[Page 229]

**Nonproduct-Related Imagery Associations:**
- **MTV staying relevant:** MTV has had to constantly evolve its imagery — from "pure music video channel" to broader entertainment — to stay relevant to each new generation of young consumers
- **Heineken flip-flopping:** Heineken has struggled with consistency in its brand imagery, shifting between premium European heritage and youthful fun, sometimes inconsistently
- **JCPenney repositioning case:**
  - 2013: CEO Ron Johnson (former Apple retail executive) introduced "everyday low pricing" strategy — eliminated coupons and frequent sales
  - Redesigning stores to be like Apple retail: concept shops within the store
  - Also eliminated coupons and sales promotions entirely
  - Result: Sales declined dramatically; loyal customers (who shopped for deals) felt alienated
  - 2014: Reversed strategy; brought back coupons and promotional events
  - **Key lesson:** JCPenney's brand equity was built around promotional value shopping — coupons and deals were not just tactics but were core to the brand identity. Removing them did not "elevate" the brand; it destroyed its reason for being

### Revitalizing Brands

[Page 230]

Brand revitalization ranges from:
- **Back-to-basics** at one end (returning to core associations that have been neglected)
- **Complete reinvention** at the other end (fundamentally redefining what the brand means)

**Branding Brief 14-4: Harley-Davidson Motor Company**

Founded 1903, Milwaukee, Wisconsin.

**Crisis:** In the 1970s, after acquisition by AMF (American Machine and Foundry), the workforce was slashed and quality declined. Japanese motorcycles (Honda, Yamaha, Kawasaki) flooded the market with superior quality at lower prices.

**Turnaround:**
- 1981: Management buyout — executives bought the company back from AMF
- Dramatically improved quality (partnered with Japanese suppliers; implemented lean manufacturing)
- Licensed the brand aggressively (Harley merchandise)
- 1983: Founded HOG (Harley Owners Group) — the definitive brand community
  - Started with 33,000 members
  - Grew to 1M+ members in 1,400+ chapters worldwide
- 1989: MotorClothes division created — revenues grew from $151M to $274M by 2011
- 12% of buyers were women (Harley targeted this growing segment)
- 50% US market share in heavyweight motorcycles
- 40+ international dealerships by 2015

**Febreze (P&G):**

Classic example of repositioning through consumer insight.

Initial positioning: **Problem-solution** — "eliminates odors." Advertised to people with specific odor problems (pet owners, etc.).

**The insight:** When researchers visited people's homes, they discovered that people don't think they have odor problems in their own homes — they become nose-blind to the smells. The problem-solution approach wasn't motivating because most consumers didn't believe they had a problem.

**New positioning:** "Finishing touch / celebration of clean" — Febreze is what you use after you've finished cleaning, as the final step. "It's not about cleaning up a problem; it's about celebrating cleanliness."

**Result:** Revenues exceeded $1 billion. Extended into air fresheners, candles, and laundry detergents. "Odor Odes" campaign; OdorClean technology.

### Expanding Brand Awareness

[Page 234]

**Two levers for expanding brand awareness:**
1. **Increasing the quantity of use** (more at each occasion)
2. **Increasing the frequency of use** (more occasions to use)

**Identifying Additional or New Usage Opportunities:**

- **V8 vegetable juice:** Famous tagline "Wow! I Could Have Had a V8" — positioned as a quick, convenient, regret-avoiding way to get vegetable nutrients. The awareness strategy reminded consumers of a product they had forgotten or undervalued

- **Chobani Greek Yogurt case:**
  - Founder: Hamdi Ulukaya (Turkish immigrant)
  - $1 billion in sales by 2010 (extraordinary growth from launch in 2007)
  - Usage expansion: Chobani Flip (mix-ins format), Chobani Meze (dips), Smoothie Week challenge
  - Digital content marketing: 33,000 engagements from just 22 blog posts; 200% ROI on content
  - Expanded beyond breakfast into snacking, cooking, entertaining

[Page 235]

**Coach case:**
Coach identified and filled "usage voids" — occasions when consumers might carry a bag but weren't using a Coach bag.

- The insight: Handbags were becoming "the shoes of the twenty-first century" — bought in multiples, for different occasions, at different price points
- Poppy line: average price $260 — accessible luxury tier
- Handbags make up 2/3 of Coach's total sales
- **Problem:** Aggressive expansion and discounting strategy damaged brand equity; scaling back flash sales and outlet overexpansion was required; launched new high-end line at $400+ to reclaim premium positioning

**Identifying New and Completely Different Ways to Use the Brand:**

- **Arm & Hammer baking soda:** Originally a baking ingredient. Repositioned as a refrigerator deodorizer (Fridge-Freezer Pack). Also extended to toothpaste, laundry detergent, cat litter deodorizer, cleaning products
- **Clorox bleach:** Multiple benefit positioning — whitening, disinfecting, stain removing
- **Wrigley's chewing gum:** Repositioned around calcium supplement benefits for teeth; oral health benefits
- **Tums antacid:** Repositioned as a calcium supplement, not just for heartburn treatment

### Improving Brand Image

[Page 236]

**Targeting options for brand revitalization:**

Four targeting strategies:
1. **Retaining vulnerable customers** — find customers who are at risk of switching and reinforce loyalty before they defect
2. **Recapturing lost customers** — previous customers who have switched can sometimes be won back with new messaging, improved products, or competitive pricing
3. **Identifying neglected segments** — segments that have been underserved may be loyal if properly addressed
4. **Attracting new customers** — expanding the total market

**Tommy Hilfiger case:**

Tommy Hilfiger peaked at $2 billion in revenue in the 1990s. The brand had become associated with urban hip-hop culture (Phat Farm, FUBU, Sean John, Ecko were the specific competing brands it was grouped with).

**The challenge:** As hip-hop fashion faded as a mainstream style, Tommy Hilfiger's brand equity eroded. Bloomingdale's reduced Tommy Hilfiger from 23 boutiques to 1.

**Revitalization strategy:**
- Cut the urban hip-hop associations
- Returned to classic preppy, nautical, American sportswear styles
- 2008: Macy's exclusive partnership
- Result: Sold in 1,000 retail locations in 65 countries by 2010
- 2010: Sold to Phillips-Van Heusen for $3 billion
- Social responsibility: Runway of Dreams — nonprofit program designing adaptive clothing for children with disabilities
- Innovation: Samsung GearVR virtual reality runway show
- Digital strategy: "De facto showman" approach; Tommy.com immediate purchase capability (see-it/buy-it)

[Page 237]

**Repositioning the Brand:**

- **Brand consistency can be "sticky"** — consumers have existing associations that are hard to change, even with significant marketing investment
- **Heritage brands:** Often seen as trustworthy but potentially boring. The challenge is maintaining the positive (trustworthy, reliable) while adding the energizing (relevant, contemporary)
- **Clorox myStain app:** Digital innovation that allowed Clorox to connect with younger, tech-savvy households — extending the utility of the brand through a stain removal guide app

**Changing Brand Elements:**

- **Federal Express → FedEx:** Name change to reflect how consumers already referred to the brand; also modernized the visual identity (the famous hidden arrow in the FedEx logo created by Lindon Leader)
- **Kentucky Fried Chicken → KFC:** Shortened to reduce emphasis on "fried" (health concerns); also modernized
- **Colonel Sanders:** Logo updated but the Colonel character retained as the brand icon

### Retiring Brands

[Page 240]

Sometimes the right decision is to discontinue or sell a brand rather than invest in revitalization.

**Orphan brand:** A brand that has been abandoned by its parent company but still has some loyal following.

**Examples:**
- **Take 5 (Hershey's salty-sweet chocolate bar):** Launched 1982 → withdrawn 2011 → relaunched 2016 (consumer demand drove return)
- **Toyota Scion discontinued 2016:** Scion was created to attract younger buyers to Toyota. After years of declining sales, Toyota concluded the youth positioning was better served by the Toyota brand itself
- **P&G portfolio rationalization:**
  - Sold beauty brands including CoverGirl, Clairol, and Wella to Coty for $12.5 billion
  - Sold Duracell to Berkshire Hathaway
  - Sold Iams pet food to Mars
- **Fruitopia:** Coca-Cola's Fruitopia became an orphan brand — still sold in Australia and Germany but abandoned in most markets

**Obsoleting Existing Products:**
- **Blackberry:** Stopped manufacturing phones in 2016 after failing to adapt to touch-screen smartphones
- **Kodak:** Failed the transition from film to digital photography; filed for bankruptcy
- **Fuji:** Contrast — Fujifilm successfully navigated the digital transition by diversifying into cosmetics (skin care using photo-chemical technology), medical equipment, and office products

### Brand Focus 14.0: Responding to a Brand Crisis — Tylenol

[Page 242]

**History of Tylenol:**
- McNeil Laboratories developed Tylenol as a liquid alternative to aspirin (originally for children and patients who couldn't take aspirin)
- Johnson & Johnson acquired McNeil Laboratories in 1959
- $50 million in sales by 1974
- 37% of the pain reliever market by 1982
- Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules — dominant product
- 100 million US users
- 8% of J&J's total sales
- $40 million advertising campaign in 1982 with tagline "Trust Tylenol — hospitals do"

**The Tylenol Product Tampering Crisis (October 1982):**
Seven people died in the Chicago area after taking Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules that had been laced with cyanide. Someone had removed capsules from store shelves, contaminated them, and replaced them.

Consumer confidence was shattered. Marketing expert Jerry Della Femina wrote in the New York Times: "You'll not see the name Tylenol in any form within a year."

[Page 243]

**The Tylenol Recovery — Actions taken:**
- Immediate worldwide alert to hospitals, distributors, pharmacies
- 24-hour toll-free consumer telephone number
- $100,000 reward for information leading to the perpetrator
- October 5: Voluntary worldwide withdrawal of 31 million bottles (retail value: $100 million) — this was the costliest product recall in US history at the time
- Stopped all advertising
- Weekly tracking surveys: 1,000 respondents/week throughout the crisis
- $1.5 million in additional marketing research investment in Q4 1982
- Capsule exchange offer: October 12 — consumers could exchange capsules for tablets at no cost
- October 24: Return to TV advertising, featuring spokesperson Dr. Thomas N. Gates; the word "Trust" appeared five times in the ad
- November 11: Press conference with 600 news reporters present — announced return to market
- New triple-sealed tamperproof packaging (created the standard for over-the-counter pharmaceutical packaging still used today)
- 60 million coupons distributed November 28
- 20 million additional coupons

**Outcome:**
- $1 billion brand by 1983
- Recovery to pre-tampering sales levels by February 1983 — just four months after the crisis
- The Tylenol response became the gold standard for brand crisis management

[Page 244]

**Tylenol's Quality Control Crises (2009–2011):**

A second wave of crises — not from external tampering but from internal quality control failures:
- Moldy-smelling bottles reported by consumers
- Wood pallets used in manufacturing contaminated with chemical (2,4,6-tribromoanisole)
- Bacterial contamination of children's Tylenol products
- Metal specks and ingredients failing quality testing
- Private contractors allegedly engaged in stealth recall of Motrin (a related product) — buying products off store shelves without alerting the FDA
- Combined impact: $1 billion in sales losses

**Management response:**
- CEO William Weldon stepped down April 2012
- By 2013, Tylenol was valued at approximately $200 million — significantly below its peak
- Tylenol fell to #4 analgesic tablet brand, behind private labels, Advil, and eventually Aleve (which had repositioned around "just 2 tablets last 24 hours" — a genuinely better consumer benefit)

**Key lesson from Tylenol:** The 1982 response was textbook-perfect; the 2009-2011 response failed on both swiftness and sincerity, demonstrating that brand equity is not a permanent insurance policy — it must be earned anew with each crisis.

### Global Branding

[Page 265]

Three organizing questions for global branding:
1. Why should a brand focus on global markets?
2. What is the definition of a global brand?
3. What are the pros and cons of standardizing vs. customizing?

#### Why Should a Brand Focus on Global Markets?

**Five reasons:**
1. Perception of slow domestic growth and increased domestic competition
2. Belief in enhanced overseas growth and profit opportunities
3. Reduce costs from economies of scale in production, R&D, and marketing
4. Diversify risk (economic downturns don't always affect all markets simultaneously)
5. Recognition of the global mobility of customers (business travelers, tourists, and immigrants expect consistent brand experience)

#### Advantages of Global Marketing

**Figure 15-1: Advantages of Global Marketing Programs** (box listing 6 items):
1. Economies of scale in production and distribution
2. Lower marketing costs
3. Power and scope (global brand image signals credibility)
4. Consistency in brand image
5. Ability to leverage good ideas quickly and efficiently
6. Uniformity of marketing practices

[Page 266]

**Elaboration on advantages:**

**Economies of Scale:** A single global product formulation eliminates duplication of R&D, manufacturing setup, and packaging costs. Single global advertising campaigns eliminate the need for separate creative development in each market.

**Lower Marketing Costs:** Global campaigns can be developed once and adapted for local markets at lower incremental cost than developing from scratch in each market.

**Power and Scope:** Consumers often associate global brands with higher quality, more prestige, and greater trustworthiness. A brand's global presence is itself a signal of credibility. Research shows consumers in developing countries often rate global brands more highly than local ones (except for "local iconness").

**Consistency:** American Express's consistent global positioning as "the card for the discerning international traveler" allows it to command a premium worldwide.

#### Disadvantages of Global Marketing

**Figure 15-2: Disadvantages of Global Marketing Programs** (7 items):
1. Differences in consumer needs, wants, and usage patterns across countries
2. Differences in consumer response to branding elements across countries
3. Differences in consumer response to marketing mix elements (pricing, promotion, product features)
4. Differences in the brand and product development environment across countries
5. Differences in the legal environment (trademark law, advertising restrictions, labeling requirements)
6. Differences in marketing institutions across countries (retailer structure, media landscape)
7. Differences in administrative procedures across countries

[Page 267]

**Krispy Kreme UK market entry case:**
Krispy Kreme positioned in the US as a mass-market donut brand (quick breakfast, drive-through). In the UK, positioning shifted to emphasize the communal sharing experience rather than breakfast food — aligning with British tea-and-biscuit culture where sweet items are shared in social settings.

**Science of Branding 15-1: Key Insights Regarding Global Brand Strategies Based on Research Findings:**

Four research-based insights:
1. Cultures vary significantly in their emphasis on the self vs. the group — brands must adapt their individualism/collectivism framing
2. Vertical and horizontal cultural contexts affect how consumers relate to high-status brands — in vertical cultures (where hierarchy is accepted), status brands are more powerful
3. Brands can become symbolic of entire cultures — the American flag is a brand element that brands use (and misuse)
4. Brands can have different personalities in different countries even when the product is identical

**Tide in India case:**
Tide had to fundamentally rethink its product and marketing for India:
- Context: Only 10% of Indian households had washing machines at time of launch
- Target: Middle-class hand-washers (a very different usage occasion from US machine-washing)
- Adaptations: Added Jasmine & Rose scent variants (local fragrance preferences); Tide Naturals sub-brand at a lower price point

### Ten Commandments of Global Branding

[Page 273]

**Figure 15-3: The Commandments of Global Branding** — a list of 10 commandments:

**Commandment 1: Understand Similarities and Differences in the Global Branding Landscape**

Firms must map both what is universal (the human needs that a brand serves) and what is locally specific (how those needs are expressed, what symbols resonate, what cultural references are relevant).

**Commandment 2: Do Not Take Shortcuts in Brand Building**

Global brands are still built the same way as local brands — through consistent, high-quality marketing over time. The temptation to cut corners in emerging markets ("they don't know better") is dangerous.

**Commandment 3: Establish Marketing Infrastructure**

- **McDonald's 90% local raw materials:** McDonald's committed to sourcing approximately 90% of its ingredients locally in each country — creating ties to local agriculture and ensuring freshness
- **Häagen-Dazs supplied free freezers in Europe:** When entering European markets, Häagen-Dazs supplied retailers with branded freezer units at no cost — establishing prime shelf/freezer presence

**Commandment 4: Embrace Integrated Marketing Communications**
- **Ben & Jerry's UK consumer flavor contest:** Using consumer engagement to create local relevance while building global brand community
- **Bilingual advertising for Hispanic-Americans:** Reaching bicultural consumers requires communications that respect both cultural identities

[Page 274]

**Branding Brief 15-3: Marketing to Bicultural Consumers Using Bilingual Advertising**

*Maria and Miguel case study:*
Maria and Miguel are fictional representative US Hispanic consumers. They are bilingual — comfortable in both English and Spanish — and bicultural.

Key research findings:
- **McDonald's and Coca-Cola** score high on what researchers call "cultural symbolism" — they represent American culture broadly, including to Hispanic consumers. These brands can use either English or Spanish advertising without seeming inauthentic
- **Coors and GAP** score lower on cultural symbolism — they are seen as more "neutral" brands
- **Research finding:** Bilingual ads work better for culturally neutral brands (like GAP) than for culturally loaded brands (like McDonald's). For culturally loaded brands, the language choice sends a signal about which cultural identity is being addressed — which can be either appealing or alienating

**Commandment 5: Cultivate Brand Partnerships**

Three entry modes for global expansion:
1. **Geographic extension:** Using the existing brand to enter new geographic markets
2. **Acquire existing brands:** Buying local brands with existing equity in a market
3. **Brand alliance/joint venture/licensing:** Partnering with local brands or companies

Examples:
- **Heineken:** Uses a sequential strategy — first enters a market through importing premium beer; if successful, licenses local production; if very successful, acquires a local brewery
- **Tata Starbucks Ltd.:** 50:50 joint venture between Tata Global Beverages and Starbucks Corporation in India. The Tata relationship gave Starbucks credibility, real estate access, and regulatory navigation in India
- **Under Armour replacing Puma at Tottenham Hotspur:** Under Armour agreed to become the kit sponsor for Tottenham Hotspur F.C., replacing Puma — using sports sponsorship for global brand building

[Page 275]

**Commandment 6: Balance Standardization and Customization**

**Factors favoring standardization:**
1. Common customer needs across markets
2. Global customers and channels (multinational companies, global retail chains)
3. Favorable trade policies reducing barriers
4. Compatible technical standards across markets
5. Transferable marketing skills (similar consumer behavior patterns)

**Branding Brief 15-4: Managing Global Nestlé Brands**

Nestlé's brand portfolio architecture:
- **6 strategic corporate brands:** Nestlé, Nescafé, Nestea, Maggi, Buitoni, Purina — these appear across essentially all markets where Nestlé operates
- **70 strategic international brands:** Brands with presence in multiple countries but not necessarily all
- **83 strategic regional brands:** Brands with strong regional presence (Asia, Europe, Latin America, etc.)
- **Local brands:** Hundreds of local brands specific to single countries

**Nestlé Bear brand condensed milk in Asia:** A classic Asian brand with enormous heritage that would be unknown in Europe. Nestlé manages it as a local brand while providing corporate support.

**1997 management shift:** Nestlé shifted from highly decentralized management (each country/region making independent brand decisions) to more centralized management of strategic brands — a recognition that the corporate brands needed consistent global management

**6 product categories likely to sell through standardized global programs:**
1. Pet food
2. Baby food
3. Confectionery
4. Coffee
5. Mineral water
6. Dairy products in some forms

[Page 276]

**Commandment 7: Balance Global and Local Control**

**Three organizational approaches:**
1. **Centralization:** Global brand management team at HQ makes all brand decisions
2. **Decentralization:** Local country managers make all brand decisions
3. **Combination/matrix:** Global guidelines + local execution authority

**Examples:**
- **Kimberly-Clark global marketing university:** Training program that builds consistent brand management capability across all local teams worldwide
- **Lexus unified global approach for NX and RC models:** Lexus decided that the NX (compact SUV) and RC (coupe) would have a unified global launch — same product positioning, same advertising, same visual identity worldwide
- **T-shaped country organization at GE/Intel/AstraZeneca:** Country managers who are both deep in local market knowledge (vertical bar of T) and broad in global brand/business understanding (horizontal bar of T)
- **Levi Strauss "thermometer" model:**
  - Fixed elements (top of thermometer — NEVER changed locally): brand soul, logos, core product specifications
  - Fluid elements (lower part of thermometer — CAN be adapted locally): quality standards, pricing strategy, advertising execution
  - The "thermometer" visualization shows that some elements are rigid and others can be flexible

**Commandment 8: Establish Operable Guidelines**

- **Disney "fun family entertainment" mantra:** All global product, licensing, and marketing decisions must pass through this filter
- **Disney's three product license categories:**
  1. "A" category: products fully consistent with family entertainment (toys, games, videos)
  2. "B" category: products acceptable but requiring review
  3. "C" category: products incompatible with the Disney brand (alcohol, tobacco, adult content) — not licensable

[Page 277]

**Commandment 9: Implement a Global Brand Equity Measurement System**

Key tools:
- **Global brand equity charter:** A document that defines the brand's values, positioning, and standards globally
- **Brand tracking across markets:** Regular (typically quarterly or semi-annual) research measuring brand awareness, associations, attitudes, and purchase behavior in each major market

**Levi Strauss global brand management:**
- Publishes Brand Value Propositions annually
- Defines global positioning and character of the brand
- Conducts ongoing consumer surveys in all major markets
- The global tracking data allows Levi Strauss to identify markets where brand health is declining before the financial results show the problem

**Commandment 10: Leverage Brand Elements**

- **Nonverbal brand elements transfer more easily** across language/cultural barriers than verbal elements. The Nike swoosh is equally understood in Beijing and New York. The Coca-Cola red can needs no words
- **Websites as sources of global brand equity:** A well-designed global website can express brand identity consistently while allowing local content customization

# SECTION 4: Journal Article — Retail Brand Orientation

## "Retail brand orientation, positional advantage and organisational performance"
**Authors:** Kerrie Bridson, Jody Evans, Felix Mavondo, Joanna Minkiewicz
**Publication:** The International Review of Retail, Distribution and Consumer Research, Vol. 23, No. 3, pp. 245–264, 2013
**DOI:** 10.1080/09593969.2013.776991
**Published online:** 11 April 2013
**PDF Pages:** ~285–320

[Page 285]

### Abstract

This study adopts a resource-based view of branding referred to as brand orientation. Despite the importance of branding in retail, relatively little empirical research has been conducted to understand the degree to which retailers can be considered brand oriented. The purpose of the present research is to establish a conceptualisation of brand orientation that is applicable in a retail context across countries.

The study seeks to empirically validate a model of the **retail brand orientation–positional advantage–organisational performance** relationship and to contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of the factors driving retailer performance.

**Method:** Mail survey collecting data from retail firms in Australia, USA, and UK.

**Unit of analysis:** The retail firm.

**Method:** Confirmatory factor analysis and structural equation modelling.

**Key findings:**
- Four elements of retail brand orientation — **functionality, distinctiveness, augmentation, and symbolism** — play different roles in relation to certain aspects of positional advantage
- Developing strength in all four areas is important
- A position of superiority in only one aspect is insufficient to assure both financial and strategic returns

**Keywords:** retail brand orientation; positional advantage; performance

---

### Introduction

[Page 286]

Within retailing, the brand concept has traditionally been applied in three ways:
1. **Branding of retailers' products** (Burt and Davies 2010)
2. **Corporate branding** (Burt and Sparks 2002; Burghausen and Fan 2002)
3. **Branding the retail store itself** (Burt and Carralero-Encinas 2000)

**The importance of branding in retail:**
Ailawadi and Keller (2004, p. 331): "Given its highly competitive nature, branding can be especially important in the retailing industry to influence customer perceptions and drive store choice and loyalty."

**The Resource-Based View (RBV):**
The premise of the RBV is that organizational capabilities are sources of positional advantage and lead to superior performance (Barney 1991; Day and Wensley 1988).

Brand orientation is viewed as a **capability** — when fully exploited, it provides positional advantage.

**Gap in existing research:**
The dominant conceptualisation of brand orientation has not yet emerged. Existing research comes from:
- Hankinson (2001a, 2001b, 2002) — charity brand orientation
- Ewing and Napoli (2005) — political brand orientation
- Baumgarth (2010) — B2B brand orientation
- Bridson and Evans (2004) — fashion retail brand orientation

This paper extends existing work by developing and validating a **Retail Brand Orientation (RBO)** construct applicable across retail sectors and multiple countries.

---

### Conceptual Development

[Page 287]

#### Brand Orientation

Most business orientations lack common conceptualisation. The fundamental tension: philosophical approach vs. behavioral approach.

- **Philosophical approach:** Brand orientation is embedded in the organization's thinking; reflected in values and beliefs; orientation as a mindset
- **Behavioral approach:** Brand orientation is reflected in implemented behaviors and activities; orientation as practice

**Table 1: Brand orientation definitions and conceptualisations**

| Study | Context | Perspective/Definition | Model Conceptualisation |
|-------|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Urde (1994, 1999) | Manufactured goods | Behavioural perspective: processes of the organisation revolve around brand creation, development, and protection of brand identity; ongoing interaction with target customers to achieve lasting competitive advantages | Behavioural conceptualisation as a seven-dimension construct: target group, corporate identity, corporate name, product, trademark, brand vision and positioning |
| Hankinson (2001a, 2001b) | Charity | Behavioural perspective: involves an understanding of what the brand stands for, building and managing audiences, using the brand as a strategic resource and managing the brand actively and deliberately. Also Philosophical/Behavioural perspective | Behavioural conceptualisation as a four-dimension construct (distinctive, functional, value adding, symbolic) |
| Bridson and Evans (2004) | Fashion retail | Philosophical/Behavioural perspective: degree to which the organisation values brands and its practices are oriented towards building brand capabilities | Behavioural conceptualisation as a four-dimension construct: distinctive, functional, value adding, symbolic |
| Ewing and Napoli (2005) | Non-profit | Behavioural perspective: organisational wide process of generating and sustaining a shared sense of brand meaning that provides superior value to stakeholders | Behavioural conceptualisation as a three-dimension construct: interaction, orchestration, affect |
| Wong and Merrilees (2007a, 2007b, 2008) | Export, Manufactured goods and service firms | Philosophical perspective: a mindset that ensures the brand will be recognised, featured and favoured in the marketing strategy | Philosophical conceptualisation as a single construct: "branding flows through all our marketing activities; essential to our strategy; brand is long-term asset for us" |
| O'Cass and Voola (2010) | Politics | Philosophical/Behavioural perspective: the degree to which party values brands and its practices are oriented towards building brand capabilities | Philosophical/Behavioural conceptualisation as a four-dimension construct: distinctive, functional, value adding, symbolic |
| Baumgarth (2010) | Business-to-business | Philosophical/Behavioural perspective: specific type of marketing orientation distinguished by the high relevance accorded to branding by top management | Philosophical/Behavioural conceptualisation as values, norms, artefacts and behaviours |

[Page 288]

**This paper's approach:**
Following Bridson and Evans (2004), the paper adopts a **behavioural perspective** on brand orientation. This is most reflected in organisational practices and behaviours.

Rather than treating the four dimensions (distinctiveness, functionality, value-adding, symbolism) as separate independent constructs (as Bridson and Evans 2004 did), this paper **classifies them as behavioural manifestations of a single business capability** — brand orientation.

**Limitation of Bridson and Evans (2004):** Their sample was small (60 fashion retailers only) and not generalizable. This paper investigates a more holistic model across three major retail countries.

---

#### Retail Brand Orientation

[Page 288–289]

RBO includes capabilities focused on both functional/tangible purpose and more global objectives. Four attributes of RBO:

**1. Distinctiveness**

The brand creates the opportunity for retailers to be distinctive (Torres-Baumgarten and Yucetepe 2007).

- Provides a symbol of ownership for legal purposes (Bhat and Reddy 1998)
- Signals the brand's origin (de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1999)
- Encompasses brand valuation, differentiation, and brand architecture (Aaker 1992; Holt 2003; Keller 2000)
- Early brand typology work (Goodyear 1996): brand as a means of identification, guarantee of consistency, and shortcut in decision-making
- In retail: a clear brand position ensures the store is distinctive and identifiable from competition
- Keller (2003): distinctiveness derives from favourable, strong, and unique brand associations in consumer memory

**2. Functionality**

The brand communicates that the store will provide superior **utilitarian benefits**.

- Relates to the extent the organisation uses its brand to communicate that the store will provide superior functional benefits
- Functionality relates to the brand's tangible, rationally assessed performance (de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1997a)
- Consumer image of a retailer is significantly driven by functional attributes: merchandise accessibility and depth of merchandise range (Mazursky and Jacoby 1986)
- Park, Jaworski, and MacInnis (1986): functional brands are those that satisfy utilitarian needs

[Page 289]

**3. Augmentation**

The brand provides experiential value by acting as a receptacle for intangible benefits.

- Ailawadi and Keller (2004): augmentation is about achieving differentiation through additional quality and service attributes
- Brodie (2009): integrating the role of the brand in value-adding processes improves customer experiences
- Brands provide experiential value including quality and service (Darden and Babin 1994; de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1998a, 1998b, 1999; Doyle 1990)
- In retail: the affective meaning derived from both functional and psychological characteristics of a well-recognised retail brand
- A brand-oriented retailer invests in the long-term future of the brand — not just reactive competitor-matching

**4. Symbolism**

The brand exploits consumers' internally generated needs for self-enhancement, group membership, lifestyle, or ego-identification.

- de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley (1997b): symbolic features are intangible, emotional values of the brand
- Park et al. (1986): symbolism = desire for products that fulfil internally generated needs for self-enhancement, role positions, group membership, or ego-identification
- Mitchell et al. (2012): retail SME managers identify their brand as symbolic in nature — often a global overall assessment
- Congruence between consumer self-concept and retail brand personality (O'Cass and Grace 2008) is critical

**Three key elements of retail positional advantage:**

**Hypotheses:**

H1: The four dimensions of RBO (distinctiveness, functionality, augmentation, symbolism) will have a significant positive relationship with merchandise advantage.

H2: The four dimensions of RBO (distinctiveness, functionality, augmentation, symbolism) will have a significant positive relationship with store design advantage.

H3: The four dimensions of RBO (distinctiveness, functionality, augmentation, symbolism) will have a significant positive relationship with customer service advantage.

H4: The three dimensions of positional advantage (merchandise, store design, customer service) will have a significant positive relationship with financial performance.

H5: The three dimensions of positional advantage (merchandise, store design, customer service) will have a significant positive relationship with strategic effectiveness.

### Discussion

[Page 259]

**Overall:** The findings support the hypothesised relationships. Positional advantage is one mechanism by which organizational capabilities convert into performance outcomes (consistent with Priem and Butler 2001).

**Key findings and explanations:**

**Distinctiveness:**
- Essential for store design advantage (confirmed)
- Does NOT significantly drive merchandise or customer service advantage
- Explanation: Highly distinctive brands may focus more on the retail experience than on merchandise and service — "destination stores" where the experience is the product
- In line with Torres-Baumgarten and Yucetepe (2007): a brand-identifiable store is a physical manifestation of the brand and a critical strategic weapon

**Functionality:**
- Leads to merchandise AND customer service advantages (confirmed)
- Does NOT drive store design advantage
- Explanation: Functionality = solving customers' purchase problems. This manifests in merchandise quality and service quality but not in the physical design of the store — a functional brand doesn't need to create a luxurious store experience

**Augmentation:**
- Essential for store design AND customer service advantages
- Does NOT drive merchandise advantage
- Explanation: Augmentation adds intangible benefits — this shows up in the store atmosphere and service quality rather than in the merchandise assortment itself
- Key insight: brand's augmented value is not translated into superiority of merchandise

**Symbolism:**
- Leads to merchandise AND store design advantages
- Does NOT drive customer service
- Explanation: Symbolic brands connect with customers' self-image and lifestyle — this manifests in the choice of merchandise (that matches customers' identity) and in the store design and atmosphere (that reflects the target market). However, symbolic qualities may not translate into customer service advantage because service is measured by functional items (after-sales service, personal selling)

[Page 260]

**Performance implications:**

- **Merchandise advantage:** Significantly enhances financial performance (β = 0.17, p < 0.05) but NOT strategic effectiveness
- **Store design advantage:** Significantly enhances strategic effectiveness (β = 0.20, p < 0.001) but NOT financial performance
- **Customer service advantage:** Significantly enhances BOTH financial performance (β = 0.11) and strategic effectiveness (β = 0.08)

**Interpretation:**
- Merchandise quality drives immediate financial returns (sales, margins)
- Store design drives long-term competitive position (strategic effectiveness)
- Customer service drives both financial and strategic performance — the most valuable positional advantage

---

### Conclusion

[Page 260–261]

**Key managerial implications:**

1. **Allocate resources to building a strong RBO** — the four dimensions play different roles in relation to positional advantages; all four need investment
2. **Different RBO dimensions drive different advantages** — managers must understand which capabilities are most critical for their specific positional advantage goals
3. **A position of superiority in only one aspect is insufficient** — you need strength across multiple dimensions to assure both financial and strategic returns
4. **Merchandise and service superiority are critical to financial success** — store design and customer service drive achievement of objectives

**Theoretical contributions:**
- Validates RBO construct across three countries
- Provides empirical support for SPP framework in retail context
- Develops psychometrically robust measures for future research

**Limitations and future research:**
- Self-reporting bias (organizational perspective study; no consumer perspective)
- Sustainability of positional advantage not investigated
- Alternative measures using objective performance data would strengthen findings
- Future research should explore brand personality and self-image congruence as additional sources of positional advantage

# SECTION 5: "How Cults Seduce and What Marketing Can Learn from Them"
**Authors:** Alex Wipperfurth and John Grant
**Publisher:** Plan B (Plan B consulting firm)
**Year:** 2002
**PDF Pages:** ~306–321

[Page 306]

### Introduction

The paper opens with a thought experiment: What would happen if you didn't send the Jehovah's Witnesses away when they came knocking? Eventually they would invite you to a Kingdom Hall meeting — but only after multiple visits, each bringing a different assistant, so that when you finally visit, you see familiar faces and feel you belong.

**The core insight:** Cults have fine-tuned their marketing to hook into nuances of human nature. They thrive under conditions of maximum resistance and minimum resources — exactly the conditions many troubled brands face.

**The relevance to marketing:**
The authors came to study cults while working with Napster during its darkest days in 2001 — when Napster had transformed from the fastest-growing technology in history into a legal black hole, with most music files barred and shutdown imminent. What remained was a hard core of fanatics willing to do anything to keep Napster alive.

**On ethics:**
"Cults are like the 'I Love You' computer worm" — reaching millions through exploiting social trust. The BBC's reputation for respectability made it uniquely susceptible to the worm. Ethics should be judged by the result, not just the act. The authors argue that cult marketing is a last resort — indicated when: the brand is in a life-or-death situation; all other avenues have been exhausted.

### THE TACTICS — 1. Boundaries: THE MOONIES

[Page 309]

**THE FIREWALL**
- Cults build a protective barrier
- Only those committed and close to the core are fully in the know
- Outsiders are kept largely in the dark

*The Moonies (Unification Church) are defended by religious status, secrecy, armies of lawyers and lobbyists, and powerful links in the Washington establishment. Responding to criticisms of brainwashing and hard-sell tactics, they rebranded themselves "The Family Federation for World Peace and Unification" and applied for state funding for social projects.*

**"BIG BROTHER"**
- Cults define "what we're not"
- A common tactic is to define a big enemy in the outside world
- Attacks from the outside only reinforce the paranoia
- The firewall is also an information firewall
- Only those inside know the truth; outsiders should not be listened to

*The Unification Church's main enemy is Christianity. They proclaim that "Moon's Church will replace Christianity in America" and that "Jesus is not in the kingdom of heaven" (because he "did not succeed in his mission"), and that "the blessed children (of Moonies) are better than Jesus."*

**INTERNAL VS. EXTERNAL COMMUNICATIONS**
- Members have privileged information
- Communication is intimate, extensive, and frequent
- Outsiders are often fed a completely different story

*The Moonies control internal communication rigorously: for instance their rule "no first weekers talking to first weekers" (i.e., sharing doubts). Externally they use affiliates — for instance supplying sex education programs to public schools ("Total Abstinence"). They also own The Washington Times.*

**THE SPOTLIGHT**
Cults provide selective glimpses of their parallel universe by staging their own dramatic news, events, and experiences.

*The Moonies stage ecstatic public rallies and mass weddings. They court celebrity endorsement through "charity awards dinners" attended by politicians at the highest level. High profile converts are prized — for instance in 2001, Catholic Archbishop Emmanuel Millingo.*

### THE TACTICS — 2. Initiation: MAHARISHI MAHESH YOGA

[Page 310]

**SELECTING RECRUITS**
- Cults focus not on "who we can get" but "who is ready"
- People going through dramatic life change make excellent recruits: new students, recent divorcees...

*85% of people who join a cult do so through a friend or acquaintance. The Premie (Prempal Rawat a.k.a. Maharaji) followers make this their hunting ground. Cult members are charged with getting a friend, colleague, or family member to come to a special introductory video screening or to "witness a ceremony" (when there, they find everyone must join in).*

**BAITING THE HOOK**
- No one wakes up one day and says "I know, I'll join a cult"
- Cults use bait which invites you in for an innocent, worthwhile purpose with high propensity for repeat visits — like a course
- During this course, you are initiated into a world view that says "learning the ways of this cult is the real purpose of my life"

*The Premie's bait is "Transcendental Meditation"™ consisting of traditional meditation techniques, a "Science of Creative Intelligence" and the "Puja" modelled on Hindu religious ceremonies. This was a winning combination in the hippy-spiritual 1960s and 70s, and TM became so widespread it had to be excluded from being taught in public schools.*

**LOVE BOMBING**
- A key human need which cults meet is belonging
- Imagine stumbling across a weird and wonderful community where everyone instantly loves and accepts you!

*Only those cult members who have shown an aptitude for attracting and reassuring potential recruits are included in these introductory sessions; and a relationship is built across a series of convivial social meetings.*

**MATRICULATION**
- This is a critical process and is carefully managed
- You can't just walk in and "join" — you have to be invited and experience an initiation
- This is the "brainwashing" stage: intense, isolating, aimed at breaking you down, getting you to internalize and identify with certain problems, then building you back up as a believer

*After the introductory sessions an instructor interviews you and decides if you're "ready for the knowledge" and to accept the Maharaji as the only source of this "inner truth". When you "pass", you get to the "knowledge session" where you are shown the techniques.*

### THE TACTICS — 3. Customs: JEHOVAH'S WITNESSES

[Page 311]

**PYRAMID**
- Cults know that treating everyone the same means making no one feel special or motivated
- They act as an emotional pyramid scheme, self-sustaining and administering through many levels
- Communication is mainly vertical

*The Jehovah's Witness organisation is known as the Watchtower. Each Kingdom Hall is led by elders. Witnesses are not allowed to take any initiatives of their own, but in all matters must await orders from their immediate superior; "meat in due season". Anyone who questions an order is kicked out for "disfellowship".*

**DUTIES**
- Cults keep people very busy (too busy to stop and think)
- Cults are about far more than fundraising — every member has many basic duties: do the leg work; recruit; improve whatever needs fixing; sell crafts...
- Members get promoted through hierarchies as reward for loyalty and achievement

*Idleness is a sin! The most common complaint of ex-cult members is not the spiritual content, so much as that all their spare time is devoted to work, like mending the Kingdom Hall roof and trudging from doorstep to doorstep.*

**PSYCHOLOGICAL TWISTS**
- Cults are fuelled by commitments of time, money, activities...
- Contrary to their image, they seldom coerce members into this
- Rather they create a context where there is competition (and strong peer pressure) to have your commitments accepted
- If there is resistance they turn this on the individual; it's clearly something they need to "work on"!

*Jehovah's Witnesses live by an exceptionally strict code. They must avoid "bad association" (non-Witnesses) and cannot lie or cheat or drink... The psychological twist is called "Theocratic Strategy"; which means they can for instance lie for the cause, to anyone who doesn't "have the right to know".*

**MOMENTUM**
- Cults stay relevant and vibrant through momentum
- If there is nothing much happening, cults rely on apparently random acts of insanity and tests of loyalty
- Deadlines and dates are common tactics

*The cases of Witnesses refusing medical treatment are famous. And the Watchtower has been saying that Armageddon (the end of the world) is "soon" since 1884.*

### THE TACTICS — 4. Ideology: THE SCIENTOLOGISTS

[Page 312]

**THE LEADER/THE APOSTLES**
- Cults tend to be personality cults
- The leaders' story is the story of the cult; their word is law
- Access to the leader is an exclusive reward to the higher ups
- These act as apostles, evangelists, and gatekeepers

*L. Ron Hubbard is revered in the Scientologists to the extent that everything he says is taken as the literal truth; his writings are described as "data." We see here a sophisticated conceptual branding lexicon. Which is another fascinating feature of cult identity...*

**THE WORD**
- Cults have their own vocabulary, way of speaking
- This has a powerful effect: words are concepts and concepts are thoughts
- Cults also foreclose awkward debate with "wise sayings"
- There is implicit social pressure; to speak like us is to belong

*Scientology has developed an extensive proprietary language system. LRH's techniques are "the tech." The hierarchy is "the gradient." Progress is across "the bridge." Recruitment is "stats." Disciplinary action is "ethics"...*
*The lexicon is designed to convey scientific authority and foreclose debate. (A bit like business jargon really.)*

**BELIEF SYSTEM**
- Cults are masters of disciplined, simple formatting
- Part of their appeal is a simple black-and-white view of reality, far from the mess of real relationships and life conflicts
- This is pinned to a general vision few would argue like Peace or Clarity
- A constant repetition of the same format displaces all else with this simple core: chanting, reciting, committing...

*The clarity of Scientology is based on the fact that it is 100% right and if you don't get it you need "handling." If you don't absorb something then you have to redo a course at your own expense. If this fails you have do "ethics." It's your fault.*

**THE RULES**
- Arbitrary restrictions and rules, oddities, and experiences reinforce a way of life "through the looking glass"
- Ritual experiences underline the "otherness of this world"
- Cutting ties with your mundane old existence is key to making this alternate reality the reality

*Scientologists are not supposed to wear spectacles (because L. Ron Hubbard once asserted that "the tech" could cure vision defects). Scientology is anti-psychiatry and once even deliberately orchestrated a Prozac health scare.*

### When Cult Marketing Is Indicated

[Page 315]

**Full cult marketing is NOT for the faint-hearted. It is a last resort, because like major surgery, the "cure" has its own dangers.**

**Reasons cult marketing is a last resort:**
- It excludes the majority of your potential audience
- It is slow
- It is difficult to transition out of again

*(Example: Harley-Davidson is now struggling to transition to a more mainstream clientele because diehard HOGs are scaring off new white-collar customers, dismissing them as "rubbies" — rich, urban bikers.)*

**Right side box: "Cult marketing is indicated for..."**
- A brand which is in a life or death situation
- They have a genuine ideology (you can't fake this)
  - ...but this is out of sync with the current context
  - ...and/or they have a crisis which has put them on the defensive
  - ...and/or they have a product that isn't working fully yet
  - ...or a product that's fallen behind
- They are small enough to be the underdog (but not so small that they're not worth fighting for)
- The cult activity is for a limited period, to buy time
- All other avenues have been exhausted

**The Napster conclusion:**
That was precisely the situation with Napster. As it turned out, they managed NOT to implement a full cult marketing program ("help us to save Napster") because a route to relaunch was found instead.

### Cult Case Studies

#### CULT CASE 1: "IN-THE-KNOW" MARKETING COMMUNICATIONS — LINUX

[Page 316]

**The Open Source movement** was a reaction from within the geek community to the commercial empires being built on what used to be common land (open-source code). The O/S had always been shared as free source code.

The movement's manifesto: "The Cathedral and the Bazaar" (famous white paper).

The cult founder: Linus Torvalds — a shy young student at Helsinki University — who uploaded his operating system project to a bulletin board for reviews and feedback. Linux was born.

**i. Members-only:** The Linux community for years extended only to other hackers and tech professionals. Even now with 15 million users, they maintain a mystique and solidarity from their invisibility to mainstream audiences. "For the establishment it was seen no threat if seen at all. It was like Hannibal coming over the Alps with elephants!"

**ii. David vs. Goliath:** Linux is a lot like Unix, only free. Its spiritual enemy has always been Microsoft. This confrontation became real as Linux emerged as an affordable alternative to Windows XP for small businesses. A Microsoft executive, Jim Allchin, was quoted: "I'm an American. I believe in the American Way. I worry if the government encourages open source..."

**iii. Internal vs. External PR:** On the Internet no one knows you are a moonlighter. The Linux code was developed in spare time by people whose full-time work was officially contracted and owned. There's a story that one Silicon Valley CTO sent an anonymous note about a secret Linux meeting at his company — 100 people turned up, including some of his direct reports.

**iv. Leaks and Stunts:** When it went public, it went PUBLIC. The Open Source movement had Silicon Valley veteran insiders who knew the one coronation event that would put Linux on the map would be a Wall Street listing. Companies like Red Hat raised billions — taking the Linux dagger to Microsoft's throat.

#### CULT CASE 2: COMMITTED CUSTOMER ACQUISITION — HARLEY-DAVIDSON

[Page 317]

Harley was nearly bankrupt in the early 1980s after the Japanese motorbike invasion. The turnaround began with a management buyout and in 1983 the formation of HOG (Harley Owners Group) — a bikers' cult.

Result: 12 continuous years of growth. HOG is the largest company-sponsored motorbike club in the world with 365,000 members in 940 chapters.

**i. Inner-Circle Targeting:** The initial HOGs were mostly Hells Angels. They drew from blue-collar America — the very group whose jobs were threatened by Japanese imports. They also drew on patriotism and disenfranchisement. One HOG went as far as to thank Harley CEO Rich Teerlink: "For giving me the parade I never got when I returned from Vietnam."

**ii. Rallying Cry:** Simple, powerful message: "Help Harley Survive." This transcended the usual "them and us" marketing divide and brought HOGs into a shared cause. There were strong overtones of "Save America" — Harley symbolizing an industrial nation's plight in chrome and steel. In true cult style they added innocent elements like fundraising for the Muscular Dystrophy Foundation.

**iii. Charismatic Events:** Harley rides and rallies (already a feature of life in the Hells Angels) were the social glue. They culminated in five-year anniversary rallies that made even Moonie mass weddings look low-key.

**iv. Driving Loyalty:** One tactic to lure new riders is **Rider's Edge** — a three-day driver's training course organized by dealers for new motorcycle riders. Lara Lee, director of Rider's Edge: "We wanted to take the person who felt like an outsider and turn them into an insider." Harley also introduced a **Ride Free exchange program**: if you bought the starter model (Harley Sportster) then you could trade it in at full value a year later for a bigger bike.

#### CULT CASE 3: PASSIONATE COMMUNITY — E-BAY

[Page 318]

In 1997, e-Bay found itself in a crisis when major fraud scandals threatened their credibility and business model. They were in danger of turning from something that felt like a neighborhood car boot sale into a nasty, risky black market. Introducing draconian regulations would kill that special, folksy vibe even quicker. So they turned to their community and established a whole architecture of trust:

**i. Loyalty Tiers:** The more you trade, the higher you get in the hierarchy — via a rating scheme. If you cheat or defraud, you get thrown out. Sellers with a higher rating can command a premium of up to 30%. Some people make a living just selling on e-Bay, supported through an extra tier — the Power-Selling program with added services.

**ii. Participation Marketing:** The average user spends 3.5 hours on the site (longer than anywhere else in e-commerce). You have to rate the other party after every sale. Some members even took it on themselves to coach newbies. "Uncle Griffiths" was so active in this coaching that he was hired by Omidyar to be the company's first customer support rep. eBabes — a club of eBay sellers — have meetings to discuss strategies and issues.

**iii. Turning the Tables:** The eBay business model turns e-commerce into a gambling game. Instead of getting in and getting out, the bidding process encourages multiple visits as buyers check the status of their bids. There's a thrill to winning a bid. Nancy Wilson, self-proclaimed e-Bay Queen: "I've never had anything I enjoy doing as much as doing this. I watch the bidding every day like a little kid."

**iv. Brand News:** In quick succession eBay introduced: the ratings system; insurance of up to $200 against fraud; a seller identity verification service ($5, by Equifax); a ban on sellers bidding their own products; a ban on buyers who win an auction but do not complete the sale. They also cleaned the site up, banning firearm sales and relegating anything questionable to an Adults Only area. eBay has also created its own news — like the entire disgruntled Silicon Valley IT department who auctioned themselves off to another company, or the woman who put her hand in marriage up for bids.

#### CULT CASE 4: BRAND RELIGION — APPLE

[Page 319–320]

"If one company can lay claim to inventing cult marketing, internally and externally, it's Apple. And for nearly thirty years they've proved — to our principle that cult marketing is only ever a short-term measure — that every rule has an exception."

Apple ticks every box on the cult scorecard:

**Boundaries:** Apple has the only proprietary system to go it alone against Windows. They have played on "Big Brother" as a constant refrain (the famous 1984 Super Bowl ad). They have drawn a fence around profitable niches like the multimedia community.

**Initiation:** OSX broke every rule of product evolution — learning curve is steep. But it over-delivers once you master it. "40% of new i-Mac orders came from first time computer buyers" — new recruits to the Apple world.

**Customs:** Apple changed the relationship between human and box. Their designs are rivaled only by Sony in aesthetics and accessibility. Apple designs are so effective that 40% of new iMac orders were from first-time computer buyers.

Core Mac users still have "a partisan spirit that's reminiscent more of fringe sects than hardware retailing." This is the Ideology — or in marketing terms, Brand Religion:

**i. Founder & Advocates:**
Steve Jobs — the man who returned to save the company on a salary of $1. Jobs started the cult with the secretive Mac team and their skull and crossbones emblem. The apostles are hardly less impressive: **Guy Kawasaki** — whose smart, empassioned articulation of the vision made him the original corporate evangelist. **Jonathan Ives** — designer of iMac, Cube, T4 — "who is fast accomplishing a one man Bauhaus."

**ii. Brand Vocabulary:**
Apple invented (or at least popularized) the whole language of graphical interface computing: folders, icons, mouse, double click.

They have been masterful meme merchants, giving loyal followers the ammunition to bring down PC users:
- "C:/ONGRTLNS.W95"
- "WINDOWS 95 = MAC 89 + 8MB"
- "Saying Windows 95 is equivalent to Macintosh is like finding a potato that looks like Jesus and believing you've witnessed the second coming!!!"
- "Macintosh. A religion, a way of life."

*Note: It is noticeable that the cult principles of the company have seeped into their language.*

**iii. Brand Manifesto:**
Many Apple people genuinely believe they are making the world a better place — reinforced by the company's initiatives in education and with disadvantaged groups. They have used cult media like collectible t-shirts to constantly refresh the Apple allegiance ("you wear your commitment on your chest"). One contractor: "Apple is the only place where you have to put your t-shirt size on your job application."

**iv. Rituals & Principles:**
Jobs introduced the ultimate counterpart to corporate normality: compulsory anarchy. They embraced diversity and soft values but never lost sight of results aggression. The Apple mantra: **"Great Developers Ship."**

### From Cult Marketing to Global Religion?

[Page 321]

**So where is it all heading?**

In an age when even the world's hottest brands are often facing a firing squad — Coca-Cola (European dioxin scare), Nike (child labor), Gap (badly out of fashion) — cult marketing is set to grow.

**Cult marketing as antidote:**
It is an antidote to the "fat, lazy years of 'nice ads, steady growth.'"

It is unusual because it is **defensive** (in a world of offensive marketing).

It is also pointing to a new killer positioning: **non-premium exclusivity.** That's why it's also the natural tactic of companies like IKEA, whose exploits include:
- Banning customers with beards from their Bristol store
- Customer sleep-overs in Stockholm to launch IKEA beds
- America's own DYKEA lesbian action group

**Cherry-picking the cult tactics:**
The broadest influence may be on mainstream marketing campaigns — cult as tactic, not strategy. Rather than the whole 16-point program, companies can cherry-pick the cult tactics:

- **Brand Religion for internal fanaticism:** Like the Nike Ekins sales force, with their swoosh tattoos
- **"In-the-know" MARCOM for niche audiences:** Like developers, pharmacists, or journalists. Like Volvo who sampled their new S60 to beautiful people, along with reservations at valet-parking restaurants

**The conclusion:**
But "cult" will never become a marketing "global religion" like brand image, because it only works in from the fringe. Some of us who have caught the cult marketing bug think that's fine.
