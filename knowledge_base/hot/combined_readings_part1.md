### What Is a Brand?

A **brand** is a name, term, sign, symbol, or design, or a combination of them, intended to identify the goods and services of one seller or group of sellers and to differentiate them from those of competition.

A brand is more than just a product. A product is anything we can offer to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption that might satisfy a need or want. A brand adds other dimensions that differentiate it from other products designed to satisfy the same need.

**Brand differences** may be:
- Rational and tangible (related to product performance)
- Symbolic, emotional, and intangible (related to what the brand represents)

[Page 2]

### Why Do Brands Matter?

**To Consumers:**
- Brands identify the source or maker of a product
- Brands assign responsibility to a particular manufacturer or distributor
- Brands reduce search costs (simplify decision making)
- Brands reduce risk (signal consistent quality)
- Brands represent a promise or bond with the maker
- Brands can be symbolic devices (self-concept)
- Brands signal quality and reduce information costs

**Types of consumer risk:**
1. Functional risk — product doesn't perform as expected
2. Physical risk — product poses a threat to physical wellbeing
3. Financial risk — product is not worth its price
4. Social risk — product leads to embarrassment
5. Psychological risk — product affects mental wellbeing
6. Time risk — failure leads to opportunity cost of finding another solution

[Page 3]

**To Manufacturers/Firms:**
- Brands provide a means of identification to simplify handling or tracing
- Brands offer legal protection for unique product features
- Brands signal quality to satisfied customers
- Brands are a means of endowing products with unique associations
- Brands are a source of competitive advantage
- Brands are a source of financial return (brand equity)

### Can Anything Be Branded?

Yes — virtually anything can be branded. Physical goods, services, retailers and distributors, online products and services, people, organizations, sports, arts, entertainment, geographic locations, and ideas.

[Page 4]

**Physical goods:** Coca-Cola, Kellogg's, Kodak, Marlboro, Gillette — some of the world's most valuable brands. Consumer packaged goods companies were early pioneers of branding.

**Services:** The intangibility of services makes branding especially important. Services are performances or actions rather than objects. Examples: McKinsey (consulting), Citibank (banking), American Airlines, Hilton hotels. Services branding is challenging because quality varies, customers are involved in production, and they cannot be stored.

**Retailers and Distributors:** Store brands, own-label brands. Retailers create their own brand names (Marks & Spencer's St. Michael label; Sainsbury's own brands).

[Page 5]

**Online products and services:** Amazon, Google, eBay, Yahoo — digital brands that have achieved enormous value.

**People and Organizations:** Michael Jordan, Oprah Winfrey, Barack Obama — individuals as brands. The Red Cross, UNICEF — organizations as brands.

**Sports, Arts, Entertainment:** Manchester United, New York Yankees, the NFL. Museums (the Louvre), performing arts organizations (Royal Shakespeare Company).

**Geographic Locations:** New Zealand ("100% Pure"), Las Vegas ("What Happens Here, Stays Here"), France as a wine location.

**Ideas and Causes:** Livestrong, PETA, religious movements — ideas can be branded.

[Page 6]

### What Are the Strongest Brands?

**Interbrand's Best Global Brands** methodology considers three key components:
1. Financial performance of the branded products or services
2. Role of brand in the purchase decision process (% of demand attributable to brand vs. other factors)
3. Brand strength (ability to generate continued future earnings)

**Top brands (historical Interbrand rankings):**
1. Apple
2. Google
3. Coca-Cola
4. Microsoft
5. IBM
6. Toyota
7. Samsung
8. GE (General Electric)
9. McDonald's
10. Amazon

[Page 7]

### Branding Challenges and Opportunities

**The New Marketing Environment:**
- Savvy consumers — more educated, more skeptical
- Brand proliferation — too many products, too many brands, not enough differentiation
- Media fragmentation — rise of digital media, decline of mass media effectiveness
- Increased competition — domestic and global
- Increased costs — higher cost of product introduction and support
- Greater accountability — short-term pressure from financial markets

[Page 8]

**The Brand Equity Concept:**
Brand equity is the added value endowed on products and services. It may be reflected in the way consumers think, feel, and act with respect to the brand.

Brand equity is an important bridge. It reflects the past investment in marketing a brand as well as a means to realize future returns.

### The Brand Equity Concept

**Customer-based brand equity (CBBE):** The differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the marketing of that brand.

Three key ingredients:
1. The differential effect (brand equity arises from differences in consumer response)
2. Brand knowledge (the differences arise from what consumers have learned, felt, seen, and heard about the brand as a result of their experiences over time)
3. Consumer response to marketing (reflected in perceptions, preferences, and behavior related to all aspects of the brand's marketing)

[Page 9]

**Positive customer-based brand equity** = consumers react more favorably to the product and the way it is marketed when the brand is identified versus when it is not identified.

**Negative customer-based brand equity** = consumers react less favorably to the brand's marketing activity compared with an unnamed or fictitiously named version.

**Marketing advantages of strong brands:**
- Improved perceptions of product performance
- Greater loyalty
- Less vulnerability to competitive marketing actions and crises
- Larger margins
- More inelastic consumer response to price increases
- More elastic consumer response to price decreases
- Greater trade cooperation and support
- Increased marketing communication effectiveness
- Possible licensing opportunities
- More favorable brand extension evaluations

[Page 10]

### Strategic Brand Management Process

A four-step process:
1. **Identifying and establishing brand positioning and values** — mental maps, competitive frame of reference, points of parity and points of difference, core brand values, brand mantra
2. **Planning and implementing brand marketing programs** — choosing brand elements, integrating brand marketing activities, leveraging secondary associations
3. **Measuring and interpreting brand performance** — brand value chain, brand audits, brand tracking, brand equity management system
4. **Growing and sustaining brand equity** — brand-product matrix, brand portfolios and hierarchies, brand expansion strategies, brand reinforcement and revitalization

## Chapter 2: Customer-Based Brand Equity and Brand Positioning
**Source:** Strategic Brand Management, Keller | PDF Pages: ~10–50

[Page 11]

### Customer-Based Brand Equity

The **CBBE model** approaches brand equity from the perspective of the consumer. The power of a brand lies in what resides in the minds and hearts of customers.

**Building a strong brand** involves four steps:
1. Ensure customer identification of the brand and an association of the brand in their minds with a specific product class or customer need
2. Firmly establish the totality of brand meaning in the minds of customers (strategically linking tangible and intangible brand associations)
3. Elicit the proper customer responses to this brand identity and brand meaning
4. Convert brand response to create an intense, active loyalty relationship between customers and the brand

These four steps correspond to four fundamental questions consumers ask about a brand:
1. **Who are you?** (Brand identity)
2. **What are you?** (Brand meaning)
3. **What about you? What do I think or feel about you?** (Brand responses)
4. **What about you and me? What kind of association and how much of a connection would I like to have with you?** (Brand relationships)

[Page 12]

### The Brand Resonance Pyramid (CBBE Pyramid)

A pyramid-shaped model with six brand building blocks arranged in four levels:

**Level 1 (Foundation — bottom): Salience**
- Brand salience relates to aspects of the awareness of the brand
- How often and easily the brand is evoked under various purchase or consumption situations
- Depth of brand awareness: ease of recognition and recall
- Breadth of brand awareness: range of purchase and usage situations where the brand comes to mind

**Level 2 (two blocks): Performance and Imagery**
- Brand performance: how well the product or service meets customers' functional needs
  - Five important types: primary characteristics and supplementary features; product reliability, durability, serviceability; service effectiveness, efficiency, and empathy; style and design; price
- Brand imagery: extrinsic properties of the product/service (user profiles, purchase/usage situations, personality/values, history/heritage/experiences)

**Level 3 (two blocks): Judgments and Feelings**
- Brand judgments: customers' personal opinions and evaluations of the brand
  - Quality, credibility, consideration, superiority
- Brand feelings: customers' emotional responses and reactions to the brand
  - Warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect

**Level 4 (top): Resonance**
- The ultimate relationship and level of identification the customer has with the brand
- Four components: behavioral loyalty (repeat purchase); attitudinal attachment (love of brand, "more than just a product"); sense of community (kinship with others); active engagement (customer invests own resources in the brand beyond purchase)

[Page 13]

**Examples of Brand Resonance:**
- Harley-Davidson: tattoos, rallies, HOG (Harley Owners Group) — the ultimate brand community
- Apple: Apple user groups, tattoos, the "Mac religion"
- eBay: community self-governance, "PowerSellers," "Uncle Griffiths" story
- Nike: NikePlus running community, Nike+ app

[Page 14]

### Brand Positioning

**Brand positioning** is the act of designing the company's offer and image so that it occupies a distinct and valued place in the target customer's minds.

**Key concepts:**
- **Target market** — who is the brand for?
- **Nature of competition** — against whom is the brand competing?
- **Points of difference (PODs)** — attributes or benefits that consumers strongly associate with a brand, positively evaluate, and believe they could not find to the same extent with a competitive brand
- **Points of parity (POPs)** — associations that are not necessarily unique to the brand but may be shared with other brands

[Page 15]

**Points of Difference (PODs)** must satisfy three criteria:
1. **Desirability** — consumers must find the POD personally relevant and important
2. **Deliverability** — the brand must have the actual or potential ability to perform on or deliver the POD
3. **Differentiability** — consumers must find the POD distinctive and superior compared to competing brands

[Page 16]

**Points of Parity (POPs):**
- **Category POPs** — associations consumers view as necessary to be a legitimate product in a particular category (the minimum threshold)
- **Competitive POPs** — associations designed to negate competitors' points of difference

**Key insight:** Sometimes it is not sufficient just to achieve POPs and PODs — brand must convince consumers that its weakness is actually a strength. Classic examples:
- Listerine: negating "bad taste" POD by arguing that bad taste means it kills germs
- Visa: negating "not accepted everywhere" (American Express) by emphasizing worldwide acceptance

[Page 17]

**Choosing POPs and PODs:**

**Means of differentiation:**
- Product quality
- Service quality
- Personnel quality
- Channel quality
- Image quality

**Competitive Frame of Reference:**
- Category membership: signals the category to which the brand belongs
- Subsuming signals: convincing consumers that the brand belongs to a well-regarded category
- Exemplary messaging: showing well-regarded brands as examples in advertising

[Page 18]

### Brand Mantras

A **brand mantra** is an articulation of the heart and soul of the brand. It is a short, three-to-five word phrase that captures the irrefutable essence or spirit of the brand positioning.

Examples:
- Nike: "Authentic Athletic Performance"
- Disney: "Fun Family Entertainment"
- McDonald's: "Fun Food Fast" (internal)

**Three key criteria for a good brand mantra:**
1. Communicate what the brand is about (what category it belongs to, what benefits it provides)
2. Define what the brand is not (simplify and clarify)
3. Be memorable and inspiring

[Page 19]

### Internal Branding

Strong brands require internal branding — making sure employees and marketing partners appreciate and understand basic branding notions and how they can help (or hurt) brand equity.

**Key elements of internal branding:**
- Brand charter/book (employees know what the brand stands for)
- Brand training programs
- Brand equity reports (internal)
- Senior management visibly leading brand building

### The Brand Value Chain

The **brand value chain** is a structured approach to assessing the sources and outcomes of brand equity and the manner in which marketing activities create brand value.

**Model Flow:**
Marketing Program Investment → Program Quality Multiplier → Customer Mind-Set → Market Conditions Multiplier → Market Performance → Investor Sentiment Multiplier → Shareholder Value

[Page 52]

**Stage 1: Marketing Program Investment**
Any marketing program investment that potentially contributes to brand value development:
- Product research, development, and design
- Trade or intermediary support
- Marketing communications (advertising, promotion, sponsorship, events, PR, etc.)

**Program quality multiplier** — the ability of the marketing program to affect the customer mind-set depends on quality of the investment:
- Distinctiveness
- Relevance
- Integration
- Value

[Page 53]

**Stage 2: Customer Mind-Set**
Everything that exists in the minds and hearts of customers with respect to the brand:
- Brand awareness
- Brand associations (strength, favorability, uniqueness)
- Brand attitudes
- Brand attachment
- Brand activity (customer engagement)

**Market conditions multiplier:**
- Competitive superiority
- Channel and other intermediary support
- Customer size and profile (how favorable and responsive customer base is)

[Page 54]

**Stage 3: Market Performance**
How the customer mind-set affects market performance:
- Price premiums and elasticities
- Market share
- Expansion success
- Cost structure
- Profitability

**Investor sentiment multiplier:**
- Market dynamics (growth rates, risk profiles)
- Growth potential
- Risk profile
- Brand contribution

[Page 55]

**Stage 4: Shareholder Value**
Determination of the financial value of the brand:
- Stock price
- Price/earnings ratio
- Market capitalization

## Chapter 4: Choosing Brand Elements to Build Brand Equity
**Source:** Strategic Brand Management, Keller | PDF Pages: ~55–107

[Page 56]

### Brand Elements

**Brand elements** (brand identities) are those trademarkable devices that serve to identify and differentiate the brand. The main ones are: brand names, URLs, logos, symbols, characters, slogans, jingles, and packaging.

**Six criteria for choosing brand elements:**

1. **Memorable** — How easily is the brand element recalled and recognized at both purchase and consumption?
2. **Meaningful** — To what extent is the brand element credible and suggestive of the corresponding category? Does it suggest something about a product ingredient or the type of person who might use the brand?
3. **Likable** — How aesthetically appealing do consumers find the brand element? Is it fun and interesting, rich visual and verbal imagery?
4. **Transferable** — Can the brand element be used to introduce new products in the same or different categories? To what extent does the brand element add to brand equity across geographic boundaries and market segments?
5. **Adaptable** — How adaptable and updatable is the brand element?
6. **Protectable** — How legally protectable is the brand element? How competitively protectable is it?

[Page 57]

**The first three criteria (memorable, meaningful, likable) are "offensive" strategy** — how to build brand equity through the brand element.

**The last three criteria (transferable, adaptable, protectable) are "defensive" strategy** — how to leverage and preserve the equity in the brand element.

### Brand Names

The brand name is perhaps the most important brand element because it often captures the central theme or key associations of a product in a very compact and economical fashion.

**Desirable qualities of brand names:**
1. Simple and easy to pronounce and spell
2. Familiar and meaningful
3. Different, distinctive, and unusual
4. Relevant to the product category

[Page 58]

**Brand naming approaches:**

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Descriptive names | Describe product/service literally | General Motors, American Airlines, British Telecom |
| Suggestive names | Suggest a benefit or quality | Snapple (snap + apple), Duracell (durable + cell) |
| Compound names | Combine two or more words | Salesforce, Facebook, YouTube |
| Classical names | Derived from Latin, Greek, or other languages | Amazon, Nike, Olympus |
| Arbitrary names | Real words but no connection to product | Apple, Camel, Shell |
| Fanciful names | Coined words with no prior meaning | Kodak, Xerox, Google, Häagen-Dazs |

[Page 59]

**Brand Naming Research:**
Brand name research includes free association, customer surveys, and linguistic analysis. Names are tested for:
- Awareness and recall
- Connotations and meaning
- Legal clearance (trademark)
- Linguistic and cultural translation

**International brand name considerations:**
- Avoid names that translate poorly
- Coca-Cola ("Bite the Wax Tadpole" in early Chinese translation — later resolved)
- Chevy Nova ("no va" = "doesn't go" in Spanish — later shown to be a myth)
- Clairol "Mist Stick" curling iron (German: "mist" = manure)

[Page 60]

### URLs (Uniform Resource Locators)

Domain names must be registered and are increasingly important as online brand presence. Key challenges:
- Many desirable domain names are already registered
- "Cybersquatting" — registering brand-related domain names for resale
- Legal resolution: the Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (US)

### Logos and Symbols

**Logos** are the visual trademark of a company. Logos range from:
- **Wordmarks:** the brand name written in a distinctive way (Coca-Cola script, Sony logo)
- **Abstract/symbolic logos:** stylized images (Nike swoosh, Target bullseye, Apple apple)
- **Character logos:** animate mascots (Michelin Man, Ronald McDonald)

[Page 61]

**Advantages of symbols:**
- Non-verbal — communicate across language barriers
- Can be updated over time without losing equity
- Can be transferred to new product categories

**Brand characters** (mascots) have special qualities:
- Human-like properties make the brand more approachable
- Can be used in advertising to entertain and demonstrate product benefits
- Examples: Tony the Tiger (Kellogg's Frosted Flakes), Geico Gecko, GEICO Caveman, Pillsbury Doughboy, Mr. Clean, Jolly Green Giant, Ronald McDonald

[Page 62]

**Updating logos:**
- Shell (oil shell logo — refined/simplified over seven iterations from 1900 to 1999)
- Betty Crocker (updated seven times from 1936 to 1996, each time modernizing the portrait)
- Pepsi (multiple logo updates — recent "smiling" globe design)
- The key is to keep the spirit and key elements while modernizing execution

### Slogans

**Slogans** are short phrases that communicate descriptive or persuasive information about a brand.

**Functions of slogans:**
- Build brand awareness
- Communicate key brand benefits
- Reinforce brand positioning
- Create emotional connections

[Page 63]

**Famous slogans:**
- Nike: "Just Do It"
- Apple: "Think Different" / "The Power to Be Your Best"
- BMW: "The Ultimate Driving Machine"
- DeBeers: "A Diamond Is Forever" (consistently rated the #1 advertising slogan of the 20th century)
- Volkswagen: "Think Small" / "Drivers Wanted"
- McDonald's: "I'm Lovin' It"
- American Express: "Don't Leave Home Without It"
- Avis: "We Try Harder"
- Allstate: "You're in Good Hands"
- Walmart: "Save Money. Live Better."

[Page 64]

**Designing slogans:**
Good slogans tend to be:
- Short (typically 3–7 words)
- Memorable and distinctive
- Meaningful and substantive (not just clever)
- Adaptable over time
- Legally protectable

**Updating slogans:**
- Should not be changed too frequently — they lose their equity
- When change is necessary, new slogan should evolve naturally from old (Chevy: "Baseball, Hot Dogs, Apple Pie and Chevrolet" → "Like a Rock" → "American Revolution")

### Jingles

**Jingles** are musical messages written around a brand. They are catchy and memorable — can contain brand-related information.

Examples:
- "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing" (Coca-Cola, 1971)
- Oscar Mayer "My Bologna Has a First Name"
- McDonald's "Ba Da Ba Ba Ba — I'm Lovin' It"

[Page 65]

### Packaging

**Packaging** is the activities of designing and producing the container or wrapper for a product. It is one of the most important brand elements, as it is often the last thing consumers see before making a purchase.

**Functions of packaging:**
1. Identify the brand
2. Convey descriptive and persuasive information
3. Facilitate product transportation and protection
4. Assist in at-home storage
5. Aid product consumption

**Packaging as a brand element:**
- Color: Tiffany's blue box, Cadbury's purple, Heinz red, Barbie pink
- Shape: Coca-Cola contour bottle (trademarked), Absolut vodka bottle
- Material: Premium vs. budget packaging signals quality expectations

[Page 66]

**Packaging research:**
- Eye-tracking studies
- Consumer preference research
- Communication effectiveness research

**Packaging changes:**
- Should signal continuity while updating
- The Tropicana redesign (2009): changed the iconic orange-with-straw design to a modern minimalist look; sales dropped 20% in two months; reverted to original design

**Secondary packaging:**
- Inner and outer packaging
- Value packs and multi-packs
- Environmentally sustainable packaging (growing importance)

[Page 67]

### Putting It All Together: Criteria for Choosing Brand Elements

**The MMLTATAP framework (Memorable, Meaningful, Likable, Transferable, Adaptable, Protectable):**

A single brand element rarely satisfies all six criteria simultaneously. Trade-offs must be made. For example:
- Descriptive names are highly meaningful and easy to remember but are hard to protect legally
- Fanciful names are easily protected but need to build meaning through marketing

**Integration of brand elements:**
- All brand elements should work together in an integrated, coherent fashion
- The name should reinforce the logo, the slogan should reinforce the logo, etc.

[Page 68]

## Chapter 5: Designing Marketing Programs to Build Brand Equity
**Source:** Strategic Brand Management, Keller | PDF Pages: ~68–107

[Page 69]

### Product Strategy

**Product** is at the heart of brand equity. The total product experience includes:
- Core product/service
- Actual product (features, quality, design, brand name, packaging)
- Augmented product (delivery, installation, after-sales service, warranty)

**Perceived quality** is one of the most important brand associations. It is a global assessment of quality, distinct from objective quality.

**Key product quality dimensions (goods):**
1. Performance (primary operating characteristics)
2. Features (supplementary characteristics)
3. Reliability (consistency of performance)
4. Conformance (match of product specifications)
5. Durability (economic and physical product life)
6. Serviceability (ease of repair)
7. Aesthetics (how a product looks, sounds, feels, smells, tastes)
8. Perceived quality (reputation and other indirect measures)

[Page 70]

**Key service quality dimensions:**
1. Reliability (performing the promised service dependably and accurately)
2. Responsiveness (willingness to help customers and provide prompt service)
3. Assurance (knowledge and courtesy of employees and ability to inspire trust)
4. Empathy (caring, individualized attention)
5. Tangibles (physical facilities, equipment, appearance of personnel)

**Relationship marketing and brand equity:**
- Customers are partners in value creation
- Customer relationship management (CRM) systems
- Database marketing and personalization
- Loyalty programs

[Page 71]

### Pricing Strategy

Price is a critical brand element — it conveys information about quality and value.

**Pricing approaches and brand equity:**
- **Everyday low pricing (EDLP)** — consistent low prices build price-value image (Walmart, Costco)
- **High-low pricing** — frequent promotions and sales cycles create deal-prone consumers
- **Premium pricing** — price communicates quality and exclusivity (luxury brands)

**Value pricing** — offers the right combination of quality and service at a fair price. Example: Procter & Gamble in the 1990s moved toward value pricing for many brands.

[Page 72]

**Price promotions and brand equity:**
Price promotions can erode brand equity if overused. The risks include:
- Consumers become sensitized to price and expect deals
- Permanent association of brand with discounts
- Damage to brand image (suggesting inferior quality)

**Price strategies for brand building:**
- Price-quality signaling (higher price = higher quality inference)
- Price segmentation (different versions for different market segments)
- Price bundling (combining products at a combined price)

### Distribution Strategy

**Channel strategy** affects brand equity through its impact on:
- Brand image (what type of channel the brand is distributed in)
- Brand availability (how easy it is to find and purchase)

[Page 73]

**Channel design considerations:**
- Direct channels (owned/company stores, Internet)
- Indirect channels (independent retailers, wholesalers)

**Push vs. pull strategies:**
- Push: use trade promotions to get retailers to carry and promote the brand
- Pull: use consumer advertising to create demand, pulling product through distribution

**Store environment:**
Physical environment of retail stores strongly influences brand perceptions. Apple stores are designed to embody Apple's brand values: clean, minimal, innovative.

[Page 74]

**Direct vs. indirect channels:**
- Nike's DTC (direct-to-consumer) strategy — Nike stores, Nike.com — gives full brand control
- Luxury brands: controlled distribution is essential to maintaining premium image (Gucci limit distribution; Louis Vuitton does not discount)

### Marketing Communications Strategy

**Marketing communications** allow firms to inform, persuade, incite, and remind consumers—directly or indirectly—about the brands they sell.

**The communications mix:**
1. Advertising
2. Sales promotion
3. Events and experiences
4. Public relations and publicity
5. Direct marketing
6. Interactive marketing
7. Word-of-mouth marketing
8. Personal selling

[Page 75]

**Role of marketing communications in building brand equity:**
Communications contribute to brand equity by:
- Creating awareness of the brand
- Linking the right associations to the brand
- Eliciting positive brand judgments or feelings
- Facilitating a stronger consumer–brand connection

**Integrated marketing communications (IMC):**
IMC mixes and matches communication options to build brand equity — various communications have different strengths and costs, and should be mixed based on brand's specific objectives.

[Page 76]

**Advertising:**
- Mass media reach and frequency
- Powerful in creating brand image and personality
- Long-term equity building capability
- Declining effectiveness in cluttered media environment

**TV advertising:** Still most powerful for image-building at scale. Can create emotional associations, memorable characters, and brand personality.

**Print advertising:** Good for detailed product information. Magazines allow for targeted audiences and premium brand image.

[Page 77]

**Online advertising:**
- Search advertising (Google AdWords)
- Display advertising
- Social media advertising
- Video advertising (YouTube)
- Key advantage: targeting precision, measurability, interactivity

**Sales promotion:**
- Consumer promotions: coupons, rebates, samples, contests/sweepstakes
- Trade promotions: price-off deals, allowances, slotting fees
- Risk: can train consumers to wait for deals

[Page 78]

**Events and sponsorships:**
- Provides direct contact with target market
- Creates emotional connections
- Can reach consumers when they are most receptive
- Examples: Red Bull extreme sports, Heineken Champions League UEFA sponsorship

**Creating brand equity through events:**
- The event must be relevant to the brand's positioning
- The association must be memorable
- Activation (not just presence) is key

[Page 79]

**Public relations:**
- Credibility — third-party endorsement
- Buzz and word-of-mouth generation
- Crisis management
- Community relations

**Word-of-mouth (WOM) and buzz marketing:**
- Consumers trust recommendations from friends and family more than advertising
- Social media amplifies WOM
- Influencer marketing: paying or partnering with social media personalities

[Page 80]

**Personal selling:**
- Most expensive per-contact method
- Most effective in building relationships and closing sales
- Brand equity impact through employee behaviors and service quality

**Direct marketing:**
- Email, direct mail, telemarketing
- Allows for personalization
- Measurable response

[Page 81]

### Brand Leveraging: Secondary Associations

A brand can borrow or leverage equity from other entities by linking itself to those entities. This creates **secondary brand associations** from:

1. **Companies** (through branding strategies)
2. **Countries or geographic areas** (through identification of product origin)
3. **Channels of distribution** (through channel strategy)
4. **Other brands** (through co-branding)
5. **Characters** (through licensing)
6. **Spokespeople** (through endorsements)
7. **Events** (through sponsorship)
8. **Third-party sources** (through awards or reviews)

[Page 82]

**Co-branding (also ingredient branding):**
Co-branding occurs when two or more existing brands are combined into a joint product or are marketed together in some fashion.

Advantages:
- Borrow equity from partner brand
- Access new market segments
- Create consumer appeal and differentiation

Disadvantages:
- Loss of control
- Potential negative association transfer
- Brand dilution risk

[Page 83]

**Examples of co-branding:**
- Intel Inside (ingredient branding) — PC manufacturers prominently display "Intel Inside" logo
- NutraSweet (sweetener ingredient in Coca-Cola, Pepsi)
- Gore-Tex fabric inside jackets (North Face, Patagonia garments feature Gore-Tex logo)
- Betty Crocker + Hershey chocolate chips in brownie mixes
- Pottery Barn + Sherwin Williams paint

[Page 84]

**Celebrity endorsement:**
- Leverages the celebrity's image, personality, and credibility
- Creates aspirational connections
- Most effective when celebrity genuinely uses and endorses the brand
- Risks: celebrity scandals (Tiger Woods, Lance Armstrong)

Key considerations:
- Match between celebrity and brand (brand-endorser fit)
- Celebrity's overall image and reputation
- Celebrity's appeal to target market

[Page 85]

**Country of origin effects:**
- Consumers associate certain product categories with specific countries
- German engineering (cars, appliances), French luxury (wine, fashion, perfume), Japanese technology (electronics, cars)
- "Made in" labels affect quality perceptions
- Brands can borrow credibility from country of origin

[Page 86]

**Licensing:**
- Brand name is licensed to another manufacturer for use on products
- Revenue stream for the licensor
- Risk of dilution if quality is not controlled
- Examples: designer fashion brands licensing sunglasses, fragrances, handbags

[Page 87]

### Measuring Brand Equity

**Brand equity measurement approaches:**

**Indirect approach (brand knowledge):**
- Brand audits (in-depth examination of the sources of brand equity)
- Brand tracking (routine collection of brand-related information)

**Direct approach (consumer response):**
- Conjoint analysis (measuring willingness to pay premium)
- Choice experiments
- Residual approach (what is left over after controlling for product characteristics)

[Page 88]

**The Brand Audit:**
A comprehensive examination of a brand involving both internal and external research.

Components:
1. **Brand inventory** — provides a current, comprehensive profile of all marketing programs (product, price, distribution, communications) for all products/services sold under the brand name
2. **Brand exploratory** — qualitative research into consumers to understand what they think and feel about the brand; what associations, beliefs, and feelings are linked to the brand

[Page 89]

**Brand Tracking:**
Continuous data collection to monitor the health of a brand over time. Typically collects:
- Brand awareness (aided and unaided)
- Brand image/associations
- Purchase intention
- Usage (current and lapsed users)
- Brand loyalty

[Page 90]

**Quantitative metrics:**
- Price premium: how much more consumers are willing to pay for the brand
- Market share
- Relative market share (vs. competitors)
- Revenue premium (brand revenue vs. private label revenue)

## Chapter 6: Integrating Marketing Communications to Build Brand Equity
**Source:** Strategic Brand Management, Keller | PDF Pages: ~90–107

[Page 91]

### Developing Integrated Marketing Communication Programs

**IMC definition:** A planning process designed to assure that all brand contacts received by a customer or prospect for a product, service, or organization are relevant to that person and consistent over time.

The challenge: Each communication vehicle has its own strengths and weaknesses.

[Page 92]

**Information processing model of communications:**

Exposure → Attention → Comprehension → Yielding → Intentions → Behavior

At each stage there is "leakage" — not everyone who sees an ad will pay attention, not everyone who pays attention will comprehend, not everyone who comprehends will yield, etc.

**Overcoming barriers:**
- Relevance: does the message speak to the consumer's needs and interests?
- Clarity: is the message easy to understand?
- Credibility: is the source trustworthy?
- Motivation: does the message motivate action?

[Page 93]

**Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM):**

Two routes to persuasion:
1. **Central route** — careful consideration of the actual merits of the information presented (for high-involvement products/decisions)
2. **Peripheral route** — use of cues not related to the quality of information (for low-involvement products; reliance on heuristics, source attractiveness, music)

Brand implications:
- For high-involvement categories: strong arguments needed
- For low-involvement categories: execution quality, spokesperson attractiveness, musical cues matter more

[Page 94]

### Role of Multiple Communications

Different communications can serve different roles:
- **Building** brand awareness and associations
- **Reinforcing** existing brand associations
- **Reminding** consumers of brand availability and benefits
- **Correcting** misconceptions or weak associations

**Synergy in communications:**
When multiple communication tools are used together, they can have a greater effect than the sum of their parts. The brand should be presented consistently across all touchpoints.

[Page 95]

**The advertising campaign:**
An integrated series of ads tied to a common theme or idea.

Key elements:
- Campaign concept (the central idea)
- Message strategy (what to communicate)
- Creative execution (how to communicate it)
- Media strategy (where, when, and how often to communicate)

[Page 96]

### Managing Brand Communications

**Brand communications guidelines:**
1. Be consistent in brand position and messaging
2. Mix communications tools strategically
3. Measure effectiveness rigorously
4. Integrate across all customer touchpoints
5. Build long-term equity while meeting short-term objectives

[Page 97]

**Television advertising:**

Television remains the most powerful mass communications medium for brand image building.

Key variables in TV ad effectiveness:
- Stopping power (attention-getting ability)
- Uniqueness (differentiation from competitive ads)
- Believability (credibility of the message)
- Relevance (does the consumer care?)
- Memory (will the consumer remember the brand after exposure?)

[Page 98]

**Print advertising:**

Print ads work differently from TV — the reader actively processes the information.

Key components:
- Headline (most read element)
- Visual (generates attention and conveys meaning)
- Body copy (provides detail for interested readers)
- Tagline (links back to brand positioning)

[Page 99]

**Radio advertising:**

Radio is the "theater of the mind" — listeners create their own visual imagery.

Advantages:
- Low cost
- High reach
- Geographic targeting
- Frequency building

Disadvantage:
- No visual component
- Low attention environment (background medium)

[Page 100]

**Out-of-home advertising:**

Billboards, transit ads, digital displays. Key characteristic: short exposure time forces brief, simple messages.

**Event marketing:**

Creates direct experiences with the brand. Events can be:
- Brand-owned events (Red Bull Air Race)
- Sponsored events (Coca-Cola sponsoring the Olympics)
- Experiential marketing activations

[Page 101]

### Online and Interactive Marketing Communications

**Digital marketing ecosystem:**
- Website (brand's owned media hub)
- Search engine marketing (SEM) — paid search, SEO
- Social media (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok)
- Display advertising
- Email marketing
- Mobile marketing

**POEM framework:**
- Paid media (advertising you pay for)
- Owned media (brand's own channels: website, social media pages, stores)
- Earned media (PR, word-of-mouth, organic social sharing)

[Page 102]

**Social media and brand equity:**

Social media creates new opportunities and risks:
- Consumers can co-create brand meaning
- Brand content can go viral at zero marginal cost
- Negative content can also spread rapidly
- Consumer-generated content (CGC) is often more trusted than brand-created content

[Page 103]

**Influencer marketing:**

Working with individuals who have large social media followings to endorse a brand.

Key considerations:
- Authenticity and fit with brand
- Reach (follower count) vs. engagement (how much followers interact)
- Micro-influencers (10,000–100,000 followers): often higher engagement, lower cost, more credibility in niche
- Macro-influencers (1M+ followers): broad reach but lower engagement rate

[Page 104]

### Measuring Communication Effectiveness

**Pre-testing (copy testing):**
- Focus groups
- Concept testing
- Theater testing (Ipsos ASI, Millward Brown)

**Post-testing (tracking):**
- Awareness (unaided and aided recall)
- Message recall/playback
- Brand linkage (can consumer correctly identify the brand in the ad?)
- Persuasion shift (change in purchase intent)
- Sales response analysis (econometrics, marketing mix modeling)

[Page 105]

**Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI):**

Increasingly, marketing departments are held accountable for ROI.

Methods:
- Marketing mix modeling (econometric analysis of marketing spending vs. sales)
- Attribution modeling (which touchpoints contributed to purchase)
- Customer lifetime value analysis

[Page 106]

### Key Takeaways: Chapters 1–6

1. **Brand equity is a differential effect** — brand knowledge causing different consumer response to marketing
2. **The CBBE pyramid** provides a framework for building strong brands from awareness to resonance
3. **Brand positioning** requires identifying target markets, competitive frames, POPs, and PODs
4. **Brand elements** (names, logos, slogans, packaging) must be memorable, meaningful, likable, transferable, adaptable, and protectable
5. **IMC** mixes multiple communication tools with consistent messaging to build equity efficiently
6. **Multiple touchpoints** (product, price, distribution, communications, secondary associations) all contribute to or detract from brand equity
