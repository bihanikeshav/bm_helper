Brand Value Proposition for Bank Customers in India 
 
Abstract 
 
Purpose - The study aims to relate the customer value proposition offered by a bank with its 
structure of ownership.  
Design/methodology/approach- The study adopted a combination of exploratory and 
descriptive approaches. The attitudes and opinions of bank customers were gauged through a 
survey. Based on existing literature, a pool of survey items was identified to measure the 
construct of value proposition. It was hypothesized that different types of banks in India are 
chosen for different customer benefits offered by them. The relationship between value 
proposition and its constituent variables – functional, emotional and self-expressive benefits – 
was analyzed using multiple regression. 
Findings – The results prove that while self-expressive benefits drive the choice of foreign 
banks, functional benefits are important for all types of banks.  
Research limitations – The research intends to study only the perceptions of customers having 
an account in Indian public sector banks, private sector banks or foreign banks. 
Practical implications – The study helps to relate the type of bank (public, private or foreign) a 
customer chooses, with the value proposition it offers. Using this study, banks can configure the 
value proposition that is appropriate for their target segments. 
Originality/value - The paper examines the value proposition offered by the three different 
types of banks (public, private and foreign) empirically. It links bank choice of the customer to 
the benefit assortment offered by different types of banks.  
Keywords: Brand value proposition, Functional benefit, emotional benefit, self-expressive 
benefits

Introduction 
 
In 2007 the world witnessed a debt crisis and several cases of bankruptcy among banking and 
financial institutions. This led to a recession in the big economies of the world such as those of 
the United States and Europe. Amidst this turmoil however, the Indian banking industry emerged 
relatively unscathed. While this demonstrates the resilience of Indian banking, there are 
challenges that lie ahead which merit further study of the customer value proposition forwarding 
by the Indian banking sector. 
  
In the banking sector of India, the service expectations of customers are rising because of 
growing competition. Information technology is empowering customers like never before. After 
1990 the entry of new foreign banks and private Indian banks exposed Indian customers to 
global service standards. Therefore today it is no longer sufficient for banks to merely offer a 
range of services. Indian banks need to develop a distinct brand identity and meaningful value 
propositions.  
 
The common definition of “value” relies on the price-quality ratio of a product or the difference 
between perceived benefits and perceived costs. A value proposition describes how a company’s 
offer differs from that of its competitors and explains why customers buy from the company 
(Lindic and Marques Da Silva, 2011). It is well known that customers seek benefits rather than 
product characteristics or features. Banks have to therefore clearly differentiate the benefits of 
their offerings vis-à-vis their competitors.

The main objective of this study is to identify and analyze the value proposition associated with 
the three types of banks (based on ownership structure) in India, namely the public sector, 
private sector and foreign banks. Further, it examines how different banking brands should 
address the challenge of developing a value proposition for their customers. 
 
Literature Review 
 
Overview of Indian Banking Industry   
 
There are three types of banks in India based on their structure of ownership. These are Public 
Sector Banks (PSBs), Indian Private Sector Banks (PVBs) and Foreign Banks (FBs). PSBs are 
those in which the Government owns a majority stake. On the other hand, in Indian PVBs, 
private capital, Indian and foreign, own the majority stake. FBs are owned by multinational 
entities headquartered outside India. FBs played a big role in Indian banking during colonial rule. 
However banking became Government driven after the nationalization of big Indian banks in 
1969. Nationalization forced banks to lend to non-profitable segments such as farmers and 
weaker sections (the so called priority sector). The role of FBs steadily diminished as regulation 
restricted their branch expansion. But this market structure could not sustain itself. Lack of 
banking sector profitability forced the Government to open the market to private participation in 
the 1990s.  
This led to the birth of several PVBs which are on the ascendant. By 2013 there were 7 new and 
13 old PVBs (RBI, 2013). Besides these today, there are 26 PSBs. The PSBs include the State 
Bank of India (SBI), its 5 associate banks and 20 nationalized banks. There are also 43 FBs. The 
new banks brought in a customer centered perspective and technology driven approach to 
banking. Due to the neck to neck competition, foreign banks were forced to lead technological

innovations in the banking sector, adding to competitive pressure (Gauba 2012, Goyal & Joshi 
2012). This led to a boom in retail banking in India, with more customers joining to avail the 
services on offer. 
Therefore, the future clearly points to intense competition. Banks will have to constantly ask 
themselves what they can do better than others to retain and expand their customer base. Each 
bank can therefore be considered as an available brand in the market for customers. Given the 
wide array of brands, which brand will customers choose? This depends on the value proposition 
offered by the respective brands.  
Ownership structure of banks 
 
There is limited literature on the relationship between the structure of ownership of a bank and 
its value proposition. However there is literature that relates ownership structure with customer 
satisfaction, which results from offering an appropriate value proposition (the link between 
customer satisfaction and value proposition is explained in the section on “brand value 
proposition” under the heading “customer perspective”). Despite this, all literature is in 
agreement that customer satisfaction holds the key to a bank’s success. Akdag and Zineldin’s 
(2011) research in Turkey stresses the importance of functional benefits for bank customers. 
Somerville (2000) points out that foreign and domestic competition are together pushing 
Canadian banks to adopt more customer friendly technologies and easy-to-use processes. But 
will domestic banks be able to offer the same benefits as foreign banks? Are the same benefits 
expected from both types of banks? Poddar et. al (2015) show that foreign bank employees in 
general are more favorably viewed by customers. This gives foreign banks more elbow room to 
deal with customer dissatisfaction. Qin and Liu’s study (2008) on Chinese bank customers also

confirms that foreign banks are generally at an advantage compared to domestic banks. Li’s 
(2008) paper on Chinese banking points to the inadequate skill levels of local banks in 
competing with multinational banks. Jenkins’s (2007) study on banking in North Cyprus shows 
that local banks were forced to introduce internet services to stop customers from migrating to 
foreign banks. While the customer seems to be at the forefront of all debate on bank performance 
what benefits does a customer specifically expect from a bank? Do they change with the 
ownership structure of the bank? Will domestic banks be able to cope with the sophisticated 
offerings of foreign banks? This line of questioning led to the literature on “value proposition”. 
Value proposition is the encapsulation of all the benefits a brand offers to its customer. It is the 
reason why a customer buys a product or a brand (Kotler et. al, 2013). A detailed on discussion 
value proposition and how it relates to the structure of ownership follows. 
 
Brand value proposition 
 
Though the customer is central to most discussions on value proposition, there are other 
stakeholder perspectives as well. Frow and Payne (2011) present six stakeholder groups that can 
be labelled as customers, referrals, suppliers, influencers (e.g. banks, Government, media etc.), 
prospective employees and current employees (more broadly the organization). Each of them 
views value proposition through a different lens. Two of the most important perspectives, 
namely the organizational and customer perspectives are described below.   
 
Organizational perspective - According to Lanning (2000), a value proposition is fundamentally 
about what is offered to the customer, but is also useful for the company. It provides a template 
to the organization in finding out what needs to be offered to the customer. Value proposition 
Downloaded by Indian Institute of Management at Bangalore At 04:27 10 January 2017 (PT)


[Page 7]
therefore informs how organizations balance their activities to address the concerns of customers 
while managing profitability (Barnes et al., 2009). The concept of value proposition as presented 
by Lanning and Michaels (1988) on the other hand, highlights the importance of an organization 
communicating the value of its offerings. This goes therefore beyond merely developing a value 
proposition. Knox and Maklan (1998) propose a Unique Organization Value Proposition 
(UOVP) to encapsulate the process-oriented approach which enables organizations to define and 
deliver customer value. UOVP integrates an organization’s value-adding processes through a 
combination of reputation, performance, product portfolio and a network of third-party 
relationships. This combination enables an organization to create profitable long term 
relationships, integrate and direct the organization towards customer value, lead unbeatable 
alliances and generate breakthrough innovation. Yi and Rui (2009) point out that companies do 
have good value propostions but they are only mentioned symbolically in propandistic materials, 
corporate reports or company reports. Corporates do not translate the value propostions into a 
corporate governance policy, nor do they develop an action plan on how to transfer the brand 
value to stakeholders. The development of a value proposition is in fact a strategic task, which is 
based on systematic analysis in order to fulfil the requirements relevant for success (Daun and 
Klinger, 2006). Therefore the organizational perspective, though concerned essentially with what 
the company should do, is also focused on what should be offered as value proposition to the 
customer. 
 
Customer perspective - Value is created when product/service attributes such as design, service 
or support match specific customer needs (Kambil et al., 1996). Chesbrough and Rosenbloom 
(2002) describe value proposition as a customer’s problem, the solution to it and value from the

[Page 8]
customer’s perspective. Anderson et al., (2006) contend that value proposition is closely 
connected with the values a company delivers to customers in order to satisfy their needs. 
However, the difficulties in delivering a value proposition that satisfies the customers are widely 
recognized.  According to Anderson et al. (2006, p. 2) “it is exceptionally difficult to find 
examples of value proposition that resonate with customers.” Their study reveals that most 
managers in Europe and the USA equate their list of benefits to their value proposition without 
much concern about customers and competitors. Companies usually think of value proposition in 
terms of what they offer their customers rather than what their customers truly value (Bower and 
Christensen, 1995; Christensen and Overdorf, 2000). Managers may consider advantages that 
offer the customer no real benefit or are common in the industry and therefore provide no 
differentiation factor (Kim and Mauborgne, 1999).  
 
So how should an organization avoid the pitfalls in the process of delivering a value proposition 
to customers? What should a value proposition offer specifically to the customer? Anderson et 
al. (2006) opine that value proposition should provide focused benefits that help solve target 
customers’ problems by being distinctive (i.e. superior to those of its competitors), measurable 
(i.e. based on tangible points of difference) and sustainable (i.e. valid for a certain time period). 
Gnecchi (2009) opines that the benefits that contribute to customer value proposition are of two 
types, economical and emotional. Rintamaki et al. (2007) list four types of needs namely 
economic (price oriented), functional (specifications), emotional (experiential requirements), and 
symbolic (self-expression needs) and emphasize delivering customer experience through 
addressing these needs. In a recent effort Blocker and Barris (2015), in their qualitative study,

A review of value proposition literature reveals that a majority of the researchers stressed the 
need for focusing on the benefits for customers. It can also be seen that the benefits offered by a 
value proposition can be summed up in 3 headings, namely functional, emotional and self-
expressive benefits as proposed by Aaker (1996). If these benefits can be made distinct from 
competition and relevant to the customer, they deliver a unique value proposition. (It may be 
noted here that economic benefits have been classified under functional benefits in this paper

Functional benefits 
 
Functional benefits are those benefits that appeal to rational motives (e.g. “this bank offers very 
attractive interest rates”, “it has a website with easy navigation”). In Maslow’s hierarchy of 
needs these could be compared to entry level “basic needs.” In the parlance of behavioral 
sciences they could be seen as “cognitive” sources of motivation for buying a brand. That is, they 
Downloaded by Indian Institute of Management at Bangalore At 04:27 10 January 2017 (PT)


[Page 10]
depend on a person’s ability to think. Functional benefits therefore appeal to the rational aspects 
of decision making. Thus functional benefits are the bare minimum a customer looks for in a 
brand. These benefits should therefore be complemented by non-functional benefits (emotional 
and self-expressive) because functional benefits are offered by every brand.  The three benefits 
together can therefore offer an abiding value proposition for the brand.  
 
Emotional benefits 
 
Emotional benefits appeal to what might be called the security needs of the customer (in 
Maslow’s hierarchy these would be safety needs). The customer buys a product or service if 
he/she is assured that it is dependable, that it will not cheat him/her (e.g. “this bank is 
trustworthy”, “Bank staff is true friends”). Emotional benefits are therefore “affective” sources 
of motivation. That is, they focus on “feelings.” Brand experience literature takes this line of 
thought further (Brakus et al., 2009). Ruth (2001) had concluded that emotional benefits and 
emotional knowledge have important implications for brand equity. Stayman et al. (1992) point 
out that the emotional benefit promised by a brand in its advertisements provides a standard by 
which the product is judged during trial or consumption. Agres et al. (1990) contends that the 
strongest brand identities have emotional benefits along with functional benefits. Thus the need 
for emotional benefits in brands is widely recognized in literature.

Besides the above there are self-expressive benefits. They appeal to ego needs of the customer 
(these correspond to “esteem needs” in Maslow’s hierarchy). They focus on the demonstrative 
impulse and pride of ownership of the customer on one hand and the badge value and “snob 
appeal” of the service on the other (e.g. “I would like everybody to know that I have an account 
in this bank”). Self-expressive impulse is what motivates a customer to buy a Ferrari car, a Vertu 
phone or a Rolex watch. In such brands functional benefits are taken for granted. They are 
purchased more because they admit the customer to an exclusive club whose membership is 
sought by everyone but only given to a select few. The purchase and use of brands is one way to 
fulfill this need for self-expression (Belk, 1988).  Consumers use brands to express and validate 
their identity (Aaker, 1996).

Six hypotheses have been developed based on the literature on banking and the objective 
conditions of banking in India.  
 
Hypotheses on Indian Public Sector Banks (PSBs) 
 
Close to two decades after independence, the Government pushed private banks to offer more 
credit to the so-called “priority sector” (RBI, 2014). Although socially desirable, these loans did 
not yield returns. The private sector (PVBs and FBs) was therefore understandably reluctant to 
lend to such customers (Aiyar, 2012). Finally the Government nationalized several big Indian 
banks in 1969 and 1980 to ensure channelization of funds to the credit starved. These banks 
became what are today commonly known as public sector banks (PSBs). Public sector banks 
rapidly expanded their branch network to reach unbanked customers (socially, economically 
weak sections) and geographies (rural areas).  
Several provisions were also made not just to offer reach but also ensure credit delivery to the 
deserving sections. Such measures enhanced the penetration of credit across the length and 
breadth of the country. This rapid growth however led to poor financial results i.e. low return on 
equity and shrinking net profit margins for PSBs (Gandhi, 2015). Observers believe that difficult 
as it might be, PSBs will be ultimately bailed out because they are backed by the Government 
(Larsen and Webb, 2014). 
 
According to The Economic Times (2012) consumer survey, the biggest bank in India, SBI 
(State Bank of India, which is a PSB) ranked much higher on “trust” than its private sector 
counterparts. Thus functional benefits (reach, funds earmarked for unbanked population) and

[Page 13]
emotional benefits (comfort and trust that the Government will not short change its citizens) are 
likely the prime reasons why customers leave money in PSBs for safekeeping.  
 
Hence the following hypotheses have been formulated. 
 
Hypothesis1: Functional benefits drive customers towards PSBs more than self-expressive 
benefits. 
 
Hypothesis 2: Emotional benefits drive customers towards PSBs more than self-expressive

Hypotheses on Foreign Banks (FBs) 
 
FBs traditionally focused on premium clientele in India. Bhattacharaya (1993) finds evidence for 
what he calls “cream skimming behavior” which essentially means targeting high net worth 
individuals (HNIs). Interestingly, despite relaxations in regulatory policies, FBs have not 
increased their geographical spread. This gives us a hint of the strategic objectives of the FBs 
(that of profitability potential taking precedence over market penetration). FB service charges are 
higher than that of Indian banks. This controlled access has been used by FBs to package their 
service as “aspirational”. The snob value of some services was augmented further by positioning 
the top end credit cards as “by invitation” only.  
 
It would however be a mistake to think that FBs merely offer “snob value” and nothing else. 
They provide several distinct functional benefits to their customers. FBs are pioneers in internet 
and mobile banking (though later the PVBs exploited these technologies more effectively). FBs

[Page 14]
even assign a specific individual to an HNI who prepares a lifetime financial game plan for 
him/her (The Economic Times, 2010). However, the demonstrative and self-expressive benefits 
that come with such an account hold the key to explaining the restricted FB footprint in India. 
Indians always had a fascination for foreign brands. The fascination for a premium foreign brand 
for its signaling value is an important reason why the Indian elite choose the FBs.   
  
On the emotional benefits though, FBs do not seem to score high. The global meltdown in 2008 
showed that FBs are fragile. The allegations of LIBOR rate rigging (Sheffield, 2015), 
suppressing information from regulatory authorities (Smith, 2013) and employees writing open 
letters to press on treating customers badly (Luyendijk, 2012) have further dented the reputation 
of FBs not just in India, but globally. The aspiring Indian customers (those who are “arriving” 
but not yet “arrived”) who were aware of global developments therefore, may not have found the 
FBs trustworthy despite their badge value. Because of this, the PSBs have gained market share 
for some time (Business Standard, 2010). According to The Economic Times (2012), no FB 
makes it to the list of 25 “most trusted service brands in India” pointing to the lack of confidence 
in them. By contrast, 11 Indian banking and financial services brands figure in the list. In short it 
is unlikely that Indian customers go to FBs for emotional benefits like trust.  
 
In view of the above the following hypotheses are formulated. 
 
Hypothesis 3: Self-expressive benefits drive customers towards FBs more than functional 
benefits.  
 
Hypothesis 4: Self-expressive benefits drive customers towards FBs more than emotional 
benefits.  
 
Hypotheses on Indian Private Sector Banks (PVBs)

The Indian private sector banks are a product of economic liberalization in the early 1990s. 
Today several PVBs are giving stiff competition to both PSBs and FBs. This is because PVBs 
had started rapidly catching up with FBs in service (Sanyal and Shankar, 2008). PVBs gave all 
the facilities that FBs were offering to a much wider audience. In fact, their target segment was 
initially predominantly the upper middle class and middle class of urban India. This segment was 
below the elite class sought by FBs and above the one sought by the PSBs.  
 
Now PVBs are catching up with PSBs as well in reach. They are rapidly expanding their 
geographical spread by opening new branches and aggressive acquisitions. They claim to have 
achieved most of the priority sector targets given by the Government. In short PVBs are 
fundamentally delivering “functional” benefits. 
 
It is unlikely that customers are heading to PVBs for self-expressive benefits. There was an 
attempt to cater to self-expressive benefits initially. But the real elite clientele were already with 
FBs. The PVB customer profile was therefore not “the arrived” but “the arriving”, as mentioned 
before. Customers who are more elite than the PSB customers but are more driven by functional 
benefits rather than self-expressive benefits are more likely to opt for PVBs. 
However, a PVB is unlikely to give the emotional comfort that a PSB gives. This is, as 
mentioned, because the Government is seen as the guarantor in case of PSBs.

Further, in the survey (The Economic Times, 2012) of service brands, 4 PSBs and LIC were 
ahead of PVBs in bank brand ratings on “trust”. Therefore it is unlikely that customers are going 
to PVBs for emotional reasons like trust and confidence. They are going to them because the 
PVBs have been providing functional benefits besides steadily ramping up their geographical 
spread. Hence the following hypotheses are proposed. 
 
Hypothesis 5: Functional benefits drive customers towards Indian PVBs more than emotional 
benefits.  
 
Hypothesis 6: Functional benefits drive customers towards Indian PVBs more than self-

Methodology 
Based on literature review, the construct of value proposition and its constituent variables were 
identified. The study used the mixed method wherein both exploratory as well as descriptive 
approach was used.  Exploratory research was used to identify the variables and define the 
constructs. Descriptive research was undertaken to determine the characteristics of the bank 
customers and to measure the variables like functional, emotional and self-expressive benefits. 
In order to study the influence of age, occupation, income etc. on certain behavioral patterns of 
bank customers, descriptive research was chosen. Under descriptive studies cross-sectional study 
was undertaken. Detailed information from sample was obtained through the survey method. The 
study used deductive approach wherein based on the existing literature and theory, hypotheses

were developed. Further the hypotheses were tested empirically. Required additions and 
modifications to the existing theory were made in the light of the findings. 
Sources of data 
The study involved collecting both primary and secondary data. Secondary data was drawn from 
Reserve Bank of India publications and the sites of different banks. Various journal and 
newspaper articles have been referenced for understanding the background of the study and for 
constructing the hypotheses. Primary data was obtained through a structured questionnaire. The 
target population was the customers from different types of Banks. The approach for the research 
was therefore quantitative. 
Sampling 
The population under the study involved the customers of the three types of banks. Data was 
collected from 237 respondents. The study was conducted in Bangalore, a major metropolitan 
city in the southern region of India, which has a large and diverse population from different parts 
of the country. Bangalore’s population has a representative character. Just 27 percent of its 
population of over 4.4 million comprises local Kannadigas: the majority are from other states. 
With its cosmopolitan make-up, the city is a microcosm of the national market according to 
Sridhan, Ogilvy Benson & Mather (OBM) Vice-President (Pratap, 1988). Bangalore scores over 
cities like Chandigarh, Ahmedabad, Madras and even Bombay, on this count. The crowd in the 
city is more cosmopolitan when compared with Mumbai or Delhi and the market size is neither 
too small nor too large (Bhattacharya, 2013). 
Multi stage sampling was undertaken which involved the selection of the customers of the bank 
in more than one stage. A sample of 237 bank customers from the city of Bangalore had to be

Research tool 
 
A survey design was used for the research. It provided a quantitative description of attitudes and 
opinions of bank customers. A structured data collection procedure was followed where a formal 
questionnaire was prepared. On the basis of the items used in the literature and the definition 
established in the study, a pool of measures was identified. The questionnaire consists of two 
different parts. Part I included the questions on general information about the respondents. This 
part collects the details on demographic factors like gender, age, education, marital status, type 
of profession, income level.  Part II consists of questions specific to different value propositions. 
Questions in part II are multiple choice questions. The items were measured using a seven point 
Likert scale anchored from 1(strongly disagree) to 7 (strongly agree). Eight items were designed 
to measure self-expressive benefits, seven items for emotional benefits and twelve items for 
functional benefits.

Discussion and implications 
 
Despite considerable interest in the concept of branding banks, there have been few attempts to 
relate their brand value propositions to their customers. Our study points out that the customers 
of different types of banks in India seek different benefits.  Based on this, banks can build 
distinct brand identities that suit their respective structures and audience.  The findings of the 
study therefore have significant implications for banks. According to Green (2003) determining 
how your customers feel when they do business with you can differentiate you in the market 
place.  
 
FBs stood high on both the functional and self-expressive benefits, indicating a strong connect 
between them and their customers. Indian PSBs were seen as laggards. They delivered less than 
PVBs and FBs on functional benefits. Functional benefits are basic. They delivered less than FBs 
on self-expressive benefits. On emotional benefits there is no significant difference between the 
three types of banks. Thus in none of the 3 categories of benefits do they stand out with respect 
to competition. Even though they displayed marginally high emotional benefits, they could not 
leverage on them because of their inability to deliver functional benefits which are seen as 
Downloaded by Indian Institute of Management at Bangalore At 04:27 10 January 2017 (PT)


[Page 27]
“must-have”. As mentioned by Bower and Christensen (1995) and Christensen and Overdorf 
(2000), banks had thought of value proposition in terms of what they offered to the customers 
rather than what customers value. For a long time (between 1947 and 1990) this was possible in 
India because customers had no choice. But post 1990, economic liberalization forced PSBs to 
ponder over their eroding customer base. The answer lies in their inability to provide functional 
benefits to begin with, for their emotional connect was not strong enough to keep their 
customers. 
  
By contrast, customers clearly looked for self-expressive benefits in a FB. As explained by 
Anderson et al. (2006) their value proposition has provided focused and distinct benefits, 
relevant to their target customers. FBs in India are preferred overridingly for self-expressive 
benefits. The surprise finding however, was that emotional benefits do not seem to matter in 
brand choice (across the 3 types of banks). Though PSBs do offer more emotional benefits than 
FBs and PVBs, analysis shows that customers do not find the difference significantly strong to 
move to PSBs.  
 
This finding has important implications for PSBs. The factors that PSBs consider as advantages 
are common in the industry and offer the customer no incremental benefit. Therefore they 
provide no differentiation (Anderson et al., 2006 and Kim and Mauborgne, 1999). For instance 
half of India’s population is less than 25 years old. They are more comfortable transacting online 
or on mobile devices. But the study clearly shows that the more tech savvy customers are 
moving to FBs. PSBs have a wider branch network. But the advantage arising out of this will 
recede as more and more of India gets digitally connected. Similarly, PVBs are aggressively 
Downloaded by Indian Institute of Management at Bangalore At 04:27 10 January 2017 (PT)


[Page 28]
expanding their branch network as mentioned. Thus PSBs could suffer from a double 
disadvantage. In the future there might be steady a migration from PSBs to non-PSBs because 
PSBs lag in delivering even functional benefits. New account holders might opt for 
foreign/private banks. The results confirm the findings of Thierno et al. (2005) that banks with 
minority domestic private ownership and foreign ownership perform better than state-owned 
banks. The study shows how the structure of ownership influences the benefits provided by the 
banks and in turn how customers evaluate these offerings.  
 
The study also points out to what specifically can be done by each type of bank to retain or gain 
customers. Self-expressive benefits mark the difference between FBs and the other two types of 
banks. However within self-expressive benefits the more elite PSB customers might switch to 
FBs for “feel good” benefits (company of the elite, sophistication). The “arriving” PVB 
customers may want to switch for “networking” (with influential people) benefits, besides “feel 
good” benefits. By contrast, functional benefits are the key to the choice of PVBs. The operative 
words should be “prompt”, “ease” and “convenience.” Thus if PSBs are ever to rival PVBs it 
will be because they surpass the latter on these benefits. However that is not enough. PSBs have 
to become financial supermarkets (one stop shops) as well as tech savvy, to rival PVBs. Thus 
this study clearly outlines the benefits to be provided by each type of bank and adds significantly 
to the literature on brand value propositions for banking services.  
Limitations and future research directions 
The research has a few limitations. It studies only the perceptions of customers having an 
account in PVBs, PSBs or FBs. Perceptions of non-customers can be an interesting supplement 
to this study. Also the perceptions and expectations of customers are limited only to the 
Downloaded by Indian Institute of Management at Bangalore At 04:27 10 January 2017 (PT)


[Page 29]
particular time period of study. A longitudinal study might throw up a different set of results. 
The perceptions about different benefits offered by banks are also constrained by geography, 
though the findings are likely to be representative of urban India. Rural India might however 
reveal a different pattern of choice, given the fact that PVBs are now expanding rapidly there. 
The results of a wider geographical, longitudinal and customer based survey might give deeper 
insights into how customers choose bank brands.
