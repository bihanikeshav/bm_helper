## Services Branding Framework — Expanded

This document presents a comprehensive framework in the form of a large table that cross-references the **5 elements of service branding** (drawn from the Brand Identity Planning Model adapted for services) with the **7Ps of services marketing** integrated into those 5 elements, and then maps how each element behaves across three categories of goods/services: **Search Good**, **Experience Good**, and **Credence Good**.

### Framework Description

The table has 5 columns:
1. **5 elements of service branding** (left-most column, with merged cells spanning multiple rows)
2. **7Ps integrated into the 5 elements** (second column)
3. **Search Good** (third column)
4. **Experience Good** (fourth column)
5. **Credence Good** (fifth column)

The three good types are defined as follows:
- **Search Good**: Quality can be assessed before purchase (e.g., a hardware store — you can examine the product)
- **Experience Good**: Quality can only be assessed after or during consumption (e.g., a restaurant, a movie)
- **Credence Good**: Quality cannot be easily assessed even after consumption; requires trust in the provider (e.g., a doctor, a lawyer, a financial advisor)

### Interpretive Notes on the Framework

**Reading the framework across rows:**

- **Brand as product — Product**: For a search good, the product should "never fail" (reliability is the key since customers chose it based on pre-evaluated criteria). For an experience good, it should be "convenient, pleasant" (since quality is evaluated during use). For a credence good, it should be "enriching, clarifying" (since the customer cannot evaluate the quality — the service must educate and add value).

- **Brand as product — Physical evidence**: For search goods, physically demonstrate the product's attributes. For experience goods, create delight through physical environment. For credence goods, use physical evidence to build confidence (e.g., a doctor's diplomas on the wall, a lawyer's tidy office).

- **Brand as product — Price**: Search good = going rate (customer comparison shops). Experience good = value for money (assessed retrospectively). Credence good = perceived value (customer has no reference point; perception of value is constructed).

- **Brand as product — Place**: Search good = close to the customer (accessibility is a competitive factor). Experience good = reasonable distance (customer will travel if the experience is worth it). Credence good = distance not an issue (for complex credence services like brain surgery, patients travel globally).

- **Brand as product — Promotion**: Search good = provide information (factual). Experience good = word-of-mouth (peer endorsement builds confidence). Credence good = general word-of-mouth (broader reputation signals trustworthiness).

- **Brand as process**: Search good = standardized with some customization. Experience good = standardized with more customization. Credence good = standardized with lot more customization. The logic: the harder quality is to evaluate, the more the customer expects the process to be tailored to their specific situation.

- **Brand as organization — People**: Search good = basic skills. Experience good = more evolved skill set. Credence good = complex skill set. Credence goods require the highest expertise since the customer depends entirely on the provider's competence.

- **Brand as person**: Search good = problem solver (functional role). Experience good = entertainer (relational/hedonic role). Credence good = advisor (consultative, trusted role).

- **Brand as symbol (should reflect)**: Search good = useful (functional symbolism). Experience good = lively (expressive/energetic symbolism). Credence good = wise (authoritative, trustworthy symbolism).

### Summary Insight

The framework reveals that as we move from Search to Experience to Credence goods, the nature of service branding shifts from functional performance and information provision towards emotional engagement and trust-building. For credence goods especially, the Brand as Person (as advisor) and Brand as Symbol (as wise) dimensions become critical because the customer cannot rely on direct evaluation and must instead rely on reputation, relationships, and signals of expertise.
