INTRODUCTION 
St Michael is "the UK's bestselling brand". 
Marks & Spencer supply 16 per cent of UK 
clothing and 5 per cent of UK food[l]. 
Statistics and claims such as these reflect 
fundamental change in the structure of 
product marketing and in the control over 
the distribution of consumer products. Yet is 
St Michael a "brand" in the same way as 
Yorkie, Persil, Sierra, Coca-Cola? If the St 
Michael name has a central value to both 
Marks & Spencer and the 14 million 
customers they serve each week, what is the 
value of the Marks & Spencer name? Indeed 
can both St Michael and Marks & Spencer 
be brand names and if so do they represent 
different values to the shopper or to a wider 
public? The business press see retailers as 
brands [2,3], as do retailers. Laura Ashley's 
Chief Executive referred to a "portfolio of 
retail brands, of which Laura Ashley will 
remain pre-eminent", in describing the future 
direction for his business[4]. W.H. Smith 
purchased the record chain of Our Price in 
1986 and justified the premium they paid 
because of the "strength of the brand name", 
even though Our Price did not retail own-
label products. According to one director of 
Tesco "the shop itself, its location, its 
atmosphere, the service it offers and the 
range of goods and prices can become the 
Brand" [5]. This view of a number of 
interacting factors contributing to a single 
retail brand is widely held[6]. 
Jones[7] quotes Kaldor's[8] view that 
product branding by manufacturers and its 
associated advertising represented a way 
manufacturers could speak over the heads of

to achieve channel control (a concept 
ascribed elsewhere by Murphy[9] to no less

that branding is a form of non-price 
competition in oligopolistic markets. Both 
the concepts of control and non-price 
competition under oligopoly are relevant 
today in discussing the reasons why branding 
by retailers should be an issue, as many retail 
sectors are potentially oligopolistic in nature. 
The more obvious threat to manufacturer 
brands is that retailers' own-label products 
will become, or are already, own-brands. By 
1991 Euromonitor were recording a 
penetration level of 32 per cent across all 
British retail sectors for own-label 
merchandise, the highest level for any 
European country. Even in the highly 
branded area of packed groceries AGB/TCA 
were measuring the market share of own-
label at nearly 30 per cent by 1988, up seven 
percentage points in a decade. Davies[10] 
correlated the growth in own-label with the 
increase in retail concentration, measured as 
the market share of multiples in grocery 
retailing. As concentration is still increasing 
in most retail sectors, the inference is that 
own-label will continue to grow both in 
Britain and elsewhere. 
Important as the growth of own-label sales 
has been, the change in positioning of own-
label products is probably more significant. 
Retailer own-label has traditionally been low 
price and often low quality merchandise, the 
exemplars of such an approach being the 
utilitarian "generics" in the grocery sector 
with their simple packaging. The trend is 
now for better quality, higher priced 
merchandise the quality of which is 
commonly supported by a strong technical

Retailer involvement in technology could be a 
key element in the transition that has taken

retailed by those companies which do not 
own their own manufacturing capacity. 
Vertically integrated companies which own 
both production capacity and retail outlets, 
such as Jaeger, British Shoe, Boots and MFI, 
are also well placed to manage the quality of 
the products they sell under their own names 
or names they control. A retailer selling own-

department would find it more difficult to 
ensure quality, but can such retailers still 
claim to be brands because of their name 
above the door? One requirement for this 
article is to propose a framework that can be 
used to discriminate between what is merely 
a name above the shop or a convenient label 
and what is a genuine brand; when, in effect, 
can an own-label be said to be an own 
brand? 
WHAT IS A BRAND? 
The concept of a brand has traditionally 
been associated with a brand name. Gardner 
and Levy[12] in their seminal article argue 
that a brand name is more than just a "label 
employed to differentiate" and see it as "a 
complex symbol that represents a variety of 
ideas and attributes". This complexity is seen 
as synonymous with the concept of a 
personality, an analogy repeated by King[13], 
who suggested that even a product such as 
toilet tissue could be made into a brand with 
its own personality. King distinguishes 
between the product and the brand, "seeing 
the brand as distinct from the product, and 
with both capable of undergoing change 
independently of one another. 
Jones [7] sees the value of a brand to the 
customer/consumer as a "guarantee of 
homogeneity and quality", arguing that the 
purchaser receives not only the functional 
benefits of a product but also other benefits, 
that are "substantially psychic". These 
benefits are "added values", stemming not 
only from the customer's experience of the 
product, but also from its presentation and 
associations. Such benefits may include the 
feeling that the customer is "doing the right 
thing" by buying the product, particularly in 
the eyes of one's peers[14]. Kim[15] sees a 
brand as being totally concerned with the 
intangible, a "mental construct" that is 
"disembodied, abstract, ephemeral", existing 
only in the imagination of the consumer, the 
process of branding being the attribution of 
social and symbolic meaning to a product. A 
brand's value, according to Kim, can be 
measured in terms of extensiveness (the 
number of people who have feelings about 
the brand), comprehensiveness (how 
generalizable these feelings are) and 
intensiveness (the strength of feeling evoked 
by the brand). 
Attempts have been made to understand 
what psychological needs the non-functional 
appeal of a brand fulfils. Park et al.[16] 
recognize only two types of need in addition 
to function, symbolic needs and experiential 
needs. They offer cars as an example of 
symbolic brands and food as an example of 
the experiential, arguing that the first is more 
associated with ego and self-image needs and 
the second with the need for variety or 
sensory pleasure. However, most 
contributions to defining why branding 
works as a marketing concept are less useful 
in differentiating between things that are 
brands and things that are not. 
One short definition of a brand is offered 
by Baker [17], who sees the brand as having 
"a set of characteristics which clearly and 
readily differentiates it from all other 
products". Two keywords can be identified in 
similar definitions of the term brand: 
"differentiation"[5,14,18,19,20], and "quality" 
or "quality guarantee"[5,7,19,21,22]. These 
two attributes of a successful brand have 
benefits for both supplier and consumer. 
Through differentiation the supplier is able 
to claim a unique offer that the consumer 
would need to consider. Through the quality 
guarantee the supplier is able to assure the 
consumer about his/her purchase. The 
consumer rewards the supplier for providing 
something distinctive and of guaranteed 
quality by purchasing and often by paying 
more than might be the case for an 
unbranded product. These two attributes of a 
brand seem to be important; differentiation 
and the ability to command a premium 
because of perceived quality and possibly 
uniqueness. 
CAN SERVICES BE BRANDS? 
Thus far the word "product" has been used 
to represent what is being purchased with the 
possible implication that branding is 
applicable mainly to tangible purchases 
rather than services which retail outlets 
represent, but services can also be branded.

Murphy[5] differentiates between branded 
intangible service products, such as financial 
products, and branded services such as 
retailers. Berry et al. [23] argue that a service 
brand is more akin to a corporate brand 
name, citing as examples Avis, Federal 
Express and Holiday Inn, who are "overall 
brand images", each marketing a range of 
offers that are themselves unbranded. 
However, there have been recent attempts in 
services marketing to create individual service 
brands underneath the corporate brand[24]. 
Midland Bank, who brand themselves as "the 
listening bank", marketed different accounts 
such as Vector. Legal and General, a well 
known insurance brand, marketed individual 
brands such as its Golden Years pension 
plan. The Economist[2] associated the notion 
of a "branded experience" with a number of 
service companies including retailers. Onkvist 
and Shaw[25] appraise the branding of 
services in exactly the same way as is done 
elsewhere for tangible products, arguing that 
branding promotes differentiation and assures 
the buyer of uniform service quality. This 
notion of uniform quality in service branding 
is repeated by Kotler[20], who argues that 
people might patronize a fast food outlet 
such as McDonald's not so much because 
they like the product but because of 
McDonald's ability to sustain a uniform level 
of service. 
THE VALUE OF BRANDING TO THE 
SELLER 
From the discussion thus far branding has a 
number of advantages to the supplier. The 
two most notable are as: 
(1) A means of differentiation that provides 
the basis for non-price competition. 
(2) A means of securing a customer franchise 
by establishing a reputation for reliable 
quality. 
These two advantages can be used in turn to 
achieve profitable control over the 
distribution chain by either retailer or 
manufacturer, but two further potential 
benefits can be obtained by successful 
branding. The first is the opportunity to 
extend the branch franchise to other 
products. An established brand name can be 
used to support a minor product that might 
not be able to generate the sales volume 
necessary to sustain the level of promotion 
needed to build a separate brand. Or this 
same process of "brand extension" can be 
used to counter the ever-increasing cost of 
launching any new product. After a while the 
umbrella brand might be removed, once the 
new product or service can stand on its own. 
It is not surprising to see a great deal of 
interest in this process of brand extension (or 
umbrella branding or brand leverage, as it is 
also called) in American literature, given the 
relatively high volume of advertising in that 
country. Tauber[26] puts the case for 
umbrella branding by arguing the value of 
"cashing in on the equity" of an established 
brand name. Sullivan [27] accepts the short-
term benefit in matters such as product 
launches but points to the danger of spill-
over from negative associations with the 
umbrella brand in the longer term. Roberts et 
al.[28] suggest that there is no one answer to 
the question whether to use what they call 
family or individual brand names. 
The issue that brands have some potential 
value, other than in the marketing of the 
product with which they were originally 
associated is, however, far wider than 
whether the same name or imagery can be 
used on another product. One further 
advantage of successful branding is that 
brands have an asset value to their owners as 
part of that company's stock of goodwill. 
Whether the worth of the company's brand 
portfolio should be represented in the annual 
report and accounts and, if so, how a 
realistic value should be calculated is a 
source of much current discussion. 
There are many aspects to this often 
controversial debate that it would be 
distracting to record in this article but some 
principles can be useful in deciding what is a 
brand and what is not. Srikanthan et al. [29] 
make the point that companies invest in 
developing brands in the same way as they 
might invest in land and machinery. Tweedie 
and Blanchet[30] point to the need to reflect 
the value, not just the cost, of a business in 
the balance-sheet. However, Company 
Reporting[31] found that only ten of their 
sample of 215 companies capitalized brands 
in their balance-sheet. Bains[32] cites the 
difference in the way goodwill, including the 
value of any brands acquired in, say, a 
takeover, are treated for accounting purposes 
around the world. In the USA goodwill is 
capitalized and written off over up to 40 
years, while in Japan the write-off period is 
five years.

the balance-sheet is becoming more common

TESTS FOR THE PRESENCE OF 
BRANDING 
Deciding whether or not a product or service 
has been successfully branded is clearly not 
so simple as using litmus to test for acidity, 
but the foregoing analysis of the literature on 
branding suggests four "tests" for a brand 
(see Figure 1). 
If a retailer's name either on the shop or 
on the product can pass these four tests of 
differentiation, separate existence, psychic 
value and premium price, then retailers may 
indeed be classified as brands. If not, the 
retailer's name is no more than a convenient 
label. Much depends on the naming strategy 
used by the company as to whether the name 
is intended to be more than a label. 
RETAILERS' NAMING STRATEGIES 
Retailers such as Marks & Spencer could be 
regarded as brands in their own right, as 
service brands, or the branding effect could 
be associated solely with own-label/own-
brand products, in this instance, St Michael 
products. Marks & Spencer uses the St 
Michael name on most, if not all, of its 
products. The name is particularly noticeable 
on food but less so on clothing, where the 
Marks & Spencer name is often just as 
prominent. All Marks & Spencer outlets in 
Europe have the company name over the 
front of the shop, irrespective of its size, and 
the product range on offer. St Michael 
products are sold elsewhere in Europe and 
throughout the world by other retailers. The 
company trades less successfully in Canada 
under the Marks & Spencer name and also 
under different store names. The acquisition 
of Brooks Brothers and Kings Supermarkets 
gave the company two further potential 
brand names. Corporately the company is 
known as Marks & Spencer. This level of 
complexity is not unusual in retailing, indeed 
Marks & Spencer is a relatively simple case 
example when it comes to the use of names. 
One of its British chain-store competitors 
is BhS, renamed from British Home Stores.

Names such as Next, Principles and Dash 
would seem to have an advantage in 
portraying an image and it is interesting to 
note how many fashion retailers' names have 
been both invented and applied to both store 
name and merchandise. 
The decision to use the store name on the 
product is not taken lightly. Gateway chose 
not to use its own name on a range of own-
label products and instead developed a range 
of product names, because it believed the 
Gateway name was "not strong enough"[36]. 
Randall[37] identifies no less than five types 
of retailers' brand names used on products: 
store names; retailer controlled names (such 
as St Michael); designer labels; licensed 
names and generics (often unnamed). Only 
the first two are likely to pass all four tests 
listed earlier and Randall's 
list excludes at least two other types of 
retailer names. For instance many retail 
conglomerates use a separate name for each 
retail chain in the group but choose one 
name as a corporate label. In some instances 
this is the same as one of the retail chains, 
as with Burton, Boots and Ratners, often 
because of the name of the original holding 
company. Others such as Sears (which owns 
British Shoe, Selfridges, Homes, Foster, 
Adams and many more), Storehouse (which 
owns BhS, Mothercare, Richards and Heals) 
and Argyll (which owns Safeway, Presto and 
Lo-Cost) use a different name to represent 
themselves to the financial world. 
At the other extreme many retailers use 
names in-store on their own products that 
appear to be no more than range names used 
for the convenience of the retailer as much as 
to brand an item. At the time of writing, one 
name, Classics, was being used to label men's 
wear in Burton but also in its competitors, 
Fosters, and in Debenhams, a part of the 
Burton Group, for a wide range of 
merchandise. Each organization used a 
different typeface for the name, but this did 
not appear to achieve differentiation. 
DIFFERENTIATION 
Names such as Classics are difficult to 
register as trade marks, as they can be 
regarded as words in common usage. Often 
only the design associated with such a name 
is potentially registrable, making such names 
open to duplication and limiting their value 
to the user. The use of such names is 
therefore unlikely to guarantee differentiation 
and as such would not pass the first and 
arguably most important of the four tests. 
The use of "range" names is common 
amongst retailers of own-label products and 
most noticeable in the fashion sector. Such a 
policy is unlikely to confer any of the 
benefits of branding. 
Other retailers have chosen to devise or 
purchase names that are registrable. One such 
example is the electrical retailer, Dixons. 
Many commodity products such as blank 
tapes are labelled with the Dixons name, but 
the Matsui name is used on hi-fi, radios, 
television, video recorders, microwaves, 
refrigerators and telephones. The Miranda 
name is used for cameras and binoculars and 
Saisho for a similar range to Matsui. In 
1988/89 such products accounted for 26.7 per 
cent of group turnover[38]. Matsui- and 
Saisho-branded goods appeared in both 
Dixons and Currys and the use of the 
various names is evidence of differentiation, 
as is the continued use by Dixons of both 
their own and the Currys' name. In this 
instance the two retailers sell a markedly 
different range of merchandise, albeit all 
being electrical goods.

targeting different customer groups with

each very different in their targeting. Argyll's

belonging to the same parent company but 
selling similar merchandise imply that 
shoppers do differentiate between such

pass the test of differentiation, the presence 
of range names casts doubt over the 
automatic value of a separate name for the 
physical product. Where the product name is 
the same as the retailer name any 
differentiation by the retailer is likely to carry 
over to the product. 
Manufacturer/retailers such as Boots seem 
better placed to develop separate brand 
names for products. Boots' 1989 annual 
report listed brands such as Nurofen, 
Strepsils, Optrex, Sweetex, Farley and 
Ostermilk as part of an extensive portfolio. 
Such products are even sold by other 
retailers, the No. 7 range of cosmetics being 
a leading brand in Finland, even though it is 
not sold in other British outlets. Since 
acquiring the manufacturers Hygena and 
Schreiber, retailer MFI has moved away 
from a policy based on range names such as 
Rio, Maria and Laura, to relying upon 
Hygena, Schreiber and other names such as 
Ashton Dean and Greaves and Thomas. 
Research evidence on the purchasing 
behaviour of shoppers towards own-label 
compared with manufacturers' brands 
suggests that both are treated very much in 
the same way[40,41]. Many retailer-controlled 
names are claimed to be brand leaders, St 
Michael in ladies' underwear and men's 
socks, Hygena in kitchens, Halford's Apollo 
brand in bikes. Just because the retailer 
controls the brand name is no reason to 
suspect that the product concerned cannot be 
a brand. The fundamental issue is one of 
control, as, if the name cannot be registered, 
then the retailer cannot guarantee 
differentiation. Even control is not always 
enough. Many own-label products are blatant 
copies of existing manufacturers' lines, 
infringing the spirit, if not the substance, of 
sanctions against passing off, the wrong 
committed by presenting your goods as if 
they were those of another. The penetration 
of own-label in the grocery sector is 
frequently highest among products where the 
technology needed to produce them is lowest. 
While such products may achieve 
differentiation, this on its own may not be 
enough to justify the status of being a brand. 
PREMIUM PRICE 
Evidence of the use of premium pricing for 
own-label products is less apparent. Much 
own-label is sold on the basis of being 
similar to existing brands but cheaper. Marks 
& Spencer is traditionally far from being the 
cheapest source of groceries[42]. Fashion 
retailers such as Benetton, Dash, Next and 
Jaeger do not appear to sell on price. The 
price points at Laura Ashley for example 
imply a 20 per cent premium over the market 
average[4]. 
There is some anecdotal evidence to 
support the view that the shop name, rather 
than the product name, is the source of any 
premium. Name changes, such as Hepworth 
to Next, Presto to Safeway, Chelsea Girl and 
Concept Man to River Island, have all been 
claimed to produce significant increases in 
sales and profit per square foot. However, 
such store name changes often involve 
product name changes as well. 
There is one significant barrier to arguing 
that retail outlets can be generally regarded 
as brands on the basis of premium pricing.

to charge a premium price? 
SEPARATE EXISTENCE 
Retail names can be transferred from the 
shop to the product. The same retailer name 
can be used above outlets selling different 
products. Boots the Chemist operates a chain 
of opticians|43| and W.H. Smith the stationer 
operates a chain of travel agencies. Boots, 
W.H. Smith, Marks & Spencer and others 
have transferred their retail names overseas, 
although with mixed success in each case. 
Nevertheless the possession of retail names 
that can transfer internationally is valued[44]. 
The Wallis and Miss Selfridge names owned 
by Sears have been used for retail outlets, 
concessions and mail order catalogues. 
Retailers such as Harrods have lent or 
rather licensed their names for use on other 
"products". Rover cars negotiated 
unsuccessfully to use the Next name but the 
retailer names of Principles and Dash have 
both appeared on cars. Dixons' interim 
statement in 1990 included as an Appendix a 
letter from consultancy Interbrand, placing a 
valuation on the retailer's own-label names of 
£47 million. While Dixons chose not to 
include the valuation in its accounts and the 
exercise could have had more to do with a 
potential takeover from Kingfisher, the 
valuation implies that the use of the name 
could have been sold. 
Perhaps the most convincing evidence of 
the potential separate existence for a retail 
brand is the success of franchising. The term 
is often taken to imply a licence purchased to 
allow one person to use the format and name 
belonging to someone else. Retail examples 
include Bodyshop, the Late Late Supershop, 
Tie Rack, many petrol stations and many 
fast-food operations. But the franchising of a 
retail format is a wider concept that 
subsumes voluntary associations such as Spar 
and even Interflora. Most car retailing is still 
performed from franchised dealerships where 
the manufacturer's brand dominates the retail 
fascia. The product brand name has been 
used almost as an umbrella brand name. The 
same could be said of the use of the Royal 
Insurance name on the many estate agencies 
they own. The name of the original business 
is retained with the Royal name and logo 
alongside. 
The transferability of a retail brand name 
may depend upon the original imagery of the 
outlet or product. Ryan[14] doubts whether 
the attributes associated with Bodyshop for 
example would be relevant to someone 
seeking to buy furniture. Yet the potential for 
transfer can also be surprisingly wide. Marks 
& Spencer now sells financial services and 
the Laura Ashley name adorns a number of 
tea shops in Japan. 
□ 
THE TRUE BRAND HAS 
TO ACHIEVE ITS 
OWN PERSONALITY 
□ 
In summary, retailer names appear to be 
readily transferable. Indeed it is surprising 
that more retailers have not followed the 
example of Boots cited earlier and promoted 
the sale of its own products via other retail 
outlets. What is wrong with the sale of, for 
example, Marks & Spencer food products in 
a well-run petrol station or to see a Next suit 
sold in a branch of Debenhams! 
PSYCHIC VALUE 
Ryanfl4| agreed that retail brands should 
"rise above the detail" of the shop and the 
products sold, as the latter represent ways of 
forming the brand rather than being the 
brand itself. The concept of a separate 
existence for the brand is not just a financial 
or compatibility issue; the true brand has to 
achieve its own personality. In the marketing 
of physical products manufacturers' 
advertising often associates brands with an 
individual (such as with Oxo's Katy and Mr 
Kipling and his cakes), whose personality 
positions the intangible aspects of the brand. 
The same approach is used by retailers in 
their use of lifestyle graphics to make 
tangible the aspiration which their products 
might help their customers to achieve. Many 
retailers are named after their founders; in 
others there is still a family interest, as with 
Sainsbury and Laura Ashley. In addition

In retailing the psychological benefit to the

that shoppers shop at outlets whose image is

of saving money tends to be double-edged — 
we might like to boast of a bargain but be 
embarrassed by the possibility that something 
bought on the cheap might infer that we too 
are cheap. This issue may be central to 
whether discounters can be considered to be 
brands. 
DISCUSSION 
Retailers' products can meet any and all of 
the four tests for branding that this article 
has used to structure the decision over 
whether retailers can be brands. The own-
label is likely to stand more chance of 
achieving brand status: if it is not a copy of 
an existing line and its name can be 
protected (differentiation); if it can achieve a 
quality image, possibly because the retailer 
has technical competence and can therefore 
exercise quality control; if other retailers 
would be willing to sell it and/or the name 
can be transferred to other products; if the 
customer does not feel cheapened by the 
purchase. 
If a retailer has built a successful product 
brand, the brand name can be sold, licensed 
or transferred. The product can be priced at 
the same level as that of manufacturers' 
brands and can be sold in other retail 
outlets. If brand valuation on the balance-
sheet should become the accepted norm, the 
valuation of such products will enhance the 
company's worth. The implications are that 
retailers will benefit from developing own-
brands rather than own-labels. The costs of 
doing so may well be lower than those for 
the manufacturer who has to advertise to 
establish a consumer franchise. 
The issue of retail stores as brands is less 
easy to argue. Differentiation and separate 
existence are clearly possible; retailers can be 
differentiated in the same way as product 
brands and retail trading names can be 
transferred to other retail formats and to 
other products and services. On the other 
two tests, discounters do not seek to 
command a premium and the fact that they 
sell cheaply may well influence the self-
perception of the people who shop there. 
□ 
PEOPLE EXCHANGE TIME WITHOUT 
NECESSARILY EXCHANGING MONEY 
WHEN SHOPPING 
□ 
To argue that discounters are brands, we need 
to argue that something different from a 
financial premium is being exchanged 
between retailer and shopper. Furthermore, 
we need to suggest how shoppers can obtain 
a positive self-image from shopping there. On 
the issue of a premium, one surrogate for 
money is time. Bagozzi[46] argued that 
marketing should not be seen as concerned 
solely with exchanges of goods or services 
for money and Dwyer et al. [47] argued that 
social exchanges develop from commercial 
transactions as part of the marketing process. 
People exchange time without necessarily 
exchanging money when shopping. While 
many shopping trips are made to the nearest 
and most convenient outlet, shoppers are 
often willing to travel further to visit a 
chosen retailer. Once in a retail outlet the 
time we spend there is related to how much 
we buy[48,49]. Store loyalty is concerned with 
how frequently we visit an outlet, one further 
aspect of an exchange that involves time as 
much as money; indeed most retail offers can 
be sampled without parting with money. The 
travel and time premium for retailers varies. 
Marks & Spencer often secures favourable 
terms to take space in a retail development 
because of their ability to draw customers to 
a retail centre and from a long distance. 
Furniture retailer, IKEA, can attract custom 
from well outside 50 miles and shoppers 
spend on average over an hour in their 
stores. The "branded experience" is an 
appropriate description for this concept of 
the retail brand. The process to which the 
customer is exposed from leaving home to

CONCLUSIONS 
Table I summarizes the main points of the 
article by distinguishing, using the four tests, 
between the features of notionally 
"successful" and "unsuccessful" retail 
brands. Differentiation between the two types 
of retail brand is important both to 
practitioner and researcher. Brand loyalty to 
the product and source loyalty to the retailer 
require different approaches both in analysis 
and in brand building. Any assumption that 
the own-brand and the retailer process brand 
represent the same values to the shopper 
must be questioned. For example, a valued 
process brand does not automatically allow a 
retailer to charge a premium for its products. 
Logically the image of one will impact on 
the other and it may be logical to aim for 
the same consumer values for both. 
□ 
BRANDING BECAME THE DEVICE BY 
WHICH MANUFACTURERS COULD 
CONTROL THE DISTRIBUTION SYSTEM 
□ 
If own-label products become own-brands, 
then the retailer can choose to offer them to 
other retailers or to license the brand name 
to other businesses. Before the industrial 
revolution most brands, such as they were, 
were those of the producer/retailer. Branding 
became the device by which manufacturers 
could control the distribution system. 
Retailers do not necessarily need to employ 
branding to achieve channel control but the 
approach will allow for higher prices. The 
current issue on the relative prices charged by 
supermarkets and independents for fresh 
produce and the margins made by the 
multiple grocers can be answered if retail 
multiples can justify their produce, sold 
without overt labels but essentially as own-
label, as own-brand. Customers need to be 
convinced they are receiving an extra 
guarantee of quality to justify any premium 
in prices and they also need to feel good, not 
cheated, about their purchases. To be 
convincing, retailers probably need to invest 
in understanding and controlling the quality 
of the products they wish to sell as own-
brand and then to position such products on 
a premium to the market average. Only then 
will own-labels be convincing as own-brands in 
all respects and produce the profitability of which 
they are capable. Finally, the immediate value 
of a valued process brand is increased patronage, 
location becomes less of an issue and the 
retailer has a name that can be beneficially 
transferred to other businesses.
