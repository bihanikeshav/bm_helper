ABSTRACT
The article aims at making a conceptual contribution to services
marketing literature by proposing an eighth “P” called “proof.”
Proof is a simple operational measure or an intuitive indicator
that helps the customer quickly assess the efficacy of a service.
The article discusses services marketing literature and develops
propositions that underscore the importance of proof as the
eighth P. When proof is provided about a service to the customer,
it affects all service outcomes (i.e., service evaluation, purchase
intent, customer satisfaction, and repurchase intent). Though
conceptual, the article provides practical implications as well.
Introduction
A service in marketing is typically specified through its marketing mix, popularly
known as the 7Ps. The 7Ps are product, price, place, promotion, physical evidence,
process, and people (Booms & Bitner, 1981). However, what the 7Ps do not cover
is a measure or an indicator of the usefulness of the service to the consumer. This
is where we propose an eighth “P” of services marketing by name of “proof.” Proof
is making good the service promise and would help to measure if the service were
delivered well through 7Ps. It is an indicator intuitively understood by the customer
(e.g., “Few customers if ever complain about our service”) or is expressed as a num-
ber (e.g., “97% of our customers received feedback on time”).
Proof is different from other measures of services or the 7Ps. While physical evi-
dence is related to factors such as signage, employee dress, and store layout, proof
goes beyond that and provides a metric either from the present or the past that
gives confidence to the customer in service. Similarly, while processes would enable
a customer to evaluate service aspects, proof of how smooth or efficient the same
processes are would stimulate them to select/consume the service. Thus, proof bor-
rows from all the 7Ps, past records, dynamically presented evidence online, and
third-party certifications. Proof can alter perceptions about a service, and hence

Given this background, it is interesting to note that there has not been much
focus on the concept of proof as a separate construct, or as we suggest, the eighth
P of services marketing. In the present study, we aim to establish the relevance of
proof conceptually as an important and independent entity in services marketing.
Thereby we undertake the following steps. First, we provide a literature review of
the major metrics in services marketing (such as service level agreement [SLA],
SERVQUAL, physical evidence, and proof of service [PoS]), and simultaneously dis-
cuss how proof is different from them. Subsequently, we discuss the role of proof as
a mediator between the 7Ps and consumer attitudinal and behavioral outcomes of
service consumption. Thereby, we develop a set of testable propositions in a con-
ceptual model that underlines the role of proof in services marketing. We end the
article with the discussion and scope for future studies in the topic.
Literature survey
The concept of proof is not entirely new and has been hinted at in four streams of
service literature viz.: (a) servicescape; (b) service quality; (c) SLA, and (d) PoS.
Servicescape and proof
Servicescape is a widely used term to describe the physical surroundings of a ser-
vice company (Bitner, 1992). Servicescape includes the exterior and interior design;
ambient conditions such as temperature, noise, and odor; and other tangible props
like business cards, brochures, and other communication material (e.g., Bitner,
1992; Wakefield & Blodgett, 1996). Zeithaml and Bitner (1996) listed facility design,
equipment, signage, employees dress, reports, business cards, statements, guaran-
tees, and other tangibles in physical evidence. Notice that statements and guaran-
tees are still intent and not proof. A testimonial from a third party or an expert that
a guarantee has been delivered can be proof.
Reimer and Keuhn (2005) offered servicescape as a surrogate for service qual-
ity. Others (Berry & Bendapudi, 2003; Lo, 2011) have also supported the use of
servicescape as an indicator of “service evidence.” The outcome of a positive ser-
vicescape is favorable on both the service provider (Parish, Berry, & Lam, 2008)
and customers (Rosenbaum & Wong, 2007). In addition, Rosenbaum and Massiah
(2011) delved deeper into the concept of servicescape and found various intangi-
ble or symbolic elements that may influence the consumer more than tangibles.
Researchers have observed various outcomes of servicescape such as satisfac-
tion/dissatisfaction (Hoffman, Kelley, & Chung, 2003; Miles, Miles, & Cannon,

Service quality and proof
Measuring service quality has been a debatable issue in services research and various
measures were suggested for the same (e.g., Cronin & Taylor, 1994; Parasuraman,
Zeithaml, & Berry, 1994). The hierarchical service quality model (Brady & Cronin,
2001) suggests that there are three levels at which service quality is measured: (a)
interaction quality, (b) physical environment quality, and (c) outcome quality (Brady
& Cronin, 2001). (Pollack, 2009) linked higher hierarchical service quality model
measures with greater satisfaction, repurchase, and word-of-mouth. SERVPERF is
the other instrument that is often compared with SERVQUAL. Badri, Abdulla, and
Al-Madani (2005) and Gica and Moisescu (2013) found SERVPERF to be more suit-
able in assessing service quality of travel agencies than SERVQUAL. In this con-
text, Ladhari’s (2009) historical research on SERVQUAL supported its usefulness in
spite of limitations. Depending on the context, SERVQUAL and SERVPERF have
been observed to yield similar (Bayraktaroglu & Atrek, 2010) and different results
(Ali, Ali, & Radam, 2010; Carrillat, Jaramillo, & Mulki, 2007; Rodrigues, Barkur,
Varambally, & Golrooy Motlagh, 2011). However, Gounaris (2005) showed that
INDSERV is more suitable for measuring service quality in business-to-business ser-
vices than SERVQUAL. From the literature, it could be inferred that all approaches
to measure service quality have merits and demerits, but none has a simple approach
and all require a structured process of estimation. Proof, as we argue, is a less rigor-
ous but a quick dipstick measure from the customer. It is an indicator that appeals
to the customer about a service in a certain context.
Service level agreement and proof
SLA is used more often in the information technology industry. A SLA is defined
as an agreement that is developed based on mutual understanding and nego-
tiations between a service provider and a client that “serves as the foundation
for the expected level of service between the consumer and the provider” (Patel,
Ranabahu, & Sheth, 2009). Majority of research in SLA focused on operational issues
such as implementation (Caggiano, Jackson, Muckstadt, & Rappold, 2007; Johnson,
2005; Robbins & Harrison, 2010); management (Trienekens, Bouman, & Van Der
Zwan, 2004); regulation (Stojanovic, Kostic-Ljubisavljevic, & Radonjic-Djogatovic,
2013), and security implications (Karadsheh, 2012; Serral-Gracia, Yannuzzi, Labit,
Owezarski, & Masip-Bruin, 2010). Most of these prescriptions are qualitative or
quantitative methodologies. They are elaborate and therefore are not simple or intu-
itive. In addition, they are more concerned with the harder parts of a process rather

“Proof of service” and proof
Proof of service (PoS), like SLA, is a concept more often used in information technol-
ogy and communications. Hsiao and Yang (2010) describe PoS in software projects
to consist of service model design, service verification, service quality, and service
efficiency. Schorr (1997) distinguished between PoS and the impact of PoS in the
context of linking research with public health program outcomes. Marquis, Douthit,
and Elliot (2011) echoed a similar view in the context of counseling, where they
distinguished between best practices and evidence supported treatments. Shostack
(1982) offered three indicators of PoS: (a) time and material consumption, (b)
resource-as-a-service (infrastructure or platform or software), or (c) process trans-
formation. The PoS literature (quite similar to SLA) delves more into methodolo-
gies rather than simple and specific metrics that can help customers quickly judge a
service.

proof is different from the aforementioned concepts. Each of the inputs (physical
evidence, SERVQUAL, SLA, and PoS) might contain the seed for proof. Depending
on the context, the customer, and the domain, the service provider has to con-
vert some aspects of these four approaches into proof. Proof has to be not only
extracted but also communicated to customers. The service provider, in their own
interest, should articulate and present proof because it positively affects factors
such as service evaluation and purchase intent. That is why we call proof as the
eighth P. In the following subsections we present a set of propositions that are
concerned with the impact of proof on service evaluation through purchase intent
and customer satisfaction to repurchase. We also present propositions of the effect
of sources of proof (friends/acquaintances/websites/referrals, etc.) and types of
proof (positive/negative).
Propositions
Service evaluation and proof
The first proposition relates service evaluation to proof. Researchers have found var-
ious aspects of service recovery and evaluation to be related to the evidence provided
by the service provider. (Gautam, 2013) found service recovery to be correlated pos-
itively to the response promptness of the service provider. A prompt response can be
converted to proof that helps customers in service evaluation. Bawden, Petuchovaite,
and Vilar (2005) found the need for an evidence-based system to assess the ser-
vice performance of libraries in a European nation. Gutierrez, Martinez-Lopez, and
Molina Fernandez (2006) showed that service quality perceptions are related to the
length of period for which the customers are associated with the service provider.
Ofir, Simonson, and Yoon (2009) extended this thought and suggested that the fact
that something is being evaluated in itself tends to bring negative evaluations from
customers. Thus, prompt action during a service failure enhances the positive eval-
uation of the service by customers.
We argue that customers are less likely to form negative evaluations if they are
given evidence to the contrary. In addition, alerting customers to the possibility of a
rating bias on either side reduces the tendency to give negative evaluations. Instead,
if effective and positive proof is offered about a service, it brings down the tendency
to rate it negatively. The parameters of service evaluation are set by the expectations
of the customer. The service provider should satisfy them to the best of their abilities
(Alden, He, & Chen, 2010). It is important therefore that the service provider gives
the customer a proof that they value. We therefore, propose:
Proposition 1: Providing relevant proof affects service evaluation positively.

customers (Huang, 2010). Good service providers help customers fight the negative
emotions generated by service failure (Smith & Bolton, 2002). In some cases of neg-
ative service experiences, conditional service guarantees after service recovery have
improved evaluation (McColl, Mattsson, & Morley, 2005). Thus, when organizations
make efforts to mitigate customer grievances, the customers view the same favorably.
Thus, giving reassuring proof at appropriate points is useful. In addition, when there
is service failure due to switching to a new mechanism like self-service, high-equity
brands suffer less if they deliver adequate service recovery (i.e., remedy service fail-
ure). They also suffer less when the cause for service failure lies outside the control
of the service provider (Liao & Cheng, 2013). Thus, even if there is a negative expe-
rience, customers are often ready to forgive a service that enjoys better reputation.
Typically, mitigation of service complaints should be addressed through regulating
the quality of service experience (Brown, 2007) such as the experience provided by
Disney theme parks and stores. A negative service experience can also be coun-
tered by addressing specific dimensions of quality that improve overall perception
of service (Odekerken-Schröder, Van Birgelen, Lemmink, De Ruyter, & Wetzels,
2000).
One way to address negative experience is providing positive PoS. In other words,
customers would rate a service more favorably even if there is a negative service
experience if: (a) the reasons are beyond control, (b) employees are seen as making
effort to address the problem, and (c) the service provider is generally known for
delivering good service. In all the mentioned cases, providing appropriate proof can
give greater room for the service provider in handling negative service experiences.
Thus, the next proposition of the study is given as:
Proposition 2: A reliable service suffers less despite a negative customer experience, if it
provides relevant proof.
Purchase intent and proof
Several different stimuli are found to trigger purchase intent such as price bundle
and discounts (Bouwman, Haaker, & De Vos, 2007; Stafford & Stafford, 2000), after
service recovery (Maxham, 2001), and service guarantee (Wirtz, Kum, & Sheang
Lee, 2000). Other soft aspects such as employee conduct may also lead to pur-
chase intent (Netemeyer, 2005; Shao, Baker, & Wagner, 2004). Among other factors,
giving relevant information enhances the desirability of a service (Pulos & Leng,
2010). Conversely, not giving appropriate information brings down its attractive-
ness (Monopoli, Nicholas, Georgiou, & Korfiati, 2002; Roxas, Peek, Peek, & Hage-
mann, 2000). Thus, we argue that providing proof can enhance the prospect of pur-
chase. Proof, as mentioned however, is contextual. Each of the aforementioned cases
demands a different proof. Providing proof of relevance (such as safety in a ferry
service, nutrition in a restaurant, and ease-of-use in a website) could alter choice.

Customer satisfaction and proof
Customer satisfaction or dissatisfaction has several sources such as the basic ser-
vice quality, employees (Guchait, Namasivayam, & Lei, 2011), service failure sever-
ity (Weun, Beatty, & Jones, 2004), and service firm’s culture (Ame, 2013). However,
when there is service recovery, satisfaction improves remarkably (Maxham, 2001).
Proof here seems to be a function of promptness of action. Therefore, proof artic-
ulated intelligently by employees can have a salutary effect on customer satisfac-
tion. Finally, the service outcome itself is crucial for customer satisfaction. Litera-
ture shows that if the outcome is satisfactory, lapses in service experience tend to
be ignored (Park, Cho, & Rao, 2012). In this aspect, Dabholkar and Overby (2005)
found that desired service outcomes are more important than desirable processes. In
other words, tangible proof the customer sees as output is more important than ser-
vice experience itself. Therefore, proof provided to customer during or at the exit
of service experience can positively affect satisfaction. This leads us to the fourth
proposition:
Proposition 4: Providing relevant proof affects customer satisfaction positively.
Repurchase intent and proof
Repurchase is based on the firsthand experience of the customer with the service and
overall customer satisfaction is an important trigger for repurchase (Tang, Jia, Zhou,
& Yin, 2010). Therefore, how the service encounter itself is handled is important for
repurchase (Park, Cho, & Rao, 2012). Maxham, and Netemeyer (2002) opined that
repurchase intent dips sharply if there are two consecutive service failures without
recovery. Hume (2008) hence contended that both core (e.g., the food served in a
restaurant) and supplementary (e.g., the steward smiling when they serve) compo-
nents of the service are important for repurchase. Consequently, a proof at the cor-
porate brand level might serve as a booster for repurchase (e.g., no IndiGo airlines
flight was rescheduled between December 1, 2013, and January 31, 2014, therefore
it is a reliable airline). This leads to the next proposition:
Proposition 5: Proof that underscores the strength of the corporate brand influences
repurchase.

influencers of repurchase (Jones, 2014). Further, what employees do in uncontrolled
service situations seems to affect repurchase intent significantly. Thus, proof pro-
vided by employees who enjoy the confidence of the customers might have a positive
impact on repurchase. Hence, the next proposition is given as:

A vast body of literature suggests that word-of-mouth from friends and acquain-
tances has a positive effect on service choice and evaluation (e.g., Godes & Mayzlin,

The Model
Combining the propositions and the 7Ps of services marketing we create the con-
ceptual model in Figure 1. Thus, we propose that the 7Ps should lead to the concept
of proof that would in turn facilitate service evaluation, purchase intent, satisfac-
tion, and repurchase intent. We are not undermining the role of the 7Ps of services
marketing, but only highlighting the role of the new P (proof) in the same model.
The propositions in the model when tested, will establish the importance of proof
as a concept. They can collectively demonstrate that appropriate proof when pro-
vided to the customer impacts service outcomes across the board. These range from
service evaluation through purchase intent and customer satisfaction to repurchase
intent. If this framework is tested and accepted then proof can be called the eighth P
of the service marketing mix. Proof is in itself a consequence of the synergy between
the elements in the 7Ps. However, it acquires a meaning of its own for the customer.
When presented appropriately for a given context, proof can make a fundamental
impact on service evaluation, purchase intent, customer satisfaction, and repurchase
intent at every stage.
Conclusion
The present study establishes the role of proof as the eighth P of services market-
ing. However, the model and arguments developed in this present study are only
at a conceptual level and need empirical validation. Thereby the study only pro-
vides a new direction that has to be tested formally using empirical data. Besides,
the propositions need to be augmented using anecdotal and theoretical evidence
to create testable hypotheses. However, the study has its theoretical implication in
providing a new P of services marketing and provides managers a new direction to
think in terms of service creation and delivery.
