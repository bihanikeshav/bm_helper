## Overview

This article explains the Brand Identity Planning Model, a framework that guides strategic brand analysis and brand identity construction. The model is structured around three major components: Strategic Brand Analysis, the Brand Identity System, and Brand Identity Implementation. The final output is the Brand Position, which is communicated through Brand-Building Programs and tracked over time.

## 1.0 Strategic Brand Analysis

Strategic brand analysis is the first step. The brand manager needs to understand three entities: customers, competitors, and the brand itself (self-analysis).

### 1.1 Customer Analysis

#### 1.1.1 Trends
Trends refer to changing consumption patterns in a market. Examples include the shift in automobile buying from diesel cars to petrol/electric vehicles. In the Indian context, following a Supreme Court order curbing diesel SUVs in Delhi, small diesel car sales fell sharply. Trends, therefore, reflect macro-level directional shifts that affect which products and brands are relevant.

#### 1.1.2 Motivation
Motivation is what prompts a consumer to buy a product. Two types exist:
- **Functional motivation**: buying a product for what it does (e.g., buying a car for transportation).
- **Lifestyle motivation**: buying a product for what it says about the buyer (e.g., buying a certain car to win peer group approval). The desire to win approval of the peer group is what prompts an individual to adopt a certain lifestyle.

#### 1.1.3 Unmet Needs
These are consumer needs left unaddressed. Smart brands identify and satisfy these needs. For a long time, no brand addressed the problem of "cracked feet." Krack cream did, and became a successful brand. Polishing shoes is not a pleasant chore. Most of us would like to skip it. Often we are reminded of it in the last minute. Kiwi therefore introduced a brand named Express Shine Sponge which gives instant shine to shoes. That makes it quicker. There are always unmet needs like this in the market that brands can fulfil.

#### 1.1.4 Segmentation
Segmentation is partitioning of the market logically based on criteria such as benefits sought, price sensitivity, brand loyalty, application, etc.

---

### 1.2 Competitor Analysis

#### 1.2.1 Brand Image
Brand image is what the customer has in his/her mind when s/he thinks of the brand. Scooty is seen as a teenage or feminine brand. Pears is seen as a medicinal soap. Lux is seen as cosmetic. Apple is seen as sophisticated. SBI is seen as "safe", ICICI is seen as "aggressive" and HDFC is seen as "smart". All the three are banks. But each is perceived differently. Brand image therefore is the perception a customer holds about a brand in his/her mind.

#### 1.2.2 Strengths/Strategies
Strengths are the positives associated with a brand. IBM's strength lies in its ability to provide an end-to-end IT solution. It can configure an IT architecture that specifically addresses the IT needs of a firm. Other IT companies may or may not have the ability to deliver end-to-end. For instance a TCS or an Infosys do not deal in hardware. Horlicks can be mixed directly with hot water and consumed as a beverage. Other beverages need milk as an additive. That is Horlicks' unique strength.

Strategy is the long term vision for a brand. For instance, Dr Reddy Labs wants to be in complex generics while the rest of the Indian pharma companies focus on generics per se. Infoedge not only owns the biggest job market website Naukri but also educational, matrimonial and realty sites. Besides, it invests in and mentors several start-ups. Thus its long term charter seems to be turning into a one stop shop for all services delivered online.

[Page 2]

#### 1.2.3 Vulnerabilities
These are the weaknesses of the brand. Asian Paints is weak in industrial paints while Kansai is weak in decorative paints. Alto is weak on style while Siemens is weak on price (seen as high priced). Indian brands tend to be weak on technological upgradation while multinational brands tend to be weak on local connect.

---

### 1.3 Self-Analysis

#### 1.3.1 Existing Brand Image
This is the customers' current perception about the brand. Brand image has already been discussed in the previous section.

#### 1.3.2 Brand Heritage
Brand heritage is the tradition that a brand represents and the values it historically stood for. Swiss watch brands stand for craftsmanship. Wells Fargo has a heritage of helping small and medium enterprises (pioneers, merchants and ranchers). Starbucks was inspired by the espresso coffee bar culture of Italy. Johnson and Johnson with products for women and kids always stood for the word "caring". The ruggedness of Levi's jeans goes back to the inspiration it draws from miner's clothes.

#### 1.3.3 Strengths/Capabilities
This was already discussed under the section titled "competitor strengths and strategies".

#### 1.3.4 Organization Values
Values are beliefs central to the organization that are communicated implicitly in the activities related to the brand. Values go deeper than anything else in the organization. People, practices and products change. Values do not change often. They are cherished and held on to. Infosys believes in ethics. Benetton believes in "clothes for humans". Every occasion demands a different type of clothing. In European winter people brave biting cold. In extreme cold, in some countries kids wrap themselves up in animal skin. In others they dress up as animals for Christmas. Benetton claims to have clothing for every occasion. They see themselves as "the clothing people".

## 2.0 Brand Identity

### 2.1 Core Identity
The essence of the brand stays with it almost throughout its life. All products of the brand should evolve from and connect with its essence. Core identity is like the social security number in US or Aadhar number in India. For a citizen it is unique. A human being can change locations, jobs, professions, even beliefs. His security number doesn't change (like his DNA). Santoor toilet soap's core identity is "achiever mother". Kellogg's is strongly identified with breakfast cereal that is "healthy". Thums Up stood for "adventure" for the last three decades. Core identity is relatively permanent.

### 2.2 Extended Identity
Extended identity is the detailing of the essence of the brand. The extended elements are not central to the brand. They change with time. That is how a brand remains true to itself and yet changes over a period of time. Suppose the core identity of a brand is "strong". That in itself does convey much to the customer. There are several ways of defining strength. For instance "strong" can mean unyielding (e.g. "unbreakable glass"). Strong can mean that it "bends but does not break" (eg a tyre that gets punctured but still runs another 50 km). Strong can mean ability to fight adversity (e.g. a tooth paste that helps your teeth survive the assault of tartar). Thus while core identity announces the intent, extended identity develops it.

### A. Brand as a Product

#### a. Product Scope
It is the class of products with which a brand is associated (Eg: Reynolds – pens, Nilkamal – furniture, Amul – milk products). Product scope can be summed up in 4 queries:

i. When I mention the product class what brand comes to your mind? (When the product category "chocolates" is mentioned, do you think of Cadbury's or Nestle? When I say bearings do you remember SKF or FAG? When someone says talc do you remember Ponds or Johnson?)

ii. When I mention the sub-product class what brand comes to your mind? (When someone mentions buses an Indian might recollect Tata or Leyland but when "multi axle buses" are mentioned Volvo might be recollected. Similarly when the category "soaps" is mentioned several soaps might be recollected but when the category is "healthy soap" Dettol or Lifebuoy might be remembered).

iii. What else can the brand become? (A Leyland can become a high end truck. But can it become an ice cream? Can a Ponds or a Johnson become a screw driver? Thus there are natural limitations to product scope because of brand heritage, history and tradition).

iv. What else should the brand become? (Should Google which owns the Android operating system become a phone as it did with Pixel? It will then compete with mobile set companies like Samsung with whom it was so far collaborating as an operating system supplier. Sunfeast brand stands for biscuits now. Should it enter snacks or other food categories? Is it feasible or wise or necessary or sustainable?)

#### b. Product Attributes
Product attributes are features or benefits that are strongly linked with the brand. For instance, Bosch auto components are associated with high quality and product consistency while LIC is associated with safety and security. Ola cabs are synonymous with convenience while Fabindia shirts are seen as ethnic.

[Page 5]

#### c. Quality/Value
Quality is the perception of how "good" the product is. Quality dimension can be captured by asking the question "Is it the best one can get in the product category?" Value can be ascertained by asking "Is it the best one can get at this price?" An Ariel is identified with quality while a Ghari detergent is associated with value.

#### d. Uses
Brands typically get associated with an occasion of use. Quick-fix is useful for instantly fixing broken items. Clothes typically smell as they dry. Comfort fabric conditioner gives clothes a fragrance that makes the chore of washing more pleasant. Iodex balm is meant to be used for all body pains whether shoulder, neck, back or feet. Moov however is associated with back pain alone.

#### e. Users
Brands associate themselves not just with uses but also users. While PC brands are more for a broader set of users, XBOX and Playstation specifically aim gamers. Fevicol targets carpenters. Vicks was strongly identified with kids and cold in the past. Today the brand addresses adults as well. Fair and Lovely sees women as its customers while Fair and Handsome aims men.

#### f. Country of Origin
Some brands associate themselves with their country of origin to signal certain values. Italy is known for fashion. India is known for spirituality. A brand like Urban Yoga that specifically aims exercising therefore gains from the spontaneous Indian association of Yoga with exercise. So also several Indian brands use the description "basmati rice" to cue premium Indian rice. Foster's sees itself as "Australian for beer".

### B. Brand as an Organization
Brand as organization is the second pillar of brand identity system. The organization has a direct impact on the functioning and well-being of its brands. Even if a brand is strong, its performance can suffer due to an indifferent or a crisis ridden parent. For instance, TCS is a strong brand. In fact it is one of the best performing brands from the Tata stable. But the Tata group itself is seized with governance issues. This may not immediately affect TCS's performance. But it can if the issue were to linger on. Thus an organization casts a long shadow on its brands.

#### a. Organization Attributes
What flows from the organization to the product through its principles, beliefs and programs impacts all brands. A culture that is say proactive at the organizational level is more difficult to imitate than the same at the product level. Amazon pioneered several products and services like online selling, book reader, cloud services and the use of drones to deliver packages. Such accomplishments across product lines are difficult for competitors to replicate. It is difficult to be inventive in multiple domains unless the inventive gene goes back to the roots of the organization. Organizational attributes like originality, consumer connect, quality driven mind set, dependability, reputation and technology upgradation are more resilient than their counterparts at the brand level. How many organizations can launch moon shot projects like smart contact lenses that monitor sugar levels, internet providing balloons and an initiative to fight aging as Google does? How many web sites are as dependable as IRCTC? It is not necessarily smart. But it is very reliable. How many organizations can boast of the level of technology upgradation that Gillette offers through its Mach 3 or Fusion? These virtues go back to the organization. They are more resistant to mimicking than product or brand level innovations.

[Page 6]

#### b. Local vs Global
Whether a brand is local or global depends on the organizational charter. Some companies like Wipro, Bharat Forge and Lupin are global by the very nature of their products. There are others who go global because it improves their reach, competitiveness and learning. IBM has its foot print in 190 countries because of its capabilities. The Aditya Birla group diversified globally to escape the restrictions of the licence raj in India. However global presence itself is not good enough as many Indian conglomerates are learning. It is more important to run those ventures profitably, learn from the best practices there and transmit them to the rest of the geographies. In this respect multinationals have been more patient and persistent than Indian companies.

Local brands are usually smart and quick. Unlike multinationals they need not get clearances from headquarters. Several Indian companies stand testimony to this. Asian Paints has been able to outwit the multinationals in the Indian market. Voltas put in a stand out performance in air conditioners. Godrej rebounded in refrigerators despite stiff competition from multinationals. Thus some Indian companies have much better local connect than their global counterparts. This is clearly reflected in their brands.

---

### C. Brand as a Person
Brand as a person is also referred to in literature as "brand personality". This results from converting the brand into a human being. This is done by mapping specific brand attributes to human qualities. For instance, Indian Oil is seen as a dependable businessman (owns a large number of petrol pumps and doesn't cheat since he is a PSU) while Reliance is seen as an aggressive businessman (opened petrol pumps, closed them and reopened them rapidly). Café Coffee Day may be seen as "young", while Baidyanath may be seen as "old". Jennifer Aaker in her article on dimensions of brand personality lists 5 factors that constitute a brand's personality scale namely sincerity, excitement, competence, sophistication and ruggedness. But does a brand need to score on all the 5 dimensions? Is it possible? There are brands like BHEL that are sincere and competent but may not be seen as exciting, sophisticated or rugged. On the other hand ABB or Siemens might be seen as both sophisticated and competent. A brand like Ceat might be more rugged than sophisticated while an Iphone might be more sophisticated than rugged. Thus different brands map to different sets of human qualities. This contributes to their distinctiveness.

[Page 7]

### D. Brand as a Symbol

It is the symbolic aspect of the brand that makes it distinct. Some brands merely have their names written in a certain fashion like Cisco or Siemens. Others have specific symbols like GE's meatball, the circle in which the distinctive letters G and E are written. So also recollect the symbols like "rainbow flower" of Wipro or Thumbs Up sign of Thums Up. Or a distinctive tagline like Surf's "Daag acche hai" ("Stains are good") or Kerala's "God's own country" that position the brand uniquely. It could be a political slogan like "Quit India" (Indian freedom movement) or "Make in India". It could be a campaign ("Swachch Bharat" or "Clean India"). It could be an evocative pictorial representation or a metaphor like LIC's "lamp protected by hands" or SBI's keyhole which represents safety and security (signifying probably that your money is safe). It could be a mascot like Nirma Girl or "Shera", the cheerful tiger of Asian Games-2010 dressed in sports shorts and shoes and welcomes the athlete guests with traditional Indian "Namaste". It could be the signature colours of a brand like Himalaya's "green and teal" or Pepsi's "Red, white and blue". It can be a jingle (Intel, Britannia, Titan) or a program ("Tata Tea's Jago Re" or "TCS Marathon") strongly identified with the brand. Customers tend to identify brands by their sights, sounds and smells.

## 3.0 Value Proposition

The final output of the analysis of the previous two stages (strategic brand analysis and brand identity) is the value proposition of the brand. This consists of the functional, emotional and self-expressive benefits of the brand.

### 1. Functional Benefits
Functional benefits are based on the specific utility provided to the customer by a brand. These are benefits that can be verified. They connect with the "head". They appeal to logic. For instance, cars promising to give a certain mileage. Nicotinell, the nicotine repelling over-the-counter product is available in several product forms like lozenge and gum. It promises to produce repulsion in the user for smoking in 2 seconds. Fogg claims to be a body spray "with all perfume and no gas". TVS Jupiter promises "Jyaada" (More). It promises more of everything (More storage, more convenience because the fuel tank can be filled without lifting the seat, more comfort from softer seat, more leg space and space to keep things, more mileage - best in class 62 km per litre).

**Some examples of functional benefits:**
- Vehicle – Touches 80 kmph in no time
- Tickets - Zero waiting time
- Courier – no extra charges
- Liquid fund – more benefit, less cost
- Pizza – money back if delivered late
- Cold cream – 30% improvement in skin texture
- Supermarket – dead cheap
- Website – delivers goods to your doorstep
- Mobile – intuitive icons
- Laptop - ease of use
- Car – high performance
- Bank loan – affordable instalments
- Housing - economical

[Page 9]

### 2. Emotional Benefits
Emotional benefits address the heart. They are characterized by words like happiness, sentimentality, nostalgia, fun, celebration, peace of mind etc. In fact any of the "Nava Rasas" (the Sanskrit word for the spectrum of 9 emotions ranging from eroticism and happiness to amazement and anger) can be used for connecting with the customer. Flipkart ad has kids speaking like "adults" contributing to the perception of cuteness/fun. Brooke Bond Red Label ad shows how a tea breaks barriers between cultural stereotypes of Hindus and Muslims and addresses the emotion of empathy. Honda tells you that its brands have been used by a crore people already in India. This plays to peace-of-mind. So did ICICI, which in the past created a character called "Chintamani" (a typical middle class Indian). ICICI's promise to this archetypal Indian was "No 'Chinta', only money" (no worry, only money). Communication in Vodafone's retirement farewell, Titan's gifting, Google's cross border friendship ads is wrapped in sentimentality.

Food products usually highlight gourmet, deliciousness and other sensory appeals. Sanitary napkin and anti-dandruff communication usually centres round the avoidable emotion of embarrassment. Even non-positive emotional cues like irritation can be used. For instance, Onida's "Neighbor's Envy, Owner's Pride" is an attempt to stand out by contrast, using negative emotions. It was very successful in its communication. Till determined multinationals entered and offered superior offerings Onida could charge a premium compared to Indian brands like Videocon or BPL.

**Some examples of emotional benefits:**
- Movie - It is a laugh riot
- Hospital - no post-operative complications
- Bank - zero tension
- Vacation – away from day to day worries
- Online – cash on delivery
- Retail – no questions asked replacement
- Resort – Near divine experience

[Page 10]

### 3. Self-Expressive Benefits
Self-expressive benefits play to ego. The words associated with self-expressive benefits are demonstrativeness, badge value, snob value, display, elitism, exclusivity and pride. An example of this is, being proud of owning a BMW, a Harley Davidson, a Rolex or a Vertu mobile service. Why is the name HP or Dell written upside down on your laptop? It is to indicate to the others that you are smart (because you bought HP). Why is Ray Ban written on your spectacles so prominently? It is to demonstrate to others that you are a proud Ray Ban user. An American Express Centurion Card more popularly called Amex black card is a by-invitation only card. It is given to a select set of wealthy customers. Some housing societies are closed to outsiders. They play up exclusivity and snob value. Close Up toothpaste promises to make one attractive to the other gender. Old Spice hands out the allurement "smell like a man". Tattooing is meant to cue to others that you are "cool". Furniture in the hall is meant to demonstrate to others that you are tasteful. Bose speakers are meant to signal the refined taste of the owner. Sleek products like Curved LED Samsung TV, HP Envy, Lenovo thin laptop allow the user to bask in the reflected glory of their sleekness. Transmission Audio has speakers that go all the way up to 2 million $. Often such high end gadgets are custom built. They are meant to showcase the discerning buyer. In an ultimate compliment to self-expressiveness, Tony Curtis, the Hollywood actor declared that he wanted to be buried with his Iphone. In sum, self-expressive benefits underscore what psychologists call "self-concept" (one's own sense of self) and how it relates to others.

**Summary Table — Three Types of Benefits:**

| Benefit | What it addresses | Typical e.g. | Other e.g.s |
|---|---|---|---|
| Functional | Head | This chair is "comfy" | Makes my life simple |
| Emotional | Heart | This is my grandfather's chair | Makes me happy/sentimental |
| Self-expressive | Ego | This chair has been imported from Germany | Makes me proud |

**Finally:** Brand identity is how the strategist wants the brand to be seen by customers. Brand image is how it is seen by customers.
