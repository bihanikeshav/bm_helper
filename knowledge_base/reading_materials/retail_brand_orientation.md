## Abstract

This study adopts a resource-based view of branding referred to as brand orientation. Despite the importance of branding in retail, relatively little empirical research has been conducted to understand the degree to which retailers can be considered brand oriented. The purpose of the present research is to establish a conceptualisation of brand orientation that is applicable in a retail context across countries. Moreover, we seek to empirically validate a model of the retail brand orientation–positional advantage–organisational performance relationship and to contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of the factors driving retailer performance. A mail survey was used to collect data from retail firms in Australia, USA and UK. The unit of analysis is the retail firm. Confirmatory factor analysis was employed to assess the measurement properties of the study constructs and structural equation modelling was performed to test the research model. The findings suggest that four elements of retail brand orientation (functionality, distinctiveness, augmentation and symbolism) play different roles in relation to certain aspects of positional advantage, which highlights the importance of developing strength in all four areas. Similarly, a position of superiority in only one aspect of a retailer's offer is insufficient to assure both financial and strategic returns.

**Keywords:** retail brand orientation; positional advantage; performance

## [Page 1 / p. 245] — Introduction

Within retailing the brand concept has been traditionally applied in three ways:
1. The branding of retailers' products (Burt and Davies 2010)
2. Corporate branding (Burt and Sparks 2002)
3. Branding the retail store (Burghausen and Fan 2002; Burt and Carralero-Encinas 2000)

This paper contributes to the field and looks at branding within the retail context from a **business capability perspective**. Historical brand research has established the benefits of a strong brand in terms of financial value, creation of equity, image, loyalty and the Brand Report Card (Aaker 1992, 1997; Jara and Cliquet 2012; Keller 2000). Branding has also been identified as a potential source of positional advantage and one of the most significant challenges facing retail management (Berinstain and Zorilla 2011; Burt and Carralero-Encinas 2000; Burt and Davies 2010). Ailawadi and Keller (2004, p. 331) assert "given its highly competitive nature, branding can be especially important in the retailing industry to influence customer perceptions and drive store choice and loyalty."

The premise of the **Resource-Based View (RBV)** is that organisational capabilities are sources of positional advantage and lead to superior performance (Barney 1991; Day and Wensley 1988). Hooley and Broderick (1998) contend that business orientations are capabilities, as they set the firm's direction and activities to ensure that resources are fully exploited. Brand researchers have embraced this capability perspective with the establishment of brand orientation (Baumgarth 2010; Bridson and Evans 2004; Ewing and Napoli 2005; Hankinson 2001a; Urde 1994; Wong and Merrilees 2007a).

Given the highly context-specific nature of conceptualisation, a dominant conceptualisation of brand orientation has yet to emerge. For instance, Hankinson (2001a, 2001b, 2002) and Ewing and Napoli (2005) focus on non-profit brand orientation, O'Cass and Voola (2010) refer to political brand orientation, Baumgarth (2010) examines business-to-business branding and Bridson and Evans (2004) study fashion retailers. Following the trend towards context-specific research, we seek to empirically validate a conceptualisation of **retail brand orientation (RBO)**.

Only limited extant research (Bridson and Evans 2004) has examined brand orientations' ability to provide a positional advantage for the retail firm. The central tenet of the RBV, as depicted in the **sources–position–performance (SPP) framework**, is that a positional advantage, created through distinctive capabilities, will enhance organisational performance (Day and Wensley 1988; Hunt and Morgan 1995; Matear et al. 2004). This current paper seeks to validate the ability of RBO to generate a positional advantage that enhances organisational performance for retail firms.

## [Page 2 / p. 246] — Conceptual Development

### Brand Orientation

Most business orientations lack common conceptualisation (Avlonitis and Gounaris 1999). The issue driving the differences is rooted in the lack of a common understanding of business orientations. Confusion arises from the fact that different authors have adopted either a **philosophical or behavioural approach** to business orientations (Avlonitis and Gounaris 1999).

- The **philosophical foundation** suggests that orientations are embedded in the organisation's thinking and reflected in organisational values and beliefs.
- The **behavioural foundation** concentrates on the orientation in terms of implemented behaviours and activities.

Table 1 illustrates the two perspectives of brand orientation.

Extant literature has applied both philosophical and behavioural perspectives to brand orientation:
- The philosophical approach defines brand orientation in terms of a perception (Hankinson 2002), shared sense of brand meaning (Ewing and Napoli 2005), organisational value (Baumgarth 2010; Bridson and Evans 2004; O'Cass and Voola 2010) or mindset (Wong and Merrilees 2007a, 2007b, 2008).
- The behavioural approach focuses on organisational processes (Ewing and Napoli 2005; Urde 1999), uses (Hankinson 2001a, 2001b), management (Hankinson 2001a, 2001b) and practices and behaviours (Baumgarth 2010; Bridson and Evans 2004; O'Cass and Voola 2010).

Despite the differences in perspective at the definitional level, most studies have applied a behavioural approach when conceptualising brand orientation, suggesting it is best reflected in organisational practices and behaviours.

Given the retail focus of this study, the authors adopt Bridson and Evans' (2004) behavioural perspective of brand orientation. Bridson and Evans (2004) view distinctiveness, functionality, value-adding and symbolism as unique business capabilities. In contrast to Bridson and Evans (2004), this current paper proposes that it would be more accurate to classify the dimensions as **behavioural manifestations of a single business capability** (brand orientation). The limitation of Bridson and Evans' (2004) study is the small sample of fashion retailers only. This current paper investigates a more holistic model of the brand orientation–positional advantage–organisational performance relationship across three major retail countries to improve the generalisability of the RBO construct.

## [Page 3 / p. 248] — Retail Brand Orientation

Much of the retail image literature discusses consumers' overall impression of the store versus its individual attributes — the **attribute versus global construct debate** (Keaveney and Hunt 1992). The authors propose RBO that includes capabilities focussed on both functional/tangible purpose for their brand and more global objectives.

The four dimensions of RBO are:

### 1. Distinctiveness
Brands create the opportunity for retailers to be *distinctive* (Torres-Baumgarten and Yucetepe 2007). The distinctive quality of the brand encompasses:
- Identification and ownership (Bhat and Reddy 1998; de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1998a)
- Brand valuation, differentiation and brand architecture (Aaker 1992; Holt 2003; Keller 2000; Mitchell, Hutchinson and Bishop 2012)
- A means of identification and, over time, a guarantee of consistency and a shortcut in decision-making (Goodyear 1996)
- Within retail literature, a clear brand position ensures the store is distinctive and clearly identifiable from its competition (Torres-Baumgarten and Yucetepe 2007)

Keller (1993, 2003) suggests that such distinctiveness derives from the favourable, strong and unique brand associations that consumers perceive and hold in memory. A brand-oriented retailer recognises the brand as a distinct asset (Mitchell et al. 2012), beyond the value of the products or stores it represents (Jara and Cliquet 2012).

### 2. Functionality
A brand-oriented retailer must also utilise its brand as a means of satisfying consumers' basic needs. Mitchell et al. (2012) found that managers of SME retailers consider the functional meaning of their brand. Keller (2003) refers to such associations as awareness of the needs the brand satisfies. This element of RBO is termed *functional* and relates the degree to which the organisation uses its brand to communicate that the store will provide superior utilitarian benefits.

Functionality, as defined by de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley (1997a), relates to the brand's tangible, rationally assessed performance. This somewhat mirrors the functional attribute qualities Mazursky and Jacoby (1986) found as critical components of consumers' image of a retailer. Functionality encompasses:
- Practical issues including store accessibility and depth of merchandise range (Keller 2003)
- The extent to which brands satisfy consumers' basic or rational needs (de Chernatony and McWilliam 1990; de Chernatony and Segal-Horn 2001)

### 3. Augmentation
Brand-oriented retailers also exhibit an *augmentation* dimension (Ailawadi and Keller 2004; de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1998b; Mitchell et al. 2012). Literature consistently refers to brands as a means of adding value. Massa and Testa's (2012) study on Italian food retailers argues that retailers undertake a variety of activities and cannot simply be said to be resellers of manufacturer brands. McEnally and de Chernatony (1999) suggest that this dimension relates to the provision of enjoyment. Brodie (2009) in his theory building paper on service branding suggests that greater attention towards integrating the role of the brand in value-adding processes improves customer experiences and performance.

In particular, the brand provides experiential value by acting as a receptacle for intangible benefits including quality and service (Darden and Babin 1994; de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1998a, 1998b, 1999; Doyle 1990; Jara and Cliquet 2012). Within retailing, this concept of the affective meaning derived from both the functional and psychological characteristics of a retail brand is well recognised (Darden and Babin 1994; Jara and Cliquet 2012; Keaveney and Hunt 1992).

A brand-oriented retailer does not simply add service and quality features to its stores as a reaction to competitor tactics. These activities are viewed as an investment in the long-term future of the brand and are directed by a desire for the brand to communicate a differentiated total store experience.

### 4. Symbolism
*Symbolism* reflects the brand's ability to exploit consumers' internally generated needs for self-enhancement, group membership, lifestyle or ego-identification (Holt 2003; Park et al. 1986). Retailers must determine how their brand will be used to reflect the consumer's current view of themselves and the manner in which it can enhance their aspirational self-identity. de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley (1997b), de Chernatony and Segal-Horn (2001), Keller (2003) and McEnally and de Chernatony (1999) define symbolic features as intangible, emotional values of the brand. Park et al. (1986) define it as the desire for products that fulfil internally generated needs for self-enhancement, role positions, group membership or ego-identification.

Mitchell et al. (2012) in their study of retail SME branding found qualitative evidence that managers identify their brand as symbolic in nature and as often a global overall assessment. This concept of symbolism has led to an extended stream of research into the congruence between a consumer's own personality with that of the retail brand's personality (O'Cass and Grace 2008).

## [Page 4 / p. 250] — RBO and Positional Advantage

Day and Wensley (1988) conceptualise competitive advantage as a holistic concept represented by the SPP framework. Positional advantage has taken different forms depending on the context:
- Doyle and Wong (1998): products, services and company reputation
- Matear et al. (2004): relationship, brand and customer service advantages in service organisations

In this study, positional advantage is conceptualised in terms of **three key elements of the retail offer**:
1. **Merchandise advantage**
2. **Store design advantage**
3. **Front line customer service advantage**

(Bridson and Evans 2004; Vida, Reardon, and Fairhurst 2000)

Mazursky and Jacoby (1986) found that attributes of merchandise and service quality were the most critical dimensions of consumer perceived store image. Torres-Baumgarten and Yucetepe (2007) incorporated service and merchandise into their studies on retail store positioning. The addition of the store design dimension recognises that retailers are a service brand (Berry 2000) and, as such, the physical elements within the space are critical and augment the merchandise and service dimensions (Jara and Cliquet 2012; Mitchell et al. 2012). Page and Herr (2002) highlight the role of product aesthetics in driving consumer attitudes and similarly the aesthetics of the store have the potential to drive retail performance.

## [Page 5 / p. 251] — Hypotheses

### Hypotheses 1–3: RBO → Positional Advantage

**H1: The four dimensions of RBO (a) distinctiveness, (b) functionality, (c) augmentation and (d) symbolism will have a significant positive relationship with merchandise advantage.**

Rationale: A distinctive brand signals quality and reliability (Holt 2003, p. 5). Brand name becomes an asset that can be extended to merchandise (Jara and Cliquet 2012). Store-branded and private-label merchandise are more difficult to imitate than supplier-branded merchandise. Distinctive, functional, augmented and symbolic retail brands may be able to command exclusive distribution agreements from suppliers. Keller (2003) asserts that consumers lock distinctive attributers in their memory to aid awareness and recall.

**H2: The four dimensions of RBO (a) distinctiveness, (b) functionality, (c) augmentation and (d) symbolism will have a significant positive relationship with store design advantage.**

Rationale: Augmentation focuses on distinguishing the brand from competitors through the addition of service facilities, quality differences or a distinctive store experience not offered by competitors. Spangenberg, Crowley, and Henderson (1996) found that environmental attributes, such as perfumed scent, can positively influence consumer evaluations. Functionality drives innovations in store format and design, manifesting in a superior store layout.

**H3: The four dimensions of RBO (a) distinctiveness, (b) functionality, (c) augmentation and (d) symbolism will have a significant positive relationship with customer service advantage.**

Rationale: The retail industry is characterised by high employee turnover (Sparks 1992). Bridson and Evans (2004) found that both distinctive and value-adding dimensions of brand orientation were significantly associated with a service advantage for fashion retailers. A brand's symbolic quality can be utilised when recruiting staff so that employees match and enhance both the brand and consumers' self image. De Chernatony and Cottam (2006) assert that "what is delivered is less important than how it is delivered" (p. 616). Functionality is likely to be a significant driver of a customer service advantage.

### Hypotheses 4–5: Positional Advantage → Organisational Performance

**H4: The three dimensions of positional advantage (a) merchandise advantage, (b) store design advantage and (c) customer service advantage will have a significant positive relationship with financial performance.**

**H5: The three dimensions of positional advantage (a) merchandise advantage, (b) store design advantage and (c) customer service advantage will have a significant positive relationship with strategic effectiveness.**

Rationale: Merchandise advantages (fashionability and quality) can have financial dividends if they match consumer needs better than competitors, resulting in higher sales levels and improved gross margin through price premiums (Mudambi 1994). Superior quality in store displays, layout and atmosphere may also have positive financial and strategic implications (Baker, Grewel and Parasuraman 1994). Beitelspacher et al. (2011) argue that customer expenditure and organisational growth are enhanced because consumers prefer superior service offers; an organisation's favourable image can reduce uncertainty for customers.

## [Page 6 / p. 253] — Research Methodology

### Sample Characteristics and Data Collection

Given the global nature of retailing (Reinartz et al. 2011), it was considered important to develop an RBO measurement scale that was equivalent and psychometrically valid across countries. The RBO scale and conceptual model were tested using data from USA, UK and Australia.

**Data collection:**
- Australia: Random sample of top 1000 retail firms by number of employees. After exclusions (corporate policy restrictions, no longer in business, incorrectly coded), 336 responses from 110 firms → **37% response rate**
- USA: Top 600 retail firms. After exclusions (290), 67 responses → **22% response rate**
- UK: Top 600 retail firms. After exclusions (110), 101 responses → **21% response rate**

Data were collected by **mail survey**. Using the key informant method, respondents targeted were senior executives responsible for strategic brand management.

### Measure Development

Following Churchill's (1979) standard construct development principles, item pools were generated for all constructs through a deductive literature review process. Twelve case study interviews were completed with Managing Directors and Directors of Marketing of retail firms increasing the applicability of the brand orientation scales within retailing. The scale asked respondents to indicate the degree to which practices were undertaken by the firm. This scale is consistent with the behavioural perspective of RBO and comparable with other orientation scales (Hankinson 2002; Narver and Slater 1990).

Existing items and scales derived from the retail literature (Bridson and Evans 2004; McGoldrick and Ho 1992; Vida et al. 2000) were minimally adapted to measure positional advantage. The positional advantage scale asked respondents to indicate their firms' competitive position relative to their closest competitor ranging from 'major disadvantage' to 'major advantage' relating to merchandise, store design and customer service.

Performance is measured in terms of **financial performance** and **strategic effectiveness**. Respondents were asked to indicate the extent to which each element had changed in the last 3-year period (or the time period in which they had been operating if it had been less than 3 years).

### Metric Equivalence Testing

A baseline model tested whether the three samples have corresponding latent variables and that the items load similarly across the variables. This demonstrated that the model is mapping the same conceptual frame in the three samples. Tests for weak factorial invariance (Model 1), then stronger constraints (Models 2 and 3) were conducted.

### Table 3: Metric Equivalence Testing

| Model | p | CMIN/DF | RMSEA | CFI | IFI | NFI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unconstrained | 0.00 | 1.97 | 0.04 | 0.97 | 0.98 | 0.95 |
| Model 1 | 0.00 | 2.11 | 0.05 | 0.93 | 0.93 | 0.88 |
| Model 2 | 0.00 | 2.25 | 0.05 | 0.90 | 0.90 | 0.83 |
| Model 3 | 0.00 | 2.33 | 0.05 | 0.88 | 0.88 | 0.80 |

A chi-square difference test was conducted (Δχ²(38) = 51.17), which is not significant at p < 0.05. Testing for even stronger measurement invariance: Δχ²(20) = 30.1 was also not significant at p < 0.05. The chi-square difference test (Δχ²(12) = 34.5) is significant at p < 0.05, indicating the groups are not equivalent when factor loadings, residual variances and co-variances are constrained. Thus, the measurement model is invariant at a strong level across the three samples.

Convergent and discriminant validity assessed by CFA: the model demonstrates acceptable fit (χ²(314) = 680.93, CFI = 0.95, IFI = 0.95, NFI = 0.91, RMSEA = 0.05). AVE was much larger than the squared correlation in all cases — discriminant validity was satisfied. All factor loadings are significant and composite reliability ranges from 0.62 to 0.89.

Common method bias (CMB) was addressed by methodological separation, varied scale anchors, and Harman's single factor test. A single latent factor specification produced an unacceptable model fit (χ²(57) = 800.67, p value = 0.00, C_min/df = 3.30, GFI = 0.88, AGFI = 0.86, CFI = 0.92, TLI = 0.91, NFI = 0.87, RMSEA = 0.07, Hoelter (p < 0.01) = 186), suggesting a single general factor did not account for the majority of the covariance, thus CMB does not account for the results.

**Interpretation of Cross-Country Differences:**

- In **Australia**, all four dimensions of RBO were significant predictors of positional advantage. Merchandise advantage relies heavily on symbolism, followed by functionality and augmentation. Store design relies heavily on distinctiveness. Store design and customer service are the primary drivers of organisational performance.
- In **USA**, distinctiveness played the strongest role for merchandise followed by symbolism. Augmentation is the only driver of store design advantage. Merchandise advantage plays the primary role in organisational performance.

- Interestingly, customer service advantage does not have a role to play in either USA or UK in driving retailer performance.

## [Pages 10–12 / pp. 259–264] — Discussion, Conclusion and References

### Discussion

The findings provide support for the hypothesised relationships. Priem and Butler (2001) argue that the major weakness of RBV is its failure to articulate the mechanisms that convert capabilities into performance outcomes — positional advantage is one such mechanism.

**Distinctiveness** is an essential ingredient for a retailer's store design advantage, supporting Goodyear's (1996) early brand typology work — distinctiveness enables retailers to create destination stores. When retailers see their brand as an asset, they see their store as an asset. Keller (1993, 2003) suggests such distinctiveness derives from the favourable, strong and unique brand associations. Distinctiveness alone does not ensure merchandise or service advantage — highly distinctive brands could prompt management to focus more on the retail experience and less on the merchandise.

**Functionality** leads to both merchandise and customer service advantages. Given that consumers are increasingly characterised as cognitive misers, a brand's ability to focus on satisfying consumers' utilitarian needs enables retailers to improve merchandise fashionability and quality (supporting Mazursky and Jacoby 1986). High-brand functionality does not influence store design advantage — the brand's ability to solve customer purchase problems is not manifested in the store's design.

**Augmentation** is essential for store design and customer service advantages. RBO provides experiential value acting as a receptacle for intangible benefits (Darden and Babin 1994; de Chernatony and Dall'Olmo Riley 1998a, 1998b, 1999; Doyle 1990; Jara and Cliquet 2012). Augmentation is not significantly linked with merchandise advantage.

**Symbolism** leads to both merchandise and store design advantages. Superiority in symbolism ensures the brand's ability to connect with customers on a personal level, to express themselves and their lifestyle. Symbolic qualities were not a driver of a customer service advantage (possibly due to measurement limitations of the items used).

Financial performance is positively associated with superiority regarding merchandise assortment, quality and fashionability, and also with superior levels of personal selling and after sales service. Strategic effectiveness is positively associated with a retailer's ability to command an advantage in store layout, displays and atmosphere, and with customer service.

### Conclusion

As competition for the consumer dollar increases so too does the need to insulate the retailer from its competitors. The evolution of retailers towards brand orientation is a growing sign of the power of brands as a business capability. This study makes a contribution by empirically validating the importance of RBO and three retail-specific aspects of positional advantage on organisational performance, utilising the SPP framework (Day and Wensley 1988; Gray 2006; Hunt and Morgan 1995; Matear et al. 2004).

**Key managerial implications:**
1. Retailers that need to improve their position of advantage should allocate resources to building a strong RBO.
2. The four elements of RBO play different roles in relation to certain aspects of positional advantage — highlights the importance of developing strength in all four areas.
3. A positional advantage driven by retail brand-oriented practices will improve organisational performance. A position of superiority in only one aspect of a retailer's offer is insufficient to assure both financial and strategic returns.

**Limitations and future research:**
- This is an organisational perspective study — retailers were self-reporting behaviours and performance. Future research may benefit from a contrasting study comparing both managerial and consumer perspectives.
- The sustainability of positional advantage was not investigated and is an interesting extension opportunity.
- Alternative measures of organisational performance using objective data would also be a significant extension.
- Constructs such as brand personality and self-image congruence could be considered as additional sources of positional advantage.
