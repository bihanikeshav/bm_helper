## Case method – boon or bane?

Tweedledum and Tweedledee are colleagues at Indian College of Management (ICM). They specialize in different streams of management education. They are friends who agree to disagree. Here is a conversation between them.

**Tweedledee (Dee)** – hi chief, how goes life?

**Tweedledum (Dum)** – going, going, not yet gone!

**Dee** – are you in a spot of bother?

**Dum** – Well, sort of! I started reading this case published by Mecca Institute of Management (MIM). Shall I say, it is grand but hard to understand! Too complicated. To convey a simple message that too many brand extensions are bad for the parent brand, this guy writes a 25 page case with 6 tables. I would have wrapped up this story in 10 pages.

**Dee** – but then you may not be capturing the nuances. Small cases often do not faithfully replicate real life problems. In real life, data doesn't stare at you in your face. You have to cull it out of a great undifferentiated mass of information. Case method is supposed to push you to look for relevant data in an ocean of bits and bytes.

**Dum** – that brings us to the mother of all questions. What exactly does the case method demonstrate?

**Dee** – well, you tell me, you are not exactly a novice to the method yourself. You have been using the method for decades man!

**Dum** – What does it demonstrate? Concepts or real life problems?

**Dee** – Well both! Come on man, you wrote truckloads of cases yourself. You really want me to answer that one?

**Dum** – How many cases you used demonstrated both concepts and solutions to real life problems equally well?

**Dee** – why, it can be done!

**Dum** – Let us be truthful. Anything that demonstrates real life problems doesn't tidily fit into concepts and frameworks. You could figure out a real out-of-the-box solution. But by its very nature, the solution may not dovetail into a prefabricated framework. On the other hand if you are demonstrating a framework in a case, it may or may not reflect a real life situation. By the way in how many cases did you really see a strong conceptual framework? I personally attended case workshops in the best universities in the world.

---

[Page 2]

The unsaid truth is case method is more about solving real life problems and less about demonstrating concepts. And paradoxically a caveat is added "this case is not about demonstrating effective or ineffective ways of solving management problems. It is a tool for class room instruction". Isn't this chickening out? Let's face it. Faculty do not always worry themselves about tying up the solution to the concept. This is true in India as well as abroad. It is as if concepts are afterthoughts. A single minded case has to choose between the framework and real life, i.e. the abstract and the concrete. Otherwise it ends up falling between the stools doing justice to neither. Does the case method suffer from a fundamental weakness, which is trying to reconcile two irreconcilables?

**Dee** – Really? Anyway since what we finally need is solution to a problem, let us drop the concepts.

**Dum** – Gotcha! If I abandon frameworks, the very basis for a b-school is gone. We go around canvassing that we teach overarching truths that transcend time. If all we need to do is become apologists for practitioners, then the day is not far off when they will substitute us in class rooms. The practitioner might have a solution for a problem. But what needs to be discussed is whether the expert's way is the best way. Is this a temporary or permanent solution? Asking such questions is what academics is all about. We declare loftily that academic institutions are about concepts that go beyond the immediate. Whether you agree with marketing concepts like product life cycle or not, you have to agree they are backed by rigorous research. They are general principles that should be taught to management students. If demonstrating general principles is not what a case really does then why don't we teach concepts straight? On the other hand if we focus on a real life solution, that may not neatly fit a framework which is what a b-school wants to teach. Now to the ultimate sacrilege! With all these contradictions, why don't we just consign the case method to the dustbin? Simply teach concepts, show applications, give real life exercises, and get done with it. The case method almost sounds like an intrusion.

**Dee** – Nobody is forcing you to use the method, drop it.

**Dum** – really Dee, we have not only brainwashed ourselves, we have also brainwashed our students. They think that if a course is not loaded with cases it just isn't a good enough course. Besides, there are case fundamentalists among faculty who seem to substitute everything in life including love and fresh air with cases. I taught courses in both the case and non-case formats. Let me tell you. The case method is seen by the both the boys the profs as some sort of a magic wand, a sort of necromancy without the supernatural. It seems to be an end in itself. It is as if, if you run your course on cases (preferably MIM cases), 20 cases for 20 sessions that in itself becomes an achievement. I am not sure if the case method really deserves this pedestal. In fact some of our faculty told me, students ask them to add cases to their courses. What follows is amusing. You add cases. They don't read them! That is their return gift. This whole thing seems jinxed.

---

[Page 3]

**Dee** – Serves us right, don't you think. This is what we did to our professors when we were students. Students are just returning the compliment. Anyway I think you are being unfair to the students but I will let that pass. You seem to be on to something. Go on. Build a case against the case method!

**Dum** – Come to think of it, the case method IS unfair to the student. It is not necessarily the student's mistake you know, that she doesn't read the case. If she is loaded with four 20 page plus cases everyday what do you think she will do? Cut corners. Not prepare adequately or worse not prepare at all. This sometimes leads to unintended dark humor. In one of the sessions I realized that each student in the group read 4 pages in the case. There were six students. They did field the questions well. Between them they did read the case. But nobody read the full case. So nobody really had the big picture. They forgot to ask a simple question – "what are the objectives of the case? What is the message this case is trying to drive home?" So this case overload is not just unfair to students it is unfair to faculty as well.

**Dee** – Well, there are also short cases. You can use them. Who forced you to use long cases? In any case when cases are long, students can be asked to read earmarked pages, not necessarily the entire case. That surely is a way of getting over the problem, what say?

**Dum** – ever checked the continuity in those long cases in which selected pages are supposed to be read. Unless the draft is written very smartly it simply fails to convey what it is supposed to. Faculty as well as students ends up reading the whole case. So there goes your short case! By the way, this conversation is not about you or me or even this institute. It is about management education. If short cases work, then all of us should be using them not just you, me or him. As for myself I have found answers to what I have to do in a course, with or without cases. What I am doing is not the point. What should b-schools do? What is the systemic solution?

**Dee** – Good, what makes you think our colleagues in India and abroad have not figured out where to fit the case method in their scheme of things? You think you are the ultimate smart Alec who knows all answers?

**Dum** – well if they have figured out all answers, why don't they give them when asked? I have posed these questions to professors from top notch institutes in India and abroad. They were either reluctant to give answers or didn't know the answers. It is unlikely that they didn't think of these questions. They stare at you every day. The professors also probably have some tentative answers. But the answers are so subjective that they cannot be called systemic solutions. Many of the case experts in India and abroad I interacted with simply ducked these questions. Without saying it in so many words they were saying – "Chief, don't pain us. What we are doing is working for us. Find a way of solving your problems with the case method. Don't ask us". Really? Why have academic colloquiums, workshops, conferences if irritating academic questions cannot be debated? By the way, even if we accept the case method without questioning its premises there are practical problems.

---

[Page 4]

**Dee** – Like?

**Dum** – Try answering this simple question. What is the ideal length of a case?

**Dee** – You can't be serious, that depends on what you want to drive home.

**Dum** – Dee, I hope we are living on the same planet. An average session in ICM is one and half hours long. My experience tells me you can't do justice to anything more than 10 page case in that time. That is, if the case has to be discussed end to end. I observe however that the average case in b-schools is much longer. So, the case discussion becomes a rush job. This case business closely resembles the Arab and the camel fable. The case method comes in claiming that it makes learning contemporary and interesting. It ends up hogging all the space and crowds out concepts. Not sure this is what we intended it to do.

**Dee** – Ya, but go ahead and use small cases. What stops you?

**Dum** – I told you, I have figured out what to do. This is not about you or me. Let us turn our attention to another interesting issue. Almost everything you write gets dated in a year or two. It will certainly not demonstrate solution to a real life problem of the day. Instead, why don't we go to the concept straight and demonstrate an illustration directly?

**Dee** – Meaning?

**Dum** – Instead of writing a case on say "marketing mix", why don't we choose two brands like Thums Up and Coke and compare them on the elements of the marketing mix like product, price, place and promotion as it stands today? Next year choose McDonalds and Burger King. Every year you choose a different topic. You can ask the students to make presentations on the topic. You can add your bit after their presentation. That will kill two birds at one shot. First, the data is current unlike the case method where the information depends on case vintage. Second you are demonstrating a concept like marketing mix which is what b-schools are all about. Teaching overarching concepts which have permanent learning value is what b-schools should do. Now tell me. Is the case method helping or harming this process? Are cases seriously overrated as a method of instruction?

**Dee** – well, if you think they are so bad, why use them?

**Dum** – they can probably be used if we figure out why, where and when. The case method is now like the bungalow in the estate. Will it become the outhouse if we give it its proper place? Used when necessary and locked up otherwise. There are also other issues.

**Dee** – like what?

---

[Page 5]

**Dum** – We can't write cases every day, besides there is also a revenue model. We sell the cases we write, everybody does. So there is a vested interest in hawking old cases. That is the best way of keeping the cash machine going. Sometimes I wonder if economics is short changing academics. I must say I heard some very innovative excuses for using dated cases. For instance, this case is very popular with students or that case has interesting learnings, and so on. Can't see how dated cases can provide real life solutions. There is also another catch. If the case is old, the solution to the problem is already known. Let us say you have a case on HP dated year 2000. That is 16 years old. What HP has done in the last 16 years is known to anybody who reads the pink papers. For instance it separated its hardware business from the rest of the HP. HP today is two companies. The student knows this. He will now cleverly suggest what HP did as the solution to its problems. His solution will not focus on what HP should have done. This is probably the worst possible academic outcome. Case discussions should not end up as endorsements of company strategies. This underscores the danger of using dated cases.

**Dee** – well, the answer is writing more cases continuously. If all of us write cases there will be a big enough stockpile of cases.

**Dum** – Sure! But here the issue is academic soundness of dated cases, not how many cases we have. The fact that some people are buying dated cases of their own will is a secondary issue. If they use old cases by design or default, they are welcome. I am right now just concerned about the scholastic desirability of such a practice.

**Dee** – by the way, I have my own set of questions about the case method.

**Dum** – Oh, that is nice. I was worried that I was the wet blanket in an otherwise perfect world in which everybody was happy with cases. What are your issues?

**Dee** – can the case method really substitute conventional teaching?

**Dum** – I doubt. But it almost substituted normal teaching of concepts. With long cases how much time do you have to meditate on the broader issues in the discipline? Let me add a few more questions. What are the strengths and weaknesses of the case method? Is there something the case method certainly cannot do? If there are things it cannot deliver we must find other ways to complete the learning experience. Further, how generalizable are the lessons we learn from a case? Can they be applied tomorrow? Can they be applied 5 or 10 years later? If they can't be, then what is the practical value of the case? There is also the question of suitability to a context. For instance how useful is a case on Walmart in US to somebody studying retailing in India? To begin with Walmart's initial forays into India have been less than successful. Also retailing in US is very different from retailing in India. For example, organized retail forms a small part of overall retailing in India. By contrast, in US organized retail contributes to most of retail sales. Is studying those models useful in any sense?

**Dee** – but that is something we should address. Why do we choose foreign cases? Why don't we write cases ourselves?

**Dum** – I agree. The fault, dear Brutus, lies in us. However, I must say, some attempts are being made now to write local cases. Just look at the number of cases our colleagues have written in the last 5 years. Some of them have become best sellers even abroad. A belated compensation, if I might say so. But let us face it. Even today the number of cases Indians write is a fraction of what leading universities abroad write about India. There is also something else that is interesting. Whenever there is an attempt at course standardization in India, the Indian teachers fall back on cases written abroad. Why? Only those cases are acceptable to all. What do you think is reason? Is it that their cases are superior or are we lazy?

**Dee** – beats me.

**Dum** – one very important dimension of case discussions which somehow never gets adequately discussed – "Can data outside cases be used while discussing cases?" If we allow some information outside to be used in case analysis then the entire analysis might change. One factoid can change the story. More importantly if each student gets in his/her data, then in a class of 75, we have 75 different cases. We are then obviously not discussing the same case. This is a recipe for endless confusion. On the other hand if we do not allow data outside the case we may not be discussing a contemporary problem. This is especially true of dated cases.

**Dee** – Have you listed all your complaints against the case method yet?

**Dum** – Not yet. The best comes last. Is there a right answer or not for a question? Of course, business education is not physics. So there are really no right answers. But let us not forget that certain terms have precise meaning in business. For instance, the meaning of "current ratio" in corporate finance is precise. If we leave these concepts to cases, will they go through? Isn't it better that we teach such things through regular class sessions rather than hope that students will infer them from cases? There was an interesting comment made by a senior faculty from a reputed academic institution. Probably the best one I heard in connection with cases. He said "I want the student to know the answer but I do not want to be the one to tell him". Bravo! That is precisely the goal of all teaching. But can you do that in one and half hours with a 25 page case?

**Dee** – sure, but what are you getting at?

**Dum** – I thought that was easy to guess. Why do so many faculty use a method whose premises are so unclear? It is as if your course is below par if you do not use cases. Why? Because some institutes abroad use cases extensively. Even quantitative methods are sought to be taught through them. Not that they should not be. But why force it? The fact is you can teach most management principles using good old conceptual methods. The case method came in chiefly to make the process of learning

interactive. That it certainly does. But why is there an untold pressure on faculty to push cases into the material?

**Dee** - what should b-schools do in your view?

**Dum** – you know, there was an ad for a sauce some time back. It says "not too sweet, not too hot, just right!". All methods of teaching are good provided we know how to use them. All tools of teaching are good servants but bad masters. We should not be apologists for any particular kind of pedagogy? We do use a lot of cases. This debate is less about cases and more about its users, the teachers and the students. It is as much an enquiry into faculty thought process as it is an investigation of the case method itself. The case method can do a lot of good, provided….

**Dee** – provided….?

**Dum** – I will answer that with a question? Is the case method, a meal or a dessert?

**Dee** – what do you think?

**Dum** – well it is like what profs say when they don't want to answer or probably don't know the answer. Figure it out for yourself! By the way, I have reduced all the issues related to cases into a dozen cut and dry queries. These are concerns about the fundamental premises of a method that is variously seen as the appetizer, meal, dessert and digester of business education (Exhibit 1 – Points to ponder). Please have a look at them.

**Dee** – will do. Well…there is at least one outcome of this chat. We have a lot of questions about cases, if not answers.

**Dum** – Sure, Profs should be searching for unanswered questions, students, innocent as they are, look for unquestionable answers. By the way, what do you think will be the student reaction to the conversation we had just now?!

## Exhibit 1 – Points to ponder

i) Given that class duration is one half hours why do b-school faculty routinely use long cases?

ii) Long cases usually mean long faces. How do we avoid overloading students? How to get over the problem of students cutting corners when too many long cases are given?

iii) What is the ideal length that fits a typical b-school session?

iv) What is the fundamental concern of the case method? Driving home concepts or discussing real life problems?

v) What is rationale for discussing dated cases? Students already know what happened to the case from business press. Will it then not defeat the academic objective of dispassionate analysis?

vi) Why should we force fit cases in disciplines like quantitative methods? Aren't such disciplines best taught in the conventional lecture mode with some supplementing of cases?

vii) How important is the case method? Is it the dessert or main course in the business education cuisine? Can it really replace conventional teaching?

viii) What are the case method's most important strengths and weaknesses? What is it that the case method definitely cannot do?

ix) What is an ideal way of complementing a case, because a case can never really cover all the aspects of a topic? What other things have to be done along with a case?

x) How generalizable are the lessons in a case? Are today's lessons applicable tomorrow? Are the lessons in India applicable in US and vice versa? What are the precautions we need to exercise to avoid obvious traps?

xi) Should data outside case be used while discussing cases? If so at what stage in case discussions?

xii) Some management concepts have precise answers (e.g. – definitions of financial ratios). Can/should the case method handle such certainties? How?

**Any answers?**
